# The Rojas Report: Physician-Owned Hospitals
## State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: New Hampshire

### 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls New Hampshire's Hospital Market?

New Hampshire's hospital market is highly consolidated and dominated by a few major nonprofit health systems. The state has 31 hospitals in total, including one academic medical center, five specialty hospitals, 12 large community hospitals, and 13 critical access hospitals [1]. The overall hospital market concentration in New Hampshire has increased significantly over the past two decades, with the statewide Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) rising from 1,884 in 1996 to 3,059 in 2023, classifying the entire state as a highly concentrated market [2].

The dominant hospital systems operating in New Hampshire are:

Market consolidation has been a defining feature of New Hampshire's healthcare landscape. In the Coos/Essex Health Service Area, the merger of three independent hospitals into North Country Healthcare in 2017 created a functional monopoly, driving the local HHI to 10,000 [2]. In the Grafton/Sullivan area, Dartmouth Health has steadily acquired community hospitals, most recently adding Valley Regional Hospital in Claremont and pursuing Littleton Regional Healthcare [2]. A proposed mega-merger between Dartmouth Health and GraniteOne Health (parent of Catholic Medical Center) was blocked by the New Hampshire Attorney General in 2022 due to severe anti-competitive concerns [4].

New Hampshire maintains a Certificate of Need (CON) program that regulates 12 different healthcare services and devices, including magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scanners, open-heart surgery, and psychiatric services [5]. The state ranks as the 27th most restrictive state for CON laws [5]. This regulatory framework interacts with Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) to create a formidable double barrier to entry for physician-owned hospitals. Even if the federal ban on Medicare billing for new physician-owned hospitals were lifted, prospective physician-owners in New Hampshire would still have to navigate the state's CON approval process, which incumbent hospital systems frequently use to block new competitors. Research indicates that New Hampshire's CON program has resulted in approximately 1,300 fewer hospital beds statewide and restricted the availability of essential imaging services [5].

### 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Based on the available data and the grandfathered facility lists from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), there are currently no operating physician-owned hospitals in New Hampshire. 

The absence of physician-owned hospitals in New Hampshire means that patients and physicians are entirely dependent on the dominant nonprofit systems and the limited for-profit presence of HCA Healthcare. There are no physician-owned facilities to provide competitive pressure on pricing, quality, or specialized care delivery.

Because there are no operating physician-owned hospitals in the state, there is no facility to identify as the most significant, nor is there data on CMS Star Ratings, Leapfrog Safety Grades, or quality awards for this category of provider in New Hampshire.

### 3. Section 6001 Impact on New Hampshire

Since the enactment of the Affordable Care Act on March 23, 2010, New Hampshire has not lost any grandfathered physician-owned hospitals because the state did not have an established baseline of these facilities prior to the ban. The attrition rate is therefore not applicable.

However, the impact of Section 6001 on New Hampshire is profound in its preventative effect. The federal ban, combined with the state's CON laws, has completely insulated the incumbent hospital systems from physician-led competition. As a result, specialties such as orthopedics, cardiology, and specialized surgical care are entirely controlled by the major health systems. 

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in New Hampshire could shift significantly, particularly in the southern and seacoast regions. The areas around Manchester, Nashua, and Portsmouth have the population density and commercial insurance base to support specialized physician-owned facilities. Orthopedic and cardiovascular specialties would be the most likely to see new physician-owned entrants, as these are high-margin service lines where physicians often seek greater operational control and efficiency than traditional hospital environments provide. However, any new entrants would still face the hurdle of the state's CON board.

### 4. Political & Regulatory Context

New Hampshire's federal delegation consists entirely of Democrats, none of whom have supported legislation to repeal the ban on physician-owned hospitals.

Neither Senator Shaheen nor Senator Hassan has co-sponsored H.R. 2191, S. 1390, H.R. 4002, or any predecessor bills aimed at reforming or repealing Section 6001 [6] [7]. Similarly, Representatives Pappas and Kuster have not sponsored or co-sponsored any POH reform bills in the House [6] [7].

At the state level, New Hampshire's regulatory environment is defined by its Certificate of Need program, which has been in place since 1979. The program requires state approval for the establishment of new healthcare facilities, changes in bed capacity, and the acquisition of major medical equipment [5]. While the state is known for its "Live Free or Die" ethos and lacks a state income or sales tax, its healthcare market remains heavily regulated. The New Hampshire Hospital Association, which represents the incumbent systems, wields significant influence and consistently highlights the financial fragility of the state's rural hospitals to justify the current regulatory protections [8].

### 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, the most underserved and highest-opportunity markets in New Hampshire for new physician-owned facilities would be the southern tier (Hillsborough and Rockingham counties). These areas, encompassing Manchester, Nashua, and the Seacoast, feature the state's highest population density, strongest commercial payer mix, and closest proximity to the Boston metropolitan economic engine.

The specialties with the widest gap between demand and physician-owned supply are orthopedics and cardiology. New Hampshire has the second-oldest population in the nation, with 26.7 percent of residents aged 60 or older [9]. This demographic reality drives immense demand for joint replacements, spine surgeries, and cardiovascular interventions. Currently, this demand is met almost entirely by the major health systems and a limited number of ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs). New Hampshire has only 29 Medicare-certified ASCs, a relatively low number that suggests significant unmet potential for physician-led surgical facilities [10].

The payer mix in New Hampshire presents both opportunities and challenges for new POH development. The state has a low uninsured rate of 4.7 percent [11]. However, the aging population means a high reliance on Medicare. Approximately 38 percent of Medicare beneficiaries in the state are enrolled in Medicare Advantage plans, though this market is currently experiencing turbulence with major carriers reducing coverage options for 2026 [12]. Commercial insurance premiums in the state are rising sharply, with increases of 7.5 to 20 percent projected for small employers in 2026, driven largely by hospital consolidation and facility fees [13]. A physician-owned hospital that could offer transparent, lower-cost bundled pricing for commercial payers and self-funded employers would have a strong competitive advantage in the southern New Hampshire market.
