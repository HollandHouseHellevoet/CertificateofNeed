# The Rojas Report: Alabama Market Intelligence Brief

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Alabama's Hospital Market?

The Alabama hospital market is characterized by significant consolidation, a high degree of concentration in both urban and rural areas, and the dominance of a few large health systems. According to a 2026 analysis by the Yale Tobin Center for Economic Policy and Becker's Hospital Review, Alabama ranks 18th nationally for hospital market concentration, with 66.3% of its general acute-care hospitals operating in highly concentrated or monopoly markets [1]. This concentration is exacerbated by the state's restrictive Certificate of Need (CON) laws, which create substantial barriers to entry for new competitors.

The market is dominated by a mix of large non-profit academic systems, regional non-profit networks, and national for-profit operators. The following table outlines the dominant hospital systems operating in Alabama, ranked by net patient revenue as of February 2026 [2].

Recent years have seen aggressive consolidation in the Alabama market. In November 2024, the University of Alabama at Birmingham (UAB) Health System Authority assumed ownership of Ascension St. Vincent's Health System, acquiring five hospitals and creating a dominant non-profit entity in the central part of the state [3]. In October 2024, Orlando Health, a Florida-based non-profit, acquired a 70% majority interest in Brookwood Baptist Health from Tenet Healthcare for approximately $910 million, taking control of five hospitals in the Birmingham area [4]. 

In northern Alabama, the non-profit Huntsville Hospital Health System has established a near-monopoly. In January 2026, the system agreed to acquire the 180-bed Crestwood Medical Center from Community Health Systems (CHS) for $450 million [5]. Because Crestwood was the only remaining hospital in Huntsville not owned by the Huntsville Hospital Health System, this acquisition gives the system a de facto monopoly on inpatient services within a 50-mile radius, raising significant antitrust concerns [5].

Alabama's Certificate of Need laws interact with Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act to create a double barrier to physician-owned hospital entry. Even if the federal ban on new physician-owned hospitals (POHs) were lifted, any physician group attempting to build a new facility in Alabama would still have to prove a "need" for their services to the State Health Planning and Development Agency (SHPDA). Because incumbent hospital systems frequently use the CON process to block or delay new competitors, physicians face both federal prohibition and state-level regulatory capture.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Based on comprehensive reviews of Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) ownership data, industry reports, and historical records, Alabama currently has zero physician-owned specialty hospitals. A 2006 analysis by the Birmingham Medical News noted that "because of certain regulatory and economic issues, Alabama has not seen physician-owned specialty hospitals" [6]. This absence has persisted through 2026.

While Alabama lacks physician-owned hospitals, it does have a robust market for physician-owned Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs). The state has 51 Medicare-certified ASCs, many of which are physician-owned or operated as joint ventures [7]. Facilities such as the Hughston Surgical Center and the Surgical Institute of Alabama provide outpatient surgical care, but they are legally and operationally distinct from inpatient hospitals and are limited in the acuity of cases they can handle.

Because there are no operating POHs in Alabama, there is no data available regarding CMS Star Ratings, Leapfrog Safety Grades, or quality awards for such facilities in the state. Similarly, there are no recorded closures or acquisitions of grandfathered POHs in Alabama since the passage of the Affordable Care Act.

The absence of POHs in Alabama is significant because it means patients in the state have never benefited from the high-quality, lower-cost care typically associated with physician-led facilities. The lack of POH competition has allowed incumbent systems to consolidate power and dictate pricing without the pressure of specialized, physician-driven alternatives.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Alabama

Because Alabama had no physician-owned hospitals prior to the enactment of the Affordable Care Act on March 23, 2010, the state has not lost any POHs to closure or acquisition, and the attrition rate is technically zero. However, the true impact of Section 6001 on Alabama is measured in lost opportunity. The federal ban has completely prevented the emergence of a POH sector in the state over the past 15 years.

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in Alabama could shift dramatically, provided that state-level CON reforms were also enacted. The most likely areas for new physician-owned facilities would be the highly consolidated markets of Birmingham, Huntsville, and Mobile. In Huntsville, where the Huntsville Hospital Health System has achieved a virtual monopoly following the Crestwood Medical Center acquisition, a physician-owned orthopedic or cardiovascular hospital could introduce desperately needed competition and provide patients with an alternative for high-margin elective procedures.

Specialties that would most likely see new physician-owned facilities include orthopedics, cardiology, and general surgery. These specialties are currently dominated by the large health systems, which rely on the revenue from these procedures to subsidize other operations. A repeal of Section 6001 would allow Alabama physicians to capture the value of their own referrals and offer specialized, efficient care outside the traditional hospital bureaucracy.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Alabama's political delegation has shown some support for repealing the ban on physician-owned hospitals. In the 119th Congress (2025-2026), U.S. Representative Dale W. Strong (R-AL) is listed as a co-sponsor of H.R. 4002, the Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act of 2025, which would repeal the Section 6001 restrictions [8]. U.S. Senators Katie Britt (R-AL) and Tommy Tuberville (R-AL) have also been identified as supporters of POH-related legislation, though their primary healthcare focus has been on securing federal funding for rural hospitals and military bases [9].

At the state level, Alabama operates under a highly restrictive Certificate of Need regime. According to the Cicero Institute's 2024 ranking of CON laws, Alabama scores 80 points, indicating a broad and restrictive regulatory environment [10]. The state requires a CON for medical inpatient facilities, medical outpatient clinics, behavioral health facilities, long-term care, and certain ancillary services [10]. Capital expenditure thresholds are set at $800,000 for operating expenses, $2 million for major medical equipment, and $4 million for construction [11].

There is growing momentum for CON reform in the Alabama Legislature. During the 2024 session, Senate Bill 236, sponsored by Senator Larry Stutts, proposed exempting certain services in rural areas from CON requirements [12]. While the bill received a public hearing, it did not advance to a floor vote. A separate bill, House Bill 247, would have abolished the CON process entirely but also failed to advance [12]. Senator Stutts has announced plans to introduce further CON reform legislation in the 2025-2026 session, supported by advocacy groups like the Alabama Policy Institute [12].

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, Alabama would present a unique, albeit challenging, opportunity for healthcare investors and physician groups. The most underserved and highest-opportunity markets are the state's rural counties, where nine hospitals have closed since 2009 and 27 more are currently at risk of closure [13]. However, the financial viability of POHs in these areas is complicated by the state's payer mix.

Alabama is one of only 10 states that has not expanded Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act [14]. The state's payer mix is heavily skewed toward commercial insurance and Medicare, with a significant uninsured population. According to 2024 data, the payer mix consists of 46% employer-sponsored insurance, 21% Medicaid, 6% non-group, and an uninsured rate of approximately 9%, which is the 18th highest in the nation [15]. Furthermore, the commercial insurance market is essentially a monopoly, with Blue Cross Blue Shield of Alabama controlling approximately 94% to 95% of the large-group market [16].

This payer landscape means that any new physician-owned hospital would have to negotiate contracts with a single dominant insurer (BCBS of Alabama) while managing a high burden of uncompensated care due to the lack of Medicaid expansion. Therefore, the most viable strategy for POH development in Alabama would be to focus on affluent suburban areas around Birmingham, Huntsville, and Mobile, targeting commercially insured patients for high-margin specialties like orthopedics and spine surgery.

While there are no publicly announced investor networks currently targeting POH development in Alabama, the recent influx of private equity into the state's healthcare sector suggests strong underlying interest. The acquisition of Brookwood Baptist Health by Orlando Health and the attempted affiliation of Jackson Hospital with HumanityCorp demonstrate that outside capital is actively seeking opportunities in the Alabama market [4] [17]. If the federal ban were lifted and state CON laws reformed, Alabama's highly consolidated markets would be prime targets for disruption by physician-led, investor-backed specialty hospitals.
