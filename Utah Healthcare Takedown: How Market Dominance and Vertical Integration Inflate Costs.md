# Utah Healthcare Takedown: How Market Dominance and Vertical Integration Inflate Costs

**Author:** Manus AI

**Date:** November 24, 2025

## Executive Summary

This report investigates the root causes of high healthcare costs in Utah, a state that, paradoxically, ranks as one of the most expensive for healthcare despite its free-market principles and lack of Certificate of Need (CON) laws. Our investigation reveals a system dominated by a single, vertically integrated nonprofit health system—Intermountain Health—that leverages its market power to inflate prices, suppress competition, and maximize profits at the expense of Utahns. While Utah's regulatory environment appears pro-competitive on the surface, the reality is a consolidated market where a few powerful players control both the delivery and financing of healthcare, leading to a crisis of affordability for residents.

## Key Findings

*   **Absence of CON Laws Has Not Prevented Market Consolidation:** Utah repealed its CON laws in the 1980s, yet the healthcare market has become highly concentrated, with Intermountain Health controlling a vast network of hospitals and clinics.
*   **Dominance of Intermountain Health:** Intermountain Health is the largest nonprofit health system in the Intermountain West, with over $9 billion in net patient revenue, more than three times its nearest competitor.
*   **Extreme Executive Compensation:** Top executives at Intermountain Health earn millions of dollars annually, with compensation packages that are 12-45 times the salary of the Utah Governor, contradicting their nonprofit, charitable mission.
*   **Exploitation of the 340B Drug Pricing Program:** Utah's 340B hospitals, particularly those under the Intermountain and University of Utah systems, generate 2.8 times more in 340B profits than they spend on charity care, using the program as a profit center.
*   **Major Conflict of Interest in Medicaid:** Intermountain Health owns SelectHealth, a health plan that manages a significant portion of Utah's Medicaid program, creating a conflict of interest where the insurer and provider are the same entity.
*   **Weak Property Tax Exemption Enforcement:** While Utah law requires nonprofit hospitals to provide community benefits exceeding their property tax exemptions, the actual enforcement and what qualifies as a 
community benefit remain questionable.

## 1. The Illusion of Competition: Utah's Lack of CON Laws

Unlike Kentucky and many other states, Utah does not have Certificate of Need (CON) laws that restrict the building of new healthcare facilities or the expansion of services [1]. These laws were repealed in the 1980s, theoretically to foster a more competitive, free-market healthcare environment. However, the absence of these regulations has not led to lower costs or a more diverse market. Instead, Utah's healthcare landscape has become a case study in how a market can become highly consolidated even without such laws, leading to some of the highest healthcare costs in the nation [2].

This paradox suggests that the simple removal of one regulatory barrier is insufficient to ensure a competitive market, especially when other factors—such as the aggressive expansion of a dominant player—are at play. The Utah experience demonstrates that market forces alone have not protected consumers from the effects of a healthcare monopoly.

## 2. The Intermountain Health Monopoly

At the heart of Utah's healthcare cost crisis is the overwhelming dominance of Intermountain Health. This "nonprofit" health system, with 33 hospitals and over $9 billion in net patient revenue, is the largest in the Intermountain West [3]. Its closest competitor, University of Utah Health, has a net patient revenue of just $2.8 billion, making Intermountain more than three times larger than the next-biggest player in the state. Together, these two nonprofit systems control a staggering $11.86 billion in net patient revenue, creating a duopoly that stifles competition and drives up prices.

| Health System | # of Hospitals | Net Patient Revenue | # of Staffed Beds | # of Discharges |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Intermountain Health** | 33 | $9,065,431,259 | 3,904 | 196,405 |
| **University of Utah Health** | 4 | $2,795,261,471 | 735 | 40,888 |
| **HCA Mountain Division** | 8 | $1,398,051,838 | 860 | 35,898 |

*Source: Definitive Healthcare, 2024* [3]

Intermountain's market power is further solidified by its vertical integration, which includes its ownership of SelectHealth, a dominant health insurance provider. This control over both the financing and delivery of care allows Intermountain to operate with minimal competitive pressure, a topic we will explore further in the Medicaid section of this report.

## 3. "Nonprofit" Profiteering: Executive Compensation

The term "nonprofit" suggests a mission of charity and community service. However, the executive compensation packages at Intermountain Health tell a different story. In 2023, the organization paid out over $44.5 million in executive compensation, with top leaders earning multi-million dollar salaries [4].

*   **Albert R. Zimmerli (Former Officer):** $8,266,670
*   **Robert W. Allen (Former Officer/COO):** $6,294,132
*   **A. Marc Harrison, MD (Former CEO):** $2,392,280

These compensation packages are between 12 and 45 times the salary of Utah's Governor, Spencer Cox, who earns $182,900 per year [5]. This stark contrast raises serious questions about the priorities of these tax-exempt organizations and whether they are truly serving the public good or simply enriching their executives.

## 4. The 340B Drug Pricing Program: A Profit Center

The 340B Drug Pricing Program was designed to help safety-net hospitals provide discounted drugs to vulnerable patients. In Utah, however, the program appears to be a significant source of profit for large hospital systems. Utah's 340B hospitals earn 2.8 times more in 340B profits than they spend on charity care [6].

Furthermore, a large portion of these discounted drugs are dispensed through out-of-state contract pharmacies. University of Utah Health, for example, has 200 contract pharmacies, 37% of which are located outside of Utah. This practice raises questions about whether the program is benefiting Utah communities or simply padding the bottom lines of hospitals and their pharmacy partners.

## 5. Medicaid and the Monopoly: A Conflict of Interest

Utah's Medicaid program is managed by four Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs), one of which is SelectHealth Community Care [7]. SelectHealth is a wholly owned subsidiary of Intermountain Health [8]. This creates a massive conflict of interest, as Intermountain is both the provider of healthcare services and, through its subsidiary, the entity responsible for managing Medicaid benefits and payments.

This vertical integration allows Intermountain to control a significant portion of the state's Medicaid market, directing taxpayer dollars to its own hospitals and clinics. With SelectHealth holding a 66.8% market share in the commercial insurance market, Intermountain's influence over healthcare financing in Utah is immense, further cementing its monopoly power [9].

## 6. Property Tax Exemptions: A Subsidy for the Rich?

Unlike Kentucky, Utah law requires nonprofit hospitals to provide community benefits in an amount that exceeds their property tax liability [10]. While this appears to be a strong safeguard, the enforcement of this provision is questionable. The definition of "community benefit" is broad and can include services that hospitals would provide anyway, such as community education or the operation of certain programs. This raises concerns about whether the value of these benefits is accurately reported and whether it truly outweighs the significant tax breaks these large hospital systems receive.

## Conclusion

Utah's healthcare system is a prime example of how market consolidation and vertical integration can lead to soaring costs, even in the absence of restrictive regulations like CON laws. The dominance of Intermountain Health, coupled with its control over the state's largest health insurance provider, has created a system where a single entity holds immense power, leading to high prices, questionable nonprofit practices, and a crisis of affordability for Utahns. To address these issues, Utah must take bold action to rein in the power of its healthcare monopolies and restore competition to the market.

## Recommendations

*   **Aggressive Antitrust Enforcement:** The state must actively investigate and challenge anti-competitive practices by dominant health systems, including the vertical integration of providers and insurers.
*   **340B Program Reform:** Utah should implement stricter oversight of the 340B program to ensure that it benefits patients, not just hospitals and pharmacies.
*   **Medicaid Reform:** The state should address the conflict of interest in its Medicaid managed care system by preventing providers from also acting as insurers.
*   **Strengthened Property Tax Exemption Requirements:** Utah must ensure that nonprofit hospitals are providing tangible community benefits that truly exceed the value of their tax exemptions.
