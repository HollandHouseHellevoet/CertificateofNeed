# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Georgia

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Georgia's Hospital Market?

The hospital market in Georgia is highly consolidated and dominated by a small number of massive, non-profit integrated delivery networks (IDNs). These systems have aggressively expanded their footprints through mergers and acquisitions, particularly over the last five years, creating significant barriers to entry for independent providers.

The dominant hospital systems operating in Georgia, ranked by net patient revenue, are:

| Rank | Hospital System | Headquarters | Status | Net Patient Revenue |
|------|-----------------|--------------|--------|---------------------|
| 1 | Northside Hospital System | Atlanta | Non-profit | $7.76 Billion |
| 2 | Piedmont Healthcare | Atlanta | Non-profit | $6.48 Billion |
| 3 | Wellstar Health System | Marietta | Non-profit | $5.54 Billion |
| 4 | Emory Healthcare | Atlanta | Non-profit | $5.29 Billion |
| 5 | Children's Healthcare of Atlanta | Atlanta | Non-profit | $2.14 Billion |

*Note: Revenue figures are based on 2023-2024 data from Definitive Healthcare and the Institute for Health Policy Accountability (IHPA).*

These non-profit systems wield immense market power. Northside Hospital System, for example, reported $7.76 billion in revenue in 2024, representing a 15.3% annual growth rate, and holds $5.9 billion in total assets [1]. In the Atlanta metropolitan area, the market is effectively an oligopoly controlled by Northside, Piedmont, Wellstar, and Emory. 

Recent market consolidation in Georgia has been rapid and transformative. In 2021 and 2022, Piedmont Healthcare acquired four hospitals from HCA Healthcare in a nearly $1 billion deal, significantly expanding its presence in north and middle Georgia [2]. Piedmont subsequently acquired three hospitals from University Health Care System. In August 2023, Wellstar Health System finalized a merger with Augusta University Health System, assuming control of the 478-bed Augusta University Medical Center and the 154-bed Children's Hospital of Georgia, effectively securing a 40-year contract with the University System of Georgia [3]. Most recently, in June 2025, Emory Healthcare officially merged with Houston Healthcare, expanding its reach into middle Georgia [4]. 

This consolidation has coincided with significant losses in safety-net capacity. In November 2022, Wellstar closed Atlanta Medical Center, a 460-bed Level 1 trauma center in downtown Atlanta, citing financial difficulties [5]. This closure placed immense strain on the remaining urban safety-net facilities, particularly Grady Memorial Hospital.

Georgia's Certificate of Need (CON) laws interact with Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act to create a formidable double barrier to physician-owned hospital (POH) entry. Section 6001 explicitly bans new physician-owned hospitals from participating in Medicare and Medicaid. However, even if Section 6001 were repealed, Georgia's CON laws would still require any new hospital to prove "community need" before construction. Because incumbent hospital systems frequently use the CON process to oppose and block new entrants, independent physicians face both federal prohibition and state-level regulatory capture.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Due to the combined effects of Section 6001 and Georgia's restrictive CON laws, the physician-owned hospital landscape in the state is exceptionally sparse. 

| Hospital Name | City | Specialty | Status |
|---------------|------|-----------|--------|
| Optim Medical Center-Tattnall | Reidsville | Orthopedics / Critical Access | Operating |
| Optim Medical Center-Jenkins | Millen | General Acute Care | Closed/Sold (2017) |

**Optim Medical Center-Tattnall** is the most significant, and likely the only remaining, operating physician-owned hospital in Georgia. Located in Reidsville, it operates as a Critical Access Hospital with a strong focus on orthopedic surgery, musculoskeletal care, spine, and pain medicine. It is owned by physicians affiliated with Optim Health System. 

Optim Medical Center-Tattnall plays a crucial role in its rural market. It has achieved a 5-star rating on the HCAHPS patient survey, which the facility notes makes it the only hospital within a 100-mile radius to achieve this distinction [6]. It has also been recognized by Becker's Hospital Review with a 5-star rating for nurse communication and is listed among the quietest hospitals in the nation [7]. As a grandfathered facility under Section 6001, it provides high-quality, specialized care in a rural setting that might otherwise lack these services.

Conversely, **Optim Medical Center-Jenkins**, previously a physician-owned facility in Millen, faced financial pressures and announced its closure in April 2017. It was subsequently sold to new ownership to remain open [8]. This transition highlights the vulnerability of rural POHs under the current regulatory regime.

Optim Medical Center-Tattnall matters immensely because it serves as the sole proof of concept for the physician-owned model in Georgia. It demonstrates that a POH can deliver top-tier patient satisfaction and specialized care in a rural environment, directly countering the narrative that physician ownership is incompatible with rural healthcare access.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Georgia

The exact number of physician-owned hospitals lost in Georgia since the enactment of the ACA on March 23, 2010, is difficult to quantify precisely due to the state's historically low baseline of POHs, a direct result of its longstanding CON laws. However, the attrition rate is effectively 50% among known facilities, with Optim Medical Center-Jenkins transitioning out of physician ownership in 2017.

The primary impact of Section 6001 in Georgia has been the absolute suppression of new market entrants. The expansion ban inherent in Section 6001 means that grandfathered facilities like Optim Medical Center-Tattnall are severely constrained in their ability to add beds, operating rooms, or procedure rooms to meet growing patient demand without navigating a complex and often prohibitive federal exception process.

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in Georgia would likely see targeted disruption, provided state CON laws were also navigated or further reformed. The most likely areas for new physician-owned facilities would be the rapidly growing suburbs of Atlanta, such as Forsyth, Gwinnett, and Cherokee counties, where patient demand for high-quality, elective procedures outpaces the efficiency of incumbent systems. Specialties such as orthopedics, cardiology, and gastroenterology would be the primary drivers of new POH development, as physicians in these fields seek to regain autonomy from the massive IDNs.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Georgia's political environment presents a complex landscape for physician-owned hospital reform. The state is represented in the U.S. Senate by Jon Ossoff (D) and Raphael Warnock (D). Neither senator has co-sponsored or publicly supported POH-specific legislation such as S. 1390 (the Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act). Senator Ossoff has focused heavily on rural hospital access and healthcare costs, launching investigations into rising health costs, but has not aligned these efforts with the POH model [9].

In the U.S. House of Representatives, however, Georgia has shown bipartisan support for POH reform. 

| Representative | Party/District | Legislation Supported |
|----------------|----------------|-----------------------|
| Rep. Austin Scott | R-GA-8 | H.R. 2191 (Original Cosponsor) |
| Rep. Richard McCormick | R-GA-7 | H.R. 2191 (Cosponsor) |
| Rep. Earl L. "Buddy" Carter | R-GA-1 | H.R. 2191 (Cosponsor) |
| Rep. Mike Collins | R-GA-10 | H.R. 4002 (118th Congress) |

Notably, Rep. Richard McCormick is an emergency medicine physician, and Rep. Buddy Carter is a pharmacist, bringing direct clinical perspective to their support for H.R. 2191.

At the state level, Georgia maintains a restrictive Certificate of Need program. According to the Cicero Institute, Georgia's CON program ranks as the 18th most restrictive in the United States, scoring 10 points for regulating medical inpatient facilities, including hospitals and destination cancer hospitals [10]. 

In April 2024, Governor Brian Kemp signed H.B. 1339, which enacted incremental CON reforms. While the legislation streamlined the application process and expanded exemptions for single-specialty Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs) and certain rural hospital developments, it fundamentally preserved the CON regime [11]. New hospitals and major expansions still require state approval, maintaining the state-level barrier to POH entry.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, Georgia would present a highly lucrative, albeit challenging, investment landscape for physician-owned hospitals. The state's payer mix is relatively favorable for commercial viability. According to 2023 data, 54% of Georgians are covered by commercial insurance (47% employer-sponsored, 7% non-group), while Medicare covers 13% and Medicaid covers 19% [12]. Georgia has not fully expanded Medicaid under the ACA, instead implementing a limited "Pathways to Coverage" waiver, which contributes to an uninsured rate of approximately 11.4%, the second highest in the nation [13].

The most underserved and high-opportunity markets for new POH development are twofold. First, the affluent, rapidly expanding suburbs of the Atlanta metropolitan area offer a dense population of commercially insured patients seeking specialized, efficient care in orthopedics, spine, and cardiovascular specialties. Second, the rural regions of south and middle Georgia, which have seen at least nine rural hospital closures since 2010, present opportunities for smaller, specialized physician-owned micro-hospitals or rural emergency hospitals, provided they can secure favorable payer contracts [14].

The gap between demand and physician-owned supply is widest in orthopedics and cardiology. Currently, physicians in these specialties are largely employed by or closely affiliated with the dominant IDNs. Private equity investment in Georgia's healthcare sector is already robust, particularly in the ASC space. For example, Summit Spine & Joint Centers, headquartered in Lawrenceville, recently completed its third private equity recapitalization with Wellspring Capital Management, operating 17 ASCs across the Southeast [15]. This demonstrates a strong appetite among investor networks to back physician-led enterprises. If the federal ban were lifted, this capital would rapidly flow from ASCs into full-scale physician-owned surgical hospitals, directly challenging the market dominance of systems like Northside and Piedmont.
