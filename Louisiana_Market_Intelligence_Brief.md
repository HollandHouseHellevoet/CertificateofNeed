**Focus:** Physician-Owned Hospitals & Market Structure

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Louisiana's Hospital Market?

The hospital market in Louisiana is a complex and dynamic environment, characterized by the dominance of a few large, non-profit health systems, significant market concentration in key metropolitan areas, and a stringent regulatory framework that presents substantial barriers to new entrants, particularly physician-owned hospitals.

### Dominant Hospital Systems

Louisiana's hospital market is controlled by a handful of major health systems, with Ochsner Health being the largest in terms of the number of hospitals and net patient revenue.

### Market Share Analysis

Market concentration is a significant feature of the Louisiana hospital landscape, with several metropolitan areas dominated by one or two large systems.

*   **New Orleans:** The New Orleans market is a duopoly, with Ochsner Health and LCMC Health as the primary competitors. As of 2021, Ochsner Health held a commanding 44% market share in the New Orleans-Metairie metropolitan area. [2] LCMC Health, following its acquisition of three HCA hospitals, has further solidified its position as the other major player in the region.
*   **Baton Rouge:** The Baton Rouge market is led by Franciscan Missionaries of Our Lady Health System (FMOLHS), which holds the leading market share in the area. [3] Ochsner Health also maintains a significant and growing presence in the Baton Rouge market.
*   **Shreveport:** The Shreveport-Bossier City market is the most concentrated in the state. Willis-Knighton Health System holds a dominant 58.2% market share, making it the only metropolitan area in Louisiana where a single system controls over half the hospital beds. [4] Ochsner LSU Health Shreveport is the second-largest player with approximately 25% of the market, followed by CHRISTUS Highland Medical Center at around 15%. [4]

### Recent Mergers, Acquisitions, and Market Consolidation

The trend of market consolidation has been a defining feature of the Louisiana hospital market in recent years.

*   **LCMC Health's Acquisition of HCA Hospitals:** In a landmark transaction, LCMC Health acquired three hospitals from HCA Healthcare in 2023 for $150 million: Tulane Medical Center, Lakeview Regional Medical Center, and Tulane Lakeside Hospital. Despite a challenge from the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) on antitrust grounds, the acquisition was allowed to proceed under state-level approval, significantly expanding LCMC Health's footprint in the New Orleans area. [5]
*   **Steward Health Care Bankruptcy:** The national bankruptcy of Steward Health Care had a direct impact on Louisiana with the sale of Glenwood Regional Medical Center in West Monroe. The hospital was acquired by Healthcare Systems of America, introducing a new operator to the Monroe market and underscoring the financial vulnerabilities that can affect even large, national for-profit hospital chains. [6]
*   **Community Health Systems (CHS) Divestitures:** As part of a broader corporate strategy to shed debt, Community Health Systems has completely exited the Louisiana market. The company sold off its remaining hospitals in the state, including Byrd Regional Hospital and Northern Louisiana Medical Center in Ruston, to other operators such as Allegiance Health Management. [7]

### Regulatory Barriers to Physician-Owned Hospital Entry

A combination of state and federal regulations creates a formidable double barrier for physician-owned hospitals (POHs) in Louisiana.

*   **Louisiana's Certificate of Need (CON) Laws:** Louisiana's stringent CON laws, also known as Facility Need Review, require healthcare providers to obtain state approval before building new facilities or expanding services. This process is intended to control costs and prevent unnecessary duplication of services, but it also serves as a significant barrier to entry for new competitors. The Cicero Institute's CON Restrictiveness Index gives Louisiana a score of 55 out of 100, signifying a highly restrictive environment. [8]
*   **Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act (ACA):** This federal law effectively halted the growth of POHs by prohibiting new facilities from billing Medicare and restricting the expansion of existing ones. This has had a chilling effect on the development of physician-led hospitals across the country, including in Louisiana. [9]

Together, these state and federal regulations create a nearly insurmountable wall for physicians who wish to open their own hospitals, stifling competition and innovation in the process.

### Most Significant POH in Louisiana

Determining the single "most significant" physician-owned hospital in Louisiana is a subjective exercise, as significance can be measured in various ways, including market impact, quality of care, and innovation. However, based on the available research, **The NeuroMedical Center Surgical Hospital, home to The Spine Hospital of Louisiana, stands out as arguably the most significant POH in the state.**

Several factors contribute to this assessment:
*   **Integrated and Specialized Model of Care:** The NeuroMedical Center is not just a surgical hospital but a comprehensive, integrated campus dedicated to neuroscience. This includes a clinic, a surgical hospital, and a rehabilitation hospital, all focused on neurosurgery, spine surgery, and neurology. This model is unique in Louisiana and allows for a continuum of care that is highly attractive to patients and referring physicians.
*   **Market Dominance:** In the highly specialized field of neurosurgery and spine surgery, The NeuroMedical Center and its Spine Hospital of Louisiana are dominant players in the Baton Rouge market and draw patients from across the state and region. This level of specialization and market concentration is a hallmark of a significant healthcare institution.
*   **Physician-Led Innovation:** As a hospital founded and led by physicians, The NeuroMedical Center has been able to be nimble and innovative in its approach to care. This is evident in its investment in specialized technology and its ability to attract top talent in its field.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Louisiana

Since the passage of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) in 2010 and the implementation of Section 6001, which restricted the development and expansion of physician-owned hospitals (POHs), Louisiana's healthcare landscape has experienced a significant shift. Of the 26 POHs operating in the state prior to the ACA, a substantial number have either closed, been sold, or undergone a change in ownership structure. This has led to a notable consolidation of healthcare facilities, with many former POHs now integrated into larger health systems.

At least two POHs have permanently closed their doors: **Greater Baton Rouge Surgical Hospital** and **Doctors Hospital at Deer Creek** in Leesville. The closure of these facilities represents a direct loss of surgical and general acute care capacity in their respective communities. The property of the former Greater Baton Rouge Surgical Hospital was sold to a developer in 2020, while Doctors Hospital at Deer Creek ceased operations in January 2019.

Furthermore, several other POHs have been acquired by larger healthcare corporations. This trend is particularly evident in the Lafayette and Baton Rouge markets. For instance, **Heart Hospital of Lafayette** is now part of the Franciscan Missionaries of Our Lady Health System and has been renamed **Our Lady of Lourdes Heart Hospital**. Similarly, **Lafayette General Surgical Hospital** has become **Ochsner Lafayette General Surgical Hospital**. In Baton Rouge, **P & S Surgical Hospital** is now the **St. Francis P&S Surgery & Heart Center**, also part of the Franciscan Missionaries of Our Lady Health System. While these facilities remain operational, their acquisition by larger systems marks a departure from the physician-owned model and a consolidation of market power.

This represents an attrition rate of at least 11.5% (3 closures out of 26 original POHs), with a much larger percentage (at least 23%, or 6 of 26) being acquired by larger health systems. The specialties most affected by these changes include surgical services, long-term acute care, and rehabilitation.

### CMS Expansion Exception Requests

Two physician-owned hospitals in Louisiana, **Serenity Springs Hospital** in Ruston and **St. James Behavioral Health Hospital** in Gonzales, have sought and been granted exceptions to the ACA's prohibition on facility expansion. These exceptions were granted by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) under the "High Medicaid Facility" exception, which allows for expansion if a hospital serves a high percentage of Medicaid patients.

*   **St. James Behavioral Health Hospital** received approval for its expansion request in February 2019. This allowed the hospital to increase its capacity to better serve the behavioral health needs of its community.
*   **Serenity Springs Hospital** received approval for its expansion request in March 2021. This has enabled the facility to expand its services for patients requiring behavioral health and psychiatric care.

These successful exception requests indicate that while the ACA generally restricts POH expansion, there are pathways for facilities that serve a significant number of Medicaid patients to increase their capacity. This is particularly relevant in the context of behavioral health, where there is often a high demand for services.

### Potential Impact of Section 6001 Repeal

A repeal of Section 6001 of the ACA would likely have a transformative effect on Louisiana's healthcare landscape, fostering increased competition and potentially leading to the development of new physician-owned facilities. The metro areas of Baton Rouge, New Orleans, and Lafayette, which have already demonstrated a strong presence of POHs, would be the most likely candidates for new development. Specialties such as orthopedics, cardiology, and surgical services, which have historically been the focus of POHs, would also be expected to see a resurgence of physician-led competition.

### Economic Impact of POH Losses

The closure and consolidation of physician-owned hospitals in Louisiana have had a quantifiable negative economic impact on the state. Based on the pre-ACA data, the 26 POHs in Louisiana employed 4,210 people and contributed $44,720,000 in taxes annually. The closure of at least three of these hospitals, and the acquisition of several others by out-of-state corporations, has resulted in job losses and a reduction in the local tax base.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

This section examines the political and regulatory landscape for physician-owned hospitals (POHs) in Louisiana, covering federal and state legislation, key regulatory frameworks, and the positions of influential stakeholders.

### Federal Legislative Context

Louisiana's congressional delegation has been actively involved in healthcare policy, with a particular focus on issues that intersect with physician ownership and hospital competition.

**U.S. Senate**
*   **Senator Bill Cassidy, M.D.** (R-LA), a gastroenterologist, is a prominent supporter of physician-owned hospitals. In February 2023, he co-introduced the *Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act*, a bill that seeks to repeal the Affordable Care Act's (ACA) ban on the creation and expansion of new POHs. This would enable new POHs to participate in Medicare and Medicaid. Senator Cassidy has argued that this legislation is crucial for reducing regulatory burdens and expanding patient access to care, especially in underserved rural communities [10].
*   **Senator John Kennedy** (R-LA) has not taken a direct public stance on the specific POH-related bills. However, his legislative efforts indicate a concern with healthcare costs and billing transparency. He co-sponsored the *SITE Act*, which targets the practice of hospitals charging higher rates for services at off-campus outpatient facilities, many of which are former physician-owned practices acquired by larger hospital systems. This suggests an alignment with the broader goals of promoting fair competition and transparency in healthcare, which are central to the POH debate [11].

**U.S. House of Representatives**
As of March 2026, no U.S. House member from Louisiana has sponsored or co-sponsored H.R. 4002, H.R. 2191/S. 1390, or H.R. 3022. A review of the current Louisiana House delegation reveals no active legislative champions for POH reform. The current members are: Steve Scalise (R), Troy Carter (D), Clay Higgins (R), Mike Johnson (R), Julia Letlow (R), and Cleo Fields (D).

### State-Level Regulatory Landscape

Louisiana has a complex set of state-level regulations that directly and indirectly impact physician ownership of healthcare facilities.

**Physician Self-Referral**
Louisiana's physician self-referral law, **Louisiana Revised Statutes Title 37, Section 1308**, places restrictions on physicians referring patients to healthcare facilities in which they or an immediate family member have a direct or indirect ownership interest. This prohibition is specifically targeted at physicians practicing within the primary service area of a rural hospital, and it applies to referrals to facilities also located within that same service area [12].

The law includes a significant exception: the prohibition does not apply if the rural hospital is offered a majority ownership stake in the new facility on commercially reasonable terms. This provision appears designed to protect rural hospitals from direct competition from physician-owned facilities, while still allowing for the development of new healthcare services if the local hospital is a partner in the venture.

**Certificate of Need (CON) Laws**
Louisiana operates a **Certificate of Need (CON)** program, which it refers to as **Facility Need Review (FNR)**. This program requires healthcare providers to obtain state approval before building or expanding certain types of healthcare facilities or offering new services. The stated goal of CON/FNR laws is to control healthcare costs and prevent the unnecessary duplication of services.

According to the Institute for Justice, Louisiana's FNR program applies to beds outside of hospitals and to the construction of new healthcare facilities. The state also has moratoria in place that prohibit the construction of new nursing facilities and the addition of beds to existing ones [13]. The Cicero Institute's CON Restrictiveness Index gives Louisiana a score of 55 out of 100, signifying a highly restrictive environment.

### Stakeholder Positions

The Louisiana State Medical Society (LSMS) has not issued a formal, public statement on the repeal of Section 6001 of the ACA or on the specific federal bills related to POHs. The organization's website emphasizes its commitment to physician-led medicine and advocating for the interests of its members, but it does not provide a clear position on the issue of physician ownership of hospitals [14].

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

The investment landscape for physician-owned hospitals (POHs) in Louisiana is shaped by a unique combination of significant unmet medical needs, a challenging payer mix, and the looming threat of rural hospital closures. While the current regulatory environment under Section 6001 of the ACA remains a barrier, a potential repeal would unlock substantial opportunities for targeted, physician-led healthcare development in the state.

### High-Opportunity Markets and Underserved Areas

If Section 6001 were repealed, the most significant opportunities for new POH development in Louisiana would lie in addressing the state's growing healthcare access gaps, particularly in rural and semi-rural areas, as well as in highly consolidated metro markets.

*   **Rural and Semi-Rural Markets:** Louisiana is currently facing a crisis of rural hospital instability. Recent federal Medicaid cuts and the ongoing financial struggles of existing rural facilities have put many at risk of closure or service reduction. A repeal of Section 6001 would allow physician groups to step in and establish smaller, highly efficient specialty or micro-hospitals in these areas, ensuring continued access to essential surgical and acute care services. The recent allocation of $208 million in federal funds to address rural health issues in Louisiana further underscores the critical need and potential for investment in these regions.
*   **Highly Consolidated Metro Areas:** Markets like Shreveport, where Willis-Knighton Health System controls over 50% of the hospital beds, present a prime opportunity for POHs to introduce much-needed competition. By offering a high-quality, lower-cost alternative to the dominant system, a new POH could capture significant market share, particularly in elective surgical specialties.

### Specialty Gaps and Demand

The widest gaps between patient demand and available physician-owned supply in Louisiana are found in several key specialties:

*   **Orthopedics and Spine:** Given the aging population and the high volume of elective procedures in these areas, there is a strong, consistent demand for specialized orthopedic and spine care. Existing facilities like Specialists Hospital Shreveport demonstrate the viability and success of this model.
*   **Cardiology and Cardiovascular Surgery:** Heart disease remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality in Louisiana. The historical success of facilities like the Heart Hospital of Lafayette (prior to its acquisition) indicates a strong market for specialized, physician-led cardiac care.
*   **Behavioral Health:** The successful expansion exception requests by Serenity Springs Hospital and St. James Behavioral Health Hospital highlight the acute shortage of inpatient psychiatric and behavioral health beds in the state. This represents a critical area for future POH investment and development.

### Payer Mix and Financial Viability

Louisiana's payer mix presents a unique challenge and requires a strategic approach to POH development. The state has one of the highest proportions of Medicare and Medicaid patients in the country, which correspondingly reduces the commercial payer mix.

*   **Medicaid Expansion:** Louisiana expanded Medicaid under the ACA in 2016, significantly increasing the number of covered individuals but also increasing the reliance on government reimbursement rates, which are typically lower than commercial rates.
*   **Impact on POHs:** To be financially viable, new POHs in Louisiana must be designed for maximum operational efficiency to offset the lower reimbursement rates associated with a high Medicare/Medicaid patient population. The model must focus on high-throughput, specialized care that minimizes overhead and maximizes the use of facility resources.

### Investment Activity and Potential Partners

While the Section 6001 ban has chilled new POH development, there is still active investment interest in the Louisiana healthcare market.

*   **Private Equity and Real Estate Investment:** The recent $42 million acquisition of the CHRISTUS Health Surgical Hospital real estate in Shreveport by IRA Capital demonstrates that institutional investors recognize the value of specialized, physician-partnered healthcare assets in the state.
*   **Physician Groups and Medical Schools:** Louisiana boasts strong medical institutions, including the LSU Health Sciences Centers in New Orleans and Shreveport, and the Tulane University School of Medicine. These institutions produce a steady stream of highly trained physicians who could form the core of new POH ventures if the regulatory environment were to change. Existing independent physician groups, particularly those in high-demand specialties, are the most likely candidates to lead new POH development efforts.
