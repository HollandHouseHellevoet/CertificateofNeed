# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Oregon

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Oregon's Hospital Market?

Oregon's hospital market is highly consolidated and dominated by large, non-profit integrated delivery networks (IDNs). The state has approximately 70 hospitals and healthcare facilities, with the vast majority operating under non-profit status. The market is characterized by significant financial instability, with a 2025 Hospital Association of Oregon report indicating that around half of the state's hospitals are losing money on operations, and more than two-thirds lack sufficient revenue to update facilities or replace outdated equipment [1].

The Portland metropolitan area is heavily concentrated, dominated by three major systems: Providence, Legacy Health, and OHSU. In May 2025, a proposed merger between OHSU and Legacy Health, which would have combined two of Portland's three major systems into a 10-hospital integrated network, was mutually abandoned following significant opposition and concerns over market dominance and potential price increases [2].

Outside of Portland, regional monopolies are common. St. Charles Health System is the sole dominant provider in Central Oregon (Bend/Redmond), while Asante dominates the Southern Oregon market (Medford/Grants Pass).

Oregon maintains one of the most restrictive Certificate of Need (CON) programs in the United States, established in 1971. The Cicero Institute ranks Oregon tied for the 27th most restrictive CON laws nationally, with a score of 65 points [3]. The CON scope covers new hospitals and new nursing facility services. This regulatory framework interacts with Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act to create a formidable double barrier to entry for physician-owned hospitals. However, Oregon explicitly exempts rural hospitals from CON requirements, implicitly acknowledging that CON laws restrict access to care in underserved areas.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

The physician-owned hospital (POH) landscape in Oregon is virtually non-existent, reflecting the state's historically hostile regulatory environment toward corporate and alternative ownership models. Oregon had zero physician-owned specialty hospitals listed in the 2008 Senate Finance Committee and Office of Inspector General review list prior to the passage of the ACA.

Currently, there is only one identified physician-owned hospital operating in the state:

| Hospital Name | City | Specialty | Status |
|---------------|------|-----------|--------|
| McKenzie-Willamette Medical Center | Springfield | General Acute Care | Operating |

**McKenzie-Willamette Medical Center**
Located in Springfield, Oregon, McKenzie-Willamette Medical Center is a 113-bed community hospital that originally opened in 1955. It is directly or indirectly owned by a partnership that includes physician owners, specifically members of the hospital's medical staff. The facility is part of Quorum Health Corporation, which spun off from Community Health Systems in April 2016 [4]. 

Despite the challenging market conditions, McKenzie-Willamette maintains a strong competitive position against the dominant non-profit systems in the Eugene/Springfield area. In August 2024, the facility received a 4-Star Rating from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) for Overall Hospital Quality [5]. Furthermore, it was named to the Forbes Top Hospitals list for 2026 and is currently planning a new emergency department expansion in West Eugene by 2027.

Because Oregon lacked grandfathered specialty POHs at the time of the ACA's passage, there is no documented history of POH closures or acquisitions driven by Section 6001 in this state. McKenzie-Willamette remains the most significant POH in Oregon simply by virtue of being the only one, demonstrating that the model can achieve high quality ratings and financial viability even in a highly regulated, non-profit-dominated market.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Oregon

Because Oregon had no physician-owned specialty hospitals operating when the Affordable Care Act was signed on March 23, 2010, the state has not experienced the direct attrition, closures, or forced sales seen in states like Texas or Oklahoma. The attrition rate for grandfathered POHs in Oregon is effectively zero, as there was no baseline inventory to lose.

However, the impact of Section 6001 on Oregon is measured in missed opportunities rather than lost facilities. The federal ban, combined with the state's restrictive CON laws, has completely suppressed the development of new physician-owned facilities over the past 15 years. Consequently, patients across all specialties in Oregon have been denied the benefits of physician-owned competition, including lower infection rates, higher patient satisfaction, and downward pressure on pricing.

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in Oregon would likely see targeted disruption rather than widespread proliferation, due to the remaining CON barriers. The most likely areas for new physician-owned facility development would be in rural markets, which are explicitly exempt from Oregon's CON requirements. Specialties such as orthopedics, cardiology, and general surgery would be prime candidates for new physician-led facilities in regions like Eastern Oregon or the rapidly growing Central Oregon corridor, where patients currently face long travel times and limited options.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

The political environment in Oregon is overwhelmingly Democratic and historically hostile to corporate healthcare ownership, though this hostility has not translated into support for physician-owned hospitals.

**Congressional Delegation**
Oregon's U.S. Senators have not co-sponsored or publicly supported any POH-related legislation, including H.R. 4002, H.R. 2191, S. 1390, or H.R. 3022.
*   **Senator Ron Wyden (D):** As the Ranking Member of the Senate Finance Committee, Senator Wyden holds critical jurisdiction over healthcare finance and Medicare policy. While he has expressed significant concern regarding rural healthcare access, he has not championed physician ownership as a solution.
*   **Senator Jeff Merkley (D):** Senator Merkley is a vocal critic of private equity and corporate consolidation in healthcare. In February 2026, he introduced the "Corporate Crimes Against Health Care Act" to target private equity abuse [6]. While his focus is on private equity rather than physician ownership, the general anti-corporate climate he fosters creates a challenging political narrative for any alternative ownership models.

In the U.S. House of Representatives, none of Oregon's six representatives have sponsored or co-sponsored POH reform bills. Notably, Representative Maxine Dexter (D, OR-3) is a physician (MD), representing a potential, though currently unengaged, voice for physician-led initiatives within the delegation.

**State-Level Legislation: SB 951**
In June 2025, Governor Tina Kotek signed Senate Bill 951 into law, creating the nation's toughest barrier to private equity investment in healthcare. The law severely limits management services organizations' (MSOs) control over medical practices and renders noncompetition agreements with medical licensees void and unenforceable [7]. 

Crucially, hospitals and hospital-affiliated clinics are explicitly exempt from SB 951. This exemption creates a massive strategic loophole: while private equity cannot easily roll up independent physician practices in Oregon, the physician-owned hospital model remains one of the few legally protected avenues for alternative capital and physician control in the state.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

Despite severe regulatory hurdles, Oregon presents distinct opportunities for physician-owned hospital development, driven by demographic shifts, hospital financial crises, and strategic regulatory exemptions.

**Demographics and Payer Mix**
Oregon is experiencing a significant demographic shift. As of May 2025, seniors outnumber children in the state for the first time, with the elderly population projected to grow at 1.6% annually over the next decade [8]. This aging population will drive disproportionate demand for high-acuity surgical and specialty care.

The state's payer mix presents challenges. Approximately 34% of Oregonians (1.43 million people) are enrolled in the Oregon Health Plan (Medicaid) [9]. Furthermore, in October 2019, Oregon implemented a hospital payment cap, limiting hospital payments to 200% of Medicare rates for care provided to state employees and educators. However, commercial insurance still pays approximately 223% of Medicare prices for inpatient services, providing necessary margin opportunities for highly efficient, specialized facilities [10].

**Underserved Markets and Healthcare Deserts**
Currently, 2.5 million Oregonians live in designated Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs). Rural Oregon faces severe shortages across physical, mental, and oral healthcare disciplines. Recent hospital closures, including the February 2026 shutdown of Vibra Specialty Hospital in Portland (the state's only long-term acute care hospital), highlight the fragility of the current infrastructure [11].

If Section 6001 were repealed, the highest-opportunity markets for new physician-owned facilities would be:
1.  **Rural Exempt Markets:** Because rural hospitals are exempt from Oregon's CON laws, areas in Eastern and Southern Oregon present immediate opportunities for physician-led development without the burden of state regulatory approval.
2.  **Central Oregon:** The Bend/Redmond corridor is rapidly growing but remains monopolized by St. Charles Health System. A physician-owned orthopedic or cardiovascular specialty hospital could capture significant commercial volume in this market.
3.  **Portland Metro:** The failure of the OHSU-Legacy merger leaves the Portland market fragmented. While CON laws apply here, the demonstrated financial instability of the incumbent systems creates an opening for highly efficient, focused factories to capture market share in high-margin specialties.

The widest gap between demand and supply exists in orthopedic surgery, cardiology, and long-term acute care, particularly in regions outside the immediate Portland metropolitan area.
