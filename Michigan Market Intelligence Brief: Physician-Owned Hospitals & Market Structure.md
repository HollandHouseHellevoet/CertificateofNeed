# Michigan Market Intelligence Brief: Physician-Owned Hospitals & Market Structure

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Michigan's Hospital Market?

Michigan's healthcare market is highly consolidated and dominated by a small number of massive, nonprofit health systems. According to the Yale Healthcare Affordability Lab, Michigan has 133 short-term acute care hospitals, with an average hospital market Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) of 5,515, which is considered highly concentrated and sits above the national average of 5,467 [1]. Nearly half (48 percent) of Michigan hospitals operate in markets classified as "Higher" concentration or "Monopoly," while only 0.75 percent operate in moderately competitive markets [1]. System ownership is pervasive, with 86 percent of hospitals belonging to one of 21 systems, leaving only 14 percent as independent facilities [1].

The market is effectively controlled by three dominant mega-systems, alongside several significant regional players.

In Southeast Michigan, the combined Henry Ford Health and Ascension joint venture controls 44.8 percent of the local hospital market, approaching monopoly levels of concentration [2]. The state has seen aggressive consolidation over the past five years, most notably the $13 billion integration of Beaumont Health and Spectrum Health to form Corewell Health in February 2022, and University of Michigan Health's acquisition of Sparrow Health System [2]. 

Michigan operates under a highly restrictive Certificate of Need (CON) regime. According to the Mercatus Center, Michigan regulates 18 different healthcare services, facilities, and equipment purchases, making it one of 32 states with four or more CON restrictions [4]. Services requiring a CON include acute hospital beds, ambulatory surgical centers (ASCs), cardiac catheterization, MRI scanners, and psychiatric services [4]. This regulatory framework interacts with Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act to create a formidable double barrier to entry for physician-owned hospitals. Even if a physician group could secure federal approval or an exception to build a new facility, they would still have to prove "need" to a state board heavily influenced by incumbent hospital systems, effectively shielding legacy providers from new competition.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Michigan has a very limited physician-owned hospital footprint, largely due to the historical chilling effect of its CON laws, which prevented widespread POH development even before the ACA's Section 6001 ban took effect in 2010. However, the state is home to one of the most notable POH success stories in the country, alongside a cautionary tale of financial struggle.

**Insight Surgical Hospital (Insight Health System)**
Insight represents the most significant physician-owned hospital enterprise in Michigan. Founded in 2006 as a small physician office in Flint by neurosurgeon Dr. Jawad Shah, Insight has grown into a multistate hospital system with over 5,000 employees and $450 million in patient revenue [5]. In Michigan, Insight acquired the former Michigan Surgical Hospital in Warren in 2020 to expand its surgical services [6]. The system recently opened a new 70-bed psychiatric hospital in Flint in December 2025 and purchased ProMedica Coldwater Regional Hospital in 2024 [7] [8]. Insight's success demonstrates that the physician-owned model can scale, rescue distressed community hospitals, and expand access to critical services like behavioral health, despite the regulatory headwinds.

**Surgeons Choice Medical Center**
Located in Southfield, this 45-bed proprietary hospital (formerly known as Oakland Regional Hospital) focuses on hand, joint, orthopedic, and spine surgery. Physician owners invested $30 million in an overhaul that led to healthy profit margins for nearly seven years before facing financial headwinds in 2016 [9]. It remains a vital alternative for specialized surgical care in the Detroit metro area.

**Doctors' Hospital of Michigan**
This Pontiac-based facility serves as a cautionary tale regarding the challenges POHs face in highly consolidated markets. Originally owned by 42 physician shareholders who bought out McLaren Health Care's 35 percent stake for approximately $3 million in 2011, the hospital struggled to compete [10]. It filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in July 2015, listing debts between $10 million and $50 million, and has undergone multiple restructuring efforts since [11]. 

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Michigan

Because Michigan's CON laws severely restricted hospital construction prior to 2010, the state did not have a massive boom in POH development to lose when Section 6001 was enacted. However, the state has still experienced attrition. Detroit Hope Hospital, a minority-owned facility focused on underserved populations, closed in January 2010 just before the ACA's passage, and Doctors' Hospital of Michigan has been lost to bankruptcy and restructuring. 

The primary impact of Section 6001 in Michigan is not measured in closures, but in suppressed innovation. The expansion ban constrains successful models like Insight Surgical Hospital from organically adding operating rooms or beds to meet patient demand without navigating complex bureaucratic exceptions. 

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in Michigan would likely see targeted disruption rather than a flood of new general acute care hospitals, primarily due to the remaining CON barrier. Physician groups would likely focus on high-margin, specialized facilities such as orthopedic, cardiovascular, and neurosurgical hospitals in affluent suburbs of Detroit (Oakland and Macomb counties) and the Grand Rapids metro area. Repeal would also allow successful operators like Insight to expand their footprint more aggressively, potentially acquiring and turning around more distressed community hospitals that legacy systems have abandoned.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Michigan's political environment is heavily influenced by incumbent hospital systems, making healthcare reform challenging.

**Federal Delegation:**
*   **U.S. Senators:** Senators Gary Peters (D-MI) and Debbie Stabenow (D-MI) have not co-sponsored or publicly supported POH-related repeal legislation such as H.R. 3022 (Restoring Rights of Physicians to Own Hospitals Act).
*   **U.S. House Members:** Representative Tom Barrett (MI-07) recently launched a "Blueprint for a Better America" healthcare reform package focused on competition, insurance reform, and price transparency, though it does not explicitly target POH repeal [12]. Representative Jack Bergman (R-MI) has been active on rural hospital issues, co-sponsoring the Save America's Rural Hospitals Act [13]. Currently, no Michigan representatives are listed as co-sponsors for H.R. 3022 [14].

**State-Level Context:**
Michigan's Certificate of Need program is authorized under the Public Health Code and is fiercely defended by the Michigan Health & Hospital Association (MHA). The MHA is a dominant political force, having contributed over $6.7 million to state political campaigns over the past three decades [15]. The association actively lobbies to maintain the CON program and protect the 340B drug pricing program. 

Recent research by the Lown Institute highlights a significant vulnerability for Michigan's legacy systems: 75 percent of the state's nonprofit hospitals have a "fair share deficit," meaning they receive more in tax breaks than they return to their communities in charity care, totaling a $605 million annual shortfall [16]. Furthermore, research indicates that Michigan hospitals participating in the 340B program generate 49 percent more patient revenue but provide 30 percent less charity care than non-participating hospitals [17]. This data provides a strong political wedge for reformers arguing that the current nonprofit monopolies are failing to deliver on their social contracts.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

Michigan represents a "Captured" market characterized by strict CON laws, nonprofit mega-system dominance, and high market concentration. However, this capture creates specific investment opportunities if federal barriers were removed.

**Highest-Opportunity Markets:**
If Section 6001 were repealed, the most attractive markets for new POH development would be the affluent, commercially insured suburbs of Detroit (such as Troy, Novi, and Rochester Hills) and the rapidly growing Grand Rapids region. These areas have the commercial payer density required to support new surgical facilities. 

**Specialty Gaps:**
The widest gap between demand and physician-owned supply exists in orthopedics, cardiology, and behavioral health. Insight Health System's recent opening of a 70-bed psychiatric hospital in Flint indicates a severe shortage of behavioral health beds that legacy systems are not adequately addressing [7]. 

**Payer Mix and Viability:**
Michigan has an aging population, with Medicare covering 52.8 percent of inpatient days and Medicaid covering 26.3 percent [2]. Commercial insurance accounts for the remaining roughly 21 percent. Blue Cross Blue Shield of Michigan dominates the commercial market with a 67 percent market share [18]. This heavy reliance on government payers and a monopolistic commercial insurer makes independent hospital development challenging. Any new POH would need to secure favorable contracts with BCBS of Michigan or focus heavily on Medicare Advantage populations, which grew by 108,000 members in 2023 [2].

The most viable investment strategy in Michigan is the "Insight Model": acquiring distressed, closed, or divested community hospitals from legacy systems (such as Ascension's current divestitures) and converting them into specialized, physician-led facilities. This approach bypasses the need to secure a new CON for hospital beds, utilizing existing licensed capacity to introduce physician-owned competition into the market.
