# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Arkansas

**Target State:** Arkansas (AR)
**Focus:** Physician-Owned Hospitals & Market Intelligence
**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report

---

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Arkansas's Hospital Market?

The hospital market in Arkansas is controlled by a mix of large non-profit health systems, with a few for-profit entities also present. The market is led by several key players, with Baptist Health, Mercy, and CHI St. Vincent emerging as the most dominant systems in terms of facilities, revenue, and overall market presence.

**Baptist Health** is the largest non-profit healthcare organization in Arkansas, operating 12 hospitals across the state. It has a commanding presence in the Little Rock metropolitan area, with its flagship hospital, Baptist Health Medical Center - Little Rock, being the largest in the state with 718 staffed beds. **Mercy**, another major non-profit Catholic healthcare organization, has a strong presence in Northwest Arkansas and the Fort Smith area. **CHI St. Vincent**, a member of the national non-profit health system CommonSpirit Health, is a prominent provider in Central and Southwest Arkansas.

Northwest Arkansas has recently experienced a significant market consolidation event. In March 2026, Community Health Systems (CHS), a for-profit system, announced the sale of its four hospitals in the region to Freeman Health System of Missouri for $112 million. This transaction includes Northwest Medical Center – Bentonville, Northwest Medical Center – Springdale, Northwest Medical Center – Willow Creek Women’s Hospital, and Siloam Springs Regional Hospital.

Arkansas is a Certificate of Need (CON) state. CON laws require healthcare providers to obtain state approval before building new facilities, expanding existing ones, or offering new services. The combination of Arkansas's CON laws and Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) creates a formidable double barrier for physicians seeking to establish their own hospitals in the state. They must navigate the complex CON application process while also contending with federal restrictions on physician ownership.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Physician-owned hospitals (POHs) in Arkansas represent a small but significant component of the state's healthcare landscape, often specializing in surgical or cardiac care and demonstrating high performance in quality metrics.

**Arkansas Heart Hospital**
Founded in 1997 by a group of cardiologists led by Dr. Bruce Murphy, the Arkansas Heart Hospital in Little Rock is arguably the most significant physician-owned hospital in Arkansas. It is a 112-bed acute-care facility that is a leader in cardiac care in the state and has earned a five-star rating from CMS. The hospital was initially a partnership with Medcath Corporation, but in 2010, Dr. Murphy led a buyout to make it a fully locally-owned institution, now under the ownership of AR-MED LLC. In 2020, the hospital expanded its footprint with the opening of the Arkansas Heart Hospital Encore Medical Center in Bryant.

**Arkansas Surgical Hospital (ASH)**
Located in North Little Rock, ASH is a prominent example of a grandfathered POH. It is an acute-care facility with 49 beds, specializing in orthopedic and spine surgery. Founded by surgeons, the hospital is owned by a group of physicians, allowing for direct physician oversight of patient care. ASH boasts a five-star rating from CMS and has been recognized with the 2024 and 2025 Human Experience (HX) Guardian of Excellence Award® by Press Ganey.

**Physicians' Specialty Hospital (Acquired)**
Physicians' Specialty Hospital in Fayetteville represents a trend of POHs being acquired by larger health systems. Originally a physician-owned facility specializing in orthopedic surgery, spine surgery, and pain management, it was acquired by Community Health Systems (CHS) in 2016. In December 2025, Washington Regional Medical Center assumed operations of the hospital, renaming it Washington Regional Physicians’ Specialty Hospital.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Arkansas

Section 6001 of the ACA effectively halted the development of new POHs in Arkansas and severely restricted the growth of those already in operation by prohibiting them from expanding the number of operating rooms, procedure rooms, or beds.

Remaining POHs in Arkansas, such as Arkansas Surgical Hospital and Arkansas Heart Hospital, operate under significant constraints imposed by the expansion ban. The inability to add beds or operating rooms without losing Medicare eligibility presents a major obstacle to growth. To circumvent these restrictions, Dr. Bruce Murphy of the Arkansas Heart Hospital gave up his practice to become a non-practicing physician, which allowed him to develop the Arkansas Heart Hospital Encore Medical Center in Bryant as a new facility, since he was no longer a referring physician.

If Section 6001 were to be repealed, the competitive landscape in Arkansas would likely see a resurgence of interest in the POH model. Given the success of existing POHs in specialties like cardiology and orthopedics, it is probable that new facilities would emerge in these and other high-demand specialty areas. Metro areas such as Little Rock and the rapidly growing Northwest Arkansas region would be prime locations for such developments.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

**Federal Delegation**
Arkansas is represented in the U.S. Senate by Senators Tom Cotton (R) and John Boozman (R). Senator John Boozman has demonstrated clear support for physician-owned hospitals. He is a cosponsor of S. 1390, the "Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act," and H.R. 4002, the "Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act of 2025." Senator Tom Cotton has not taken a direct stance on POH-specific legislation but has focused on rural healthcare affordability and access.

Arkansas's four-member House delegation shows varying levels of engagement. Representative Bruce Westerman (R-AR, 4th District) is the most active supporter, cosponsoring H.R. 4002 and introducing "The Fair Care Act" to promote competition. Representatives Rick Crawford, French Hill, and Steve Womack have not sponsored specific POH-related bills but generally align with principles of market competition.

**State-Level Context**
Arkansas law, specifically the Medical Corporation Act, permits the formation of medical corporations owned and operated by licensed medical professionals. However, Arkansas maintains a robust Certificate of Need (CON) program. The Cicero Institute's 2024 report assigns Arkansas a CON Restrictiveness Index score of 60, ranking it the 15th most restrictive state in the nation. The Arkansas Health Services Permit Commission administers the CON program, which requires approval for new hospitals, ambulatory surgery centers, and major medical equipment.

The Arkansas Hospital Association (AHA) has historically opposed the expansion of physician-owned hospitals, arguing that these facilities engage in "cherry-picking" profitable patients, which destabilizes community hospitals.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

A significant portion of Arkansas qualifies as a healthcare professional shortage area. According to a report by the Arkansas Center for Research in Economics, 59 of the state's 75 counties are classified as medically underserved.

**Specialty Gaps**
Arkansas faces a significant shortage of physicians across various specialties. A 2018 report highlighted severe gaps in Neurology (population per specialist: 86,892 vs. recommended 22,727), Psychiatry, Urology, Gastroenterology, and Pulmonology. A large percentage of these specialists are over 60 years old, suggesting shortages will worsen.

**Payer Mix and Demographics**
The payer mix in Arkansas is approximately 42% employer-sponsored insurance, 25% Medicaid, and 16% Medicare. The high percentage of Medicare and Medicaid patients, particularly in rural areas, requires careful financial planning for any new POH to ensure viability by attracting sufficient commercially insured patients.

Arkansas has an aging population, with 24% of the population over age 60 in 2020-2024. This demographic shift will drive increased demand for healthcare services, particularly in cardiology, oncology, and orthopedics. Furthermore, Northwest Arkansas is one of the fastest-growing metropolitan areas in the United States, adding an estimated 38 people per day, creating a significant demand for new healthcare services and infrastructure in that region.
