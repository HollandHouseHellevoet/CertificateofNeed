# The Rojas Report: Missouri State Intelligence Brief

**Date:** March 14, 2026  
**Subject:** Missouri Healthcare Market Analysis, Physician-Owned Hospitals, and Section 6001 Impact  
**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report Architecture

## Executive Summary

Missouri represents a highly consolidated, anti-competitive healthcare market where Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act has effectively eliminated physician-owned hospital (POH) competition. The state currently has **zero** operating traditional physician-owned hospitals, following the divestiture of physician shares at Ozarks Community Hospital in 2011 due to ACA restrictions [1]. 

The absence of physician-led competition has allowed mega-systems like BJC HealthCare, Mercy, and SSM Health to dominate the market. In St. Louis, just three health systems control over 90% of inpatient utilization and emergency department visits [2]. This consolidation, protected by Missouri's restrictive Certificate of Need (CON) laws, has contributed to rising healthcare costs, with Missouri families now spending nearly 10% of their median household income on employer-provided health insurance premiums and deductibles [3]. Furthermore, the lack of competition has not protected rural access; 21 hospitals have closed in Missouri since 2014, with 12 of those being rural facilities [4].

## Section 6001 Impact: The Eradication of POHs in Missouri

The impact of Section 6001 on Missouri's healthcare landscape is stark and measurable. Prior to the ACA's passage in 2010, physician ownership was a viable model in the state. However, the ban on expansion and new Medicare certification for POHs forced immediate market changes.

The most prominent example is Ozarks Community Hospital in Springfield, Missouri. In February 2011, shortly after the ACA's passage, the physician shareholders of SGOH Acquisition (the company owning the hospital) were forced to divest their shares [1]. The divestiture was explicitly driven by the healthcare reform provisions restricting the expansion of physician-owned hospitals. Today, analysis of CMS Hospital All Owners data reveals no traditional physician-owned hospitals operating within Missouri's borders [5]. 

While some specialty facilities exist near the border, such as the Kansas City Orthopaedic Institute (a joint venture with Saint Luke's), this facility is located in Leawood, Kansas, not Missouri [5]. The only recent physician-led hospital activity in the state is the December 2025 opening of Archview ER & Hospital in North St. Louis by Nutex Health, a publicly traded, physician-owned company operating a micro-hospital model rather than a traditional full-service hospital [6].

## Market Consolidation and Mega-System Dominance

Without the competitive pressure of physician-owned facilities, Missouri's hospital market has become highly concentrated. The state is dominated by large, predominantly non-profit integrated delivery networks (IDNs).

The concentration is particularly severe in major metropolitan areas. A December 2024 report by the Midwest Health Initiative found that in the St. Louis market, three health systems controlled over 90% of the market for inpatient utilization and emergency department visits, and 70% of all outpatient surgeries [2]. Springfield, Missouri, was previously identified by the Health Care Cost Institute as the most highly concentrated inpatient hospital market in the United States, with a Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) score of 0.78 (where 1.0 represents a pure monopoly) [9].

## Regulatory Environment: Certificate of Need Laws

Missouri's market consolidation is shielded by restrictive Certificate of Need (CON) laws, which have been in place since 1979 [10]. The Cicero Institute ranks Missouri as the 33rd most restrictive state in the nation, tied with Alabama, assigning it a restrictiveness score of 80 points [11].

Missouri's CON program requires state approval for both medical inpatient facilities (hospitals) and medical outpatient facilities (ambulatory surgical centers) [11]. This regulatory barrier serves as an incumbent protection mechanism, preventing new market entrants—including independent physicians seeking to open ASCs—from competing with established mega-systems. The requirement to prove "community need" to a state board heavily favors well-capitalized hospital systems with dedicated lobbying resources over independent physician groups.

## Rural Hospital Crisis

The American Hospital Association (AHA) frequently argues that the ban on physician-owned hospitals is necessary to protect traditional hospitals and ensure rural access to care. The data in Missouri contradicts this assertion. Despite the complete absence of POH competition and the presence of massive, consolidated health systems, rural healthcare in Missouri is collapsing.

According to the Missouri Hospital Association, 21 hospitals have closed in the state since 2014 [4]. Of these closures, 12 were rural hospitals, including facilities in Osceola, Sweet Springs, Farmington, Boonville, and Kennett [4]. The collapse of the private equity-backed Noble Health in 2022 led to the shuttering of two additional rural facilities: Callaway Community Hospital and Audrain Community Hospital [12]. The protectionist policies of Section 6001 and state CON laws have failed to prevent the erosion of rural healthcare access in Missouri.

## Payer Mix and Economic Burden

The financial burden of this consolidated, uncompetitive market falls heavily on Missouri employers and patients. A February 2026 report highlighted that Missouri and Kansas families now spend nearly 10% of their median household income on employer-provided health insurance premiums and deductibles [3].

The payer mix for Missouri hospitals reflects a heavy reliance on government programs, which systems use to justify higher commercial rates. According to the Missouri Hospital Association, the average payer mix includes 46.4% Medicare/Medicare Advantage and 18.1% Medicaid/Medicaid Managed Care [13]. Hospitals argue that they must absorb Medicare reductions by contracting higher payment rates from the 29.2% of their business that comes from commercial insurance [13]. This cost-shifting is exacerbated by the lack of lower-cost, high-quality alternatives like physician-owned hospitals.

## Political Landscape and Legislative Action

Missouri is a Republican trifecta state, with Governor Mike Kehoe taking office in January 2025. Despite the conservative political leaning, the state expanded Medicaid in 2021 following a voter-approved ballot initiative [14].

At the federal level, Missouri's congressional delegation has been active on healthcare issues, though not leading the charge on POH reform. Senator Josh Hawley (R-MO) introduced the Protect Medicaid and Rural Hospitals Act in July 2025 to reverse Medicaid cuts he had previously voted for in a larger budgetary package [15]. However, neither Senator Hawley nor Senator Eric Schmitt (R-MO) have been prominent vocal champions of H.R. 2191 or S. 1390, the bipartisan legislation aimed at repealing the Section 6001 ban on physician-owned hospitals.

## Strategic Opportunities for Physician Independence

Despite the restrictive environment, there are emerging opportunities for physician independence in Missouri:

1.  **Ambulatory Surgery Center (ASC) Partnerships:** Companies like NueHealth are expanding in Missouri, announcing new ASC management partnerships in St. Joseph and Columbia in January 2026 [16]. These physician-aligned models offer an alternative to hospital employment, though they must navigate the state's CON process.
2.  **Micro-Hospital Models:** The entry of Nutex Health into the St. Louis market demonstrates that physician-owned entities can still operate by utilizing the micro-hospital model, focusing on emergency and short-stay care in underserved areas [6].
3.  **Legislative Advocacy:** Missouri presents a prime target for advocacy regarding the repeal of Section 6001. The state perfectly illustrates the failure of the AHA's arguments: POHs were eliminated, mega-systems consolidated, costs rose, and rural hospitals closed anyway. Engaging Senators Hawley and Schmitt with this data could build crucial support for S. 1390.
