# New Mexico Market Intelligence Brief: Physician-Owned Hospitals

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls New Mexico's Hospital Market?

The New Mexico hospital market is highly consolidated and dominated by a mix of large nonprofit systems, an academic medical center, and significant private equity-backed for-profit entities. According to KFF, all four of New Mexico's metropolitan areas are rated as highly concentrated [1]. The state is unique nationally for its high degree of private equity hospital ownership, with 36.2 percent of all private non-government hospitals backed by private equity firms, the highest proportion of any state in the country [2]. 

The dominant hospital systems operating in New Mexico are ranked below by their number of facilities and net patient revenue based on July 2024 data from Definitive Healthcare [3].

Presbyterian Healthcare Services is the largest locally owned nonprofit system, serving approximately one in three New Mexicans. The UNM Health System operates the state's only academic medical center and its only Level 1 Trauma Center. Lovelace Health System, owned by the Nashville-based, private equity-backed Ardent Health Services, is the dominant for-profit player, primarily concentrated in the Albuquerque metropolitan area. 

Recent market consolidation events have further concentrated healthcare delivery in the state. In October 2023, CHRISTUS Health bought out its partner to fully own St. Vincent Regional Medical Center in Santa Fe. In July 2025, CHRISTUS Health announced its acquisition of Gerald Champion Regional Medical Center in Alamogordo. Additionally, the closure of Nexus Health in Santa Fe in January 2026 removed an independent competitor from that market, leaving a significant healthcare void in the community [4].

New Mexico repealed its Certificate of Need (CON) laws for medical facilities in 1983 and currently only requires a CON for ground ambulance services [5]. The Cicero Institute assigns New Mexico a CON Restrictiveness Index score of 0 points for medical inpatient and outpatient facilities [6]. Because New Mexico lacks CON laws, the primary barrier to entry for new physician-owned hospitals is the federal expansion ban under Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act, rather than state-level regulatory hurdles.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

New Mexico currently has no operating physician-owned hospitals that are grandfathered under the Affordable Care Act. The state's physician-owned hospital landscape is defined entirely by historical facilities that were acquired by larger health systems prior to or shortly after the enactment of Section 6001.

The most significant historical physician-owned hospital in the state was the Heart Hospital of New Mexico, located in Albuquerque. Originally developed as a joint venture between local cardiologists and MedCath Corporation, the facility was a premier specialty hospital focused exclusively on cardiovascular care. In August 2011, Lovelace Health System acquired the Heart Hospital of New Mexico from MedCath for $119 million [7]. 

Following the acquisition, the facility was integrated into the Lovelace network as the Heart Hospital of New Mexico at Lovelace Medical Center. Despite the change in ownership, the facility has maintained the high-quality care model established by its physician founders. It remains New Mexico's only hospital dedicated exclusively to cardiovascular care and the only facility in the state performing left ventricular assist device (LVAD) implants [8]. The hospital has earned the Leapfrog Top Small Teaching Hospital award for five consecutive years (2020 through 2025) and maintains a Leapfrog Safety Grade of B [9]. The success and sustained quality of the Heart Hospital of New Mexico demonstrate the efficacy of the physician-owned specialty model, even after its transition to corporate ownership.

Another notable historical entity was the New Mexico Heart Institute, a large independent cardiology practice that was subsequently acquired by Lovelace Health System in 2018, further consolidating cardiovascular care under the Ardent Health Services umbrella.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on New Mexico

Since the Affordable Care Act was signed into law on March 23, 2010, New Mexico has experienced a 100 percent attrition rate of its physician-owned hospitals. The state lost its only major physician-owned specialty hospital when the Heart Hospital of New Mexico was sold to Lovelace Health System in 2011. This acquisition eliminated physician-owned competition in the cardiovascular specialty market, transferring control of the state's premier cardiac facility to a private equity-backed corporate system.

Because there are no remaining grandfathered physician-owned hospitals in New Mexico, there are no facilities currently constrained by the Section 6001 expansion ban. However, the ban entirely prevents the creation of new physician-owned facilities to address the state's severe healthcare access issues.

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in New Mexico would likely see significant changes, particularly in the Albuquerque and Santa Fe metropolitan areas. Given the high concentration of corporate and private equity ownership, independent physicians would have the opportunity to establish competing specialty hospitals in cardiology, orthopedics, and surgical care. The recent closure of Nexus Health in Santa Fe indicates a market vulnerability where a physician-owned multi-specialty hospital could successfully capture market share from the dominant CHRISTUS Health system.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

The political environment in New Mexico is currently dominated by the Democratic Party, which controls both U.S. Senate seats and all three U.S. House districts. There is currently no federal legislative support from the New Mexico delegation for the repeal of Section 6001.

At the state level, New Mexico has taken aggressive legislative action to monitor and regulate hospital consolidation, driven by concerns over private equity ownership and rising healthcare costs. In 2024, the state enacted the Health Care Consolidation Oversight Act (SB 15), which granted temporary authority to review and approve hospital mergers [10]. In 2025, the legislature passed HB 586, making this oversight authority permanent and expanding it to include acquisitions of independent healthcare practices by insurers and changes in hospital real estate control [10]. 

While New Mexico does not have CON laws restricting the construction of new hospitals, this new consolidation oversight framework means that any future acquisitions or mergers involving physician-owned facilities would be subject to strict state scrutiny. Additionally, in March 2026, the governor signed comprehensive medical malpractice reform (HB 99) aimed at reducing insurance costs to attract and retain physicians in the state [11].

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

New Mexico presents a unique and highly favorable investment landscape for physician-owned hospital development if Section 6001 were repealed, primarily due to its lack of CON laws and severe physician shortage. The state is currently facing a critical healthcare access crisis, with 32 of its 33 counties designated as Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs) and over 1 million residents living in these underserved regions [12]. 

The most significant opportunities for new physician-owned facilities exist in the Albuquerque metropolitan area and the Santa Fe market. The Albuquerque market is heavily consolidated between Presbyterian and Lovelace, creating an opening for a high-quality, physician-led alternative. In Santa Fe, the recent closure of Nexus Health has left a void in surgical and specialty care that a physician-owned hospital could fill. Furthermore, rural areas such as Valencia County, which is currently seeing the construction of a new 15-bed Lovelace affiliate hospital, demonstrate the demand for localized acute care facilities [13].

The specialties with the widest gap between demand and supply include orthopedics, general surgery, and behavioral health. The high proportion of private equity ownership in the state has led to physician dissatisfaction, creating a strong potential pool of physician investors who desire clinical autonomy and an alternative to corporate employment.

However, the payer mix in New Mexico presents a structural challenge for new hospital development. The state has one of the highest Medicaid enrollment rates in the country, with 34.3 percent of the population covered by Medicaid or CHIP [14]. The approximate payer mix for New Mexico hospitals is 15.9 percent Medicare, 23.1 percent Medicaid, and 63.4 percent Commercial/Self-pay/Other [15]. Because Medicaid reimbursement rates are generally lower than commercial rates, any new physician-owned hospital would need to carefully balance its service lines and geographic placement to ensure a sustainable proportion of commercial insurance revenue while fulfilling community needs.
