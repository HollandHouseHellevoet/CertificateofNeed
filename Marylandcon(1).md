# Maryland

## 1. Regulatory Status & History  
- **CON Law:** Maryland has a statewide Certificate-of-Need program in effect.  The Maryland Health‑General Code §19-120 created the CON law, which first took effect in 1968【26†L304-L308】.  (Early studies note Maryland adopted CON soon after New York’s 1967 law【18†L259-L263】.)  
- **Establishment Year:** The program has been active since 1968【26†L304-L308】.  It has never been fully repealed and remains in force in 2026.  
- **Major Changes:**  A major statutory reform occurred in 2006, when the legislature raised the capital thresholds (to $10 million for hospitals and $5 million for other facilities)【85†L13-L21】.  These thresholds are now indexed to inflation each year.  Most other CON requirements have remained in place; in late 2023 Maryland adopted new COMAR regulations tightening the criteria for intervention (see below), but there has been no wholesale repeal of the CON law【45†L133-L140】.

## 2. The Process (The Gritty Details)  
- **Review Authority:**  CON applications in Maryland are reviewed and approved by the **Maryland Health Care Commission (MHCC)**, specifically its Health Care Facility Services division【1†L55-L60】.  The MHCC is the state agency that issues certificates and oversees the CON process.  
- **Application Fee:**  Maryland law authorizes the MHCC to set a CON application fee by regulation【127†L1-L4】.  (The statute does not fix an amount; fees vary by project type as defined in COMAR.)  In practice, sponsors must pay the MHCC’s published fee schedule when filing a CON application.  
- **Timeline:**  By regulation, the MHCC must act on applications within fixed time limits.  If no public evidentiary hearing is held, the Commission must decide within **90 days** of a complete application; if a hearing is held, within **150 days**【79†L336-L340】.  Importantly, if the MHCC takes no final action within 120 days of docketing (for uncontested projects), the application is *deemed approved* by law【79†L336-L340】.  In practice, many CON cases conclude within a few months, but the regulatory deadlines ensure timeliness.  
- **Intervention Rights:**  Affected providers can formally intervene in CON proceedings.  Maryland law and regulations allow “interested parties” (usually competing hospitals or physicians) to file comments or objections during the review.  Under the latest (2023) rules, any intervenor must show a likely adverse impact on quality or resources to justify opposition【45†L133-L140】.  (Before 2023, the regulations did not explicitly require such a showing.)  Thus, while competitors can object, they must meet the regulatory standards to influence the outcome【45†L133-L140】.

## 3. Scope of Regulation  
- **Covered Facilities/Services:**  Maryland’s CON law applies to a broad list of facilities and services.  Covered projects include **new or expanded hospitals**, nursing homes, and residential treatment centers【38†L214-L221】.  It also covers **ambulatory surgical facilities** with two or more operating rooms, intermediate care facilities, and **home health and hospice agencies**【38†L214-L221】【39†L239-L247】.  In addition, the law explicitly covers “specialized health services” – for example, organ transplant programs, open-heart surgery, neonatal intensive care units, burn units, and similar specialty services【39†L239-L247】.  (Home health agencies and hospices are regulated if they are new or relocating.)  
- **Capital Threshold:**  Projects that involve substantial capital investment trigger CON review.  Maryland law sets a **base threshold of $10 million** for hospital construction or expansion (2013 law §19-120(k))【125†L353-L361】【85†L13-L21】.  For other facilities (nursing homes, ASCs, etc.), the base threshold is **$5 million** (both figures as of 2006).  These dollar thresholds are adjusted annually for inflation.  For example, in 2014 the inflation-adjusted thresholds were about \$11.75 million for hospitals and \$5.90 million for other facilities【85†L35-L43】.  Any project with capital costs exceeding the current threshold must apply for a CON.  

## 4. Market Concentration (Who Benefits)  
- **Largest Health Systems:**  Maryland’s health system landscape is dominated by a few large IDNs.  The **Johns Hopkins Health System** (Baltimore-based) and **MedStar Health** (based in Columbia/Baltimore and D.C.) are the largest.  Hopkins reported roughly **\$8.6–8.7 billion** in operating revenue in FY2023【96†L1-L4】.  MedStar’s FY2025 net operating revenue is about **\$9.0 billion**【97†L185-L193】.  The **University of Maryland Medical System (UMMS)** is the next largest; its flagship academic hospital (UM Medical Center) had about **\$1.85 billion** in net patient revenue in recent data【90†L243-L250】 (and the full UMMS system comprises multiple hospitals totaling several billion).  Other sizable systems include **LifeBridge Health** (Sinai, Northwest, Mercy, GBMC) and **Adventist HealthCare**, but each is considerably smaller than Hopkins/MedStar/UMMS.  
- **Local Concentration:**  There are no simple public stats on city-level market share.  However, anecdotal patterns suggest Hopkins and UMMS dominate Baltimore city and region, MedStar dominates parts of suburban Baltimore/Washington, etc.  No source quantified a specific “80% share in City Y.”  (Overall, Maryland has very high hospital system affiliation: 100% of acute-care hospitals are system-owned【103†L7-L10】, indicating near-complete consolidation.)  In short, a few systems control most hospitals statewide, but detailed local market-share numbers are not published.  
- **Health Insurers:**  The commercial insurance market is also concentrated.  **CareFirst BlueCross BlueShield** (the BCBS plan) is by far the largest carrier, covering roughly **61.5%** of Maryland’s fully insured market【130†L1-L4】.  The next largest is **Kaiser Permanente** (a provider-integrated HMO), with about **21.3%** market share【129†L4505-L4510】.  Other national carriers (UnitedHealthcare, Aetna (CVS), Cigna) hold the remaining share.  (CareFirst’s share falls to ~48% if self-funded plans are included【130†L12-L15】.)  No other insurer has anywhere near the top two’s scale.  
- **Provider-Sponsored Plans:**  Maryland has no major hospital system–owned health plan of its own.  Kaiser (21% share) is a national integrated provider-insurer, not owned by a Maryland hospital system.  Aside from Kaiser, there are **no significant provider-owned insurance companies** in Maryland.  (No dominant hospital group in Maryland operates its own plan with measurable market share in filings.)

## 5. Case Law & Scandals  
- **Documented Denials:**  We found *no publicly reported cases* of a Maryland CON application being outright denied with named parties.  The MHCC publishes approval statistics but not detailed case reports.  (For example, one MHCC status report noted zero projects denied in a given period【124†L0-L6】.)  In searching news and regulatory documents, we identified no specific instances of a provider’s expansion being refused or withdrawn.  There is no accessible record of a contested denial decision.  **Data not publicly available** on individual denial cases – if any have occurred, they have not been widely documented.  
- **Notable Litigation/Scandals:**  Similarly, we found no Maryland court cases or scandals centered on CON denials.  (Nationally, CON laws have faced criticism and legal challenges, but none stood out as involving Maryland specifically.)  In short, aside from general critiques of CON, there were no identifiable high-profile scandals or lawsuit outcomes to report.
