# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Idaho
**Topic:** Physician-Owned Hospitals & Market Intelligence
**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Idaho's Hospital Market?

Idaho's healthcare market is defined by a unique combination of rapid population growth, a consolidated hospital landscape dominated by a few large nonprofit systems, and a legacy of deregulation that sets it apart from many other states. As one of the fastest-growing states in the nation, Idaho is experiencing significant strain on its healthcare infrastructure, a factor that amplifies the strategic importance of existing hospital assets and market positions.

### Dominant Hospital Systems

The hospital market in Idaho is largely controlled by three major nonprofit health systems: St. Luke's Health System, Saint Alphonsus Health System, and Kootenai Health. These systems command significant market share in their respective regions, with a smaller but notable presence from for-profit operators like HCA Healthcare and the expanding influence of Utah-based Intermountain Health.

| System/Hospital | Parent Company | System Type | Primary Service Area(s) | Key Idaho Facilities |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| St. Luke's Health System | St. Luke's Health System, Ltd. | Nonprofit | Boise, Magic Valley, Wood River | 8 hospitals, including St. Luke's Boise Medical Center |
| Saint Alphonsus Health System | Trinity Health | Nonprofit (Catholic) | Treasure Valley (Boise, Nampa) | Hospitals in Boise, Nampa, and medical centers across the region |
| Kootenai Health | Kootenai Health | Nonprofit | North Idaho (Coeur d'Alene) | Kootenai Health Medical Center (381 beds) |
| Eastern Idaho Regional Medical Center (EIRMC) | HCA Healthcare | For-Profit | Eastern Idaho (Idaho Falls) | 318-bed full-service hospital |
| Intermountain Health | Intermountain Health | Nonprofit | Expanding in Eastern & Southern Idaho | Partnership with Mountain View & Idaho Falls Community Hospital |

**St. Luke's Health System** is the largest healthcare provider in Idaho, operating as a locally-based, not-for-profit entity. It boasts a commanding market share, estimated at approximately 68% in its five-county primary service area centered around Boise [1]. The system's flagship, St. Luke's Boise Medical Center, is the state's highest-grossing hospital with a net patient revenue of $1.9 billion [2].

**Saint Alphonsus Health System**, part of the national Catholic healthcare giant Trinity Health, is the primary competitor to St. Luke's in the populous Treasure Valley. Headquartered in Boise, Saint Alphonsus operates as a nonprofit ministry and has a significant presence in both southwestern Idaho and eastern Oregon. Its Boise hospital is the second-largest in the state by revenue [2].

**Kootenai Health**, based in Coeur d'Alene, is the dominant provider in North Idaho. Until recently, it operated as a public hospital district but converted to a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization in early 2024 [3].

For-profit presence in Idaho is led by **HCA Healthcare's Eastern Idaho Regional Medical Center (EIRMC)** in Idaho Falls. As the largest medical facility in the region, EIRMC serves as a major referral center for a vast geographic area. It competes directly with the physician-owned Idaho Falls Community Hospital and Mountain View Hospital.

### Market Consolidation and Antitrust Scrutiny

Despite the absence of CON laws, Idaho's hospital market is highly consolidated, particularly in the Boise and Coeur d'Alene regions. This concentration has led to significant antitrust challenges from both federal and state regulators.

In a landmark case, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) successfully sued to block **St. Luke's Health System's** 2012 acquisition of Saltzer Medical Group, one of the largest independent physician practices in the state. The courts agreed with the FTC and rival Saint Alphonsus that the merger would have given St. Luke's excessive market power over primary care physicians in the Boise area, leading to higher prices. St. Luke's was ultimately forced to divest the practice in 2014.

More recently, in May 2024, the Idaho Attorney General took action against **Kootenai Health**, requiring it to end a management and operational agreement with Syringa Hospital, a smaller facility in a neighboring county. The state argued that the affiliation between the two entities, which included shared leadership, reduced competition in the region [4].

### The Regulatory Environment: A Market Without a Gatekeeper

Idaho repealed its Certificate of Need (CON) laws in 1983. CON laws typically require healthcare providers to obtain state approval before building new facilities or offering new services, acting as a form of market entry control. The repeal in Idaho theoretically opened the door to greater competition and development.

However, the passage of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) in 2010 introduced Section 6001, which placed a federal moratorium on the creation of new physician-owned hospitals that wish to participate in the Medicare program. Existing POHs were grandfathered in, but the law effectively prevents new physician-led hospitals from entering the market and billing federal health programs. Therefore, while Idaho removed its own state-level barrier to entry (CON), a significant federal barrier remains. This dynamic makes the few existing, grandfathered POHs in Idaho exceptionally valuable.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Idaho's lack of Certificate of Need (CON) laws has created a favorable environment for POH development, allowing these facilities to emerge as significant players in their local healthcare ecosystems.

### Operating Physician-Owned Hospitals

**Treasure Valley Hospital (Boise):** Treasure Valley Hospital (TVH) is a physician-owned surgical and imaging specialty hospital in Boise. Established around 2000, it was formerly known as HealthSouth Treasure Valley Hospital and is affiliated with Surgical Care Affiliates (SCA). TVH provides a range of specialized services, including anesthesiology, cardiology, ear, nose, and throat (ENT), general surgery, neurosurgery, orthopedics, radiology, and total joint replacements. TVH has established a strong competitive position by emphasizing its high ratings for patient satisfaction. The hospital's website claims it is the only hospital in the Boise area to receive a five-star rating from the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) for patient satisfaction [5].

**Mountain View Hospital (Idaho Falls):** Mountain View Hospital (MVH) is a multi-specialty, physician-owned hospital in Idaho Falls. Since its establishment in 2000, MVH has grown to become a significant provider in the Eastern Idaho region, competing directly with the HCA-owned Eastern Idaho Regional Medical Center (EIRMC). In April 2023, Mountain View Hospital and its affiliate, Idaho Falls Community Hospital, announced a strategic partnership with Intermountain Health, a large, non-profit health system based in Salt Lake City, Utah. Under the terms of the agreement, Intermountain Health became a minority investor in both hospitals. However, both MVH and Idaho Falls Community Hospital will remain locally owned and managed, retaining control of their day-to-day operations [6].

**Northwest Specialty Hospital (Post Falls):** Northwest Specialty Hospital (NWSH) is a multi-specialty surgical hospital located in Post Falls, serving the North Idaho region. The hospital has a partnership with Surgery Partners (NASDAQ: SGRY), a national operator of surgical facilities. In August 2022, the real estate assets of Northwest Specialty Hospital, including its campus and buildings, were sold to Hammes Partners, a healthcare real estate investment firm, for $67.5 million. Despite this sale, the hospital's operations remain under physician ownership, with the hospital now leasing the facility from Hammes Partners [7].

**Idaho Falls Community Hospital (Idaho Falls):** Idaho Falls Community Hospital (IFCH) is a full-service community hospital that opened in 2019. It is a physician-owned facility and an affiliate of Mountain View Hospital. The hospital was established to provide an alternative to the existing hospital in Idaho Falls and to increase competition in the local market. Since its opening, IFCH has experienced significant growth and has established itself as a key competitor to HCA's Eastern Idaho Regional Medical Center (EIRMC).

### Most Significant POH in Idaho

While all four POHs play important roles in their respective markets, the combined entity of **Mountain View Hospital and Idaho Falls Community Hospital** in Idaho Falls is arguably the most significant POH in Idaho. The two hospitals, operating in close affiliation, represent a significant challenge to the incumbent HCA-owned hospital in Idaho Falls, creating a more competitive and dynamic market. The recent partnership with Intermountain Health provides access to capital, expertise, and a broader network of care, positioning the two hospitals for future growth and success.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Idaho

Section 6001 of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA), enacted in 2010, effectively halted the growth of physician-owned hospitals (POHs) by prohibiting new facilities from billing Medicare and severely restricting the expansion of existing ones.

### POH Attrition and Attrition Rate in Idaho

Since the enactment of the ACA on March 23, 2010, Idaho has not experienced any outright closures of its grandfathered physician-owned hospitals. The state had three POHs on the original OIG list that were grandfathered in under the ACA: Treasure Valley Hospital in Boise, Mountain View Hospital in Idaho Falls, and Northwest Specialty Hospital in Post Falls. All three of these hospitals remain in operation today. Given that none of the original three grandfathered POHs have closed, the attrition rate for physician-owned hospitals in Idaho since 2010 is 0%.

### Expansion Constraints on Existing POHs

The expansion ban imposed by Section 6001 has been a significant constraint on Idaho's existing POHs. These hospitals are unable to add new beds, operating rooms, or procedure rooms to meet the growing demand for their services. This is particularly problematic in a state like Idaho, which has experienced rapid population growth over the past decade.

Mountain View Hospital in Idaho Falls provides a clear example of these constraints. In 2024, the hospital submitted an expansion exception request to the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS), seeking to increase its capacity. However, the request was later withdrawn, and a rescission notice was published in the Federal Register in April 2025 [8]. While the reasons for the withdrawal were not made public, the episode highlights the challenges that POHs face in trying to expand their facilities to meet community needs.

### The Opening of Idaho Falls Community Hospital

Despite the restrictions of Section 6001, a new physician-owned hospital, Idaho Falls Community Hospital, opened in 2019. This was made possible through a creative legal and organizational structure. The hospital was established as a separate entity from Mountain View Hospital, although it is closely affiliated with it and shares a joint entrance and lobby. By creating a new, legally distinct hospital, the physician-owners were able to bypass the ACA's prohibition on new POHs. This case demonstrates the strong demand for physician-owned facilities in Idaho and the lengths to which physicians will go to create them.

### The Potential Impact of Repealing Section 6001

The repeal of Section 6001 would likely have a significant impact on Idaho's healthcare landscape. It would open the door for the creation of new POHs, which would increase competition and provide patients with more choices. Given Idaho's rapid population growth and the high demand for healthcare services, it is likely that new POHs would be established in the state's metropolitan areas, such as Boise and Idaho Falls. These new hospitals would likely focus on high-demand surgical specialties, such as orthopedics, cardiology, and neurosurgery.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

### Federal Legislative Landscape

Idaho's representation in the U.S. Congress reflects a generally conservative and free-market-oriented political philosophy, which often aligns with the principles of physician-owned hospitals (POHs). However, direct legislative action from Idaho's federal representatives on recent POH-related bills has been limited.

Idaho is represented in the U.S. Senate by Senators Jim Risch and Mike Crapo, both Republicans. A review of recent federal legislation aimed at reforming or repealing the Affordable Care Act's (ACA) restrictions on POHs reveals that neither senator has sponsored or co-sponsored the key bills in the 119th Congress (H.R. 4002, H.R. 2191, H.R. 3022). Similarly, in the U.S. House of Representatives, Idaho's congressional delegation has not been at the forefront of sponsoring or co-sponsoring the aforementioned POH-related legislation.

### State-Level Regulatory Landscape

Idaho’s regulatory environment is notably less restrictive than that of many other states, a factor that has significant implications for the development and operation of physician-owned hospitals.

**Certificate of Need (CON):** Idaho is one of a minority of states that has fully repealed its CON laws. The state’s CON program was eliminated in 1983, a move that has had a lasting impact on the competitive landscape of its healthcare market. The absence of CON requirements means that healthcare providers, including physicians, are not required to obtain state approval to build new facilities, expand existing ones, or offer new services. The Cicero Institute, in its 2024 report "Ranking Certificate of Need Laws in All 50 States," gives Idaho a score of 0 on its CON Restrictiveness Index, the lowest possible score.

**State Legislation and Regulatory Actions:** Beyond the repeal of its CON laws, Idaho has a regulatory framework that is generally favorable to physician ownership of healthcare facilities. While the federal Stark Law governs physician self-referral for Medicare and Medicaid patients, Idaho’s state laws do not impose additional, more restrictive limitations. In 2016, the Idaho Board of Medicine reversed its previous position that prohibited non-physicians from employing physicians, further opening the door for various ownership and investment models for medical practices and facilities.

### Political Orientation and Antitrust Context

Idaho is widely recognized as a conservative state with a strong preference for free-market principles, limited government intervention, and individual liberty. This political orientation is generally favorable to the POH model, which is often seen as a market-based alternative to the large, consolidated hospital systems that dominate many healthcare markets.

Despite its free-market orientation, Idaho has also demonstrated a willingness to challenge anticompetitive behavior in the healthcare market. The state has been the site of two major antitrust cases in recent years (FTC v. St. Luke's Health System and Idaho Attorney General v. Kootenai Health), which have had a significant impact on the structure and dynamics of its healthcare market. These antitrust cases create a more level playing field for independent healthcare providers, including POHs.

## 5. Investment and Opportunity Landscape

### Idaho's Healthcare Market: A Story of Rapid Growth and Persistent Gaps

Idaho's economy is experiencing a period of unprecedented growth, and the healthcare sector is a significant contributor to this expansion. The state's population is booming, with the Boise metropolitan area ranking among the fastest-growing in the United States. The healthcare sector in Idaho is a major economic driver, contributing over $10.4 billion in economic activity annually [9]. The hospital industry alone has seen an average annual growth rate of 5.7% between 2020 and 2025 [10].

However, this growth is not without its challenges. Idaho faces a significant shortage of healthcare professionals, particularly physicians. The state ranks last in the nation for active physicians per capita, a situation exacerbated by a recent trend of doctors leaving the state [11]. This creates a critical gap between the supply of and demand for healthcare services, presenting both a challenge for the state and a significant opportunity for investment and expansion.

### Payer Mix and Financial Viability

The financial viability of new physician-owned hospitals (POHs) is heavily influenced by the state's payer mix. In Idaho, the payer landscape is a mix of employer-sponsored insurance, government programs, and individual plans. According to 2024 data, the approximate breakdown is as follows [12]:

| Payer Type          | Percentage |
| ------------------- | ---------- |
| Employer-Sponsored  | 48%        |
| Medicaid            | 18%        |
| Medicare            | 16%        |
| Non-Group/Individual| 7%         |

This payer mix presents both opportunities and challenges for POHs. The significant portion of commercially insured patients (Employer-Sponsored and Non-Group) is attractive, as these plans typically offer higher reimbursement rates than government programs.

### Identifying High-Opportunity Markets

Given Idaho's rapid growth and physician shortage, several markets present significant opportunities for new POH development, especially if the federal restrictions in Section 6001 of the ACA were repealed.

*   **Boise Metropolitan Area (including Meridian, Nampa, and Caldwell):** As the state's economic and population hub, the Boise metro area is a prime market for new healthcare facilities. The area's rapid growth has strained existing healthcare infrastructure, creating a need for additional capacity.
*   **Idaho Falls:** This city is already home to two successful POHs, Mountain View Hospital and Idaho Falls Community Hospital. Their success demonstrates the viability of the POH model in this market and suggests that further expansion or new specialized facilities could be successful.
*   **Coeur d'Alene and Post Falls:** Located in the rapidly growing northern panhandle, this region is another attractive market. The presence of Northwest Specialty Hospital, a POH, indicates a favorable environment for physician-led initiatives.

### Specialty-Specific Opportunities

While Idaho faces a broad-based physician shortage across nearly all specialties, certain areas present particularly acute gaps between patient demand and the available supply of specialized care:

*   **Orthopedics and Sports Medicine:** Idaho's active and growing population, combined with its aging demographic, is driving strong demand for orthopedic services.
*   **Cardiology:** Heart disease is a leading cause of death in Idaho, and the state's aging population will only increase the demand for cardiovascular care.
*   **Surgical Specialties:** As demonstrated by the success of Treasure Valley Hospital and Northwest Specialty Hospital, there is a strong market for POHs focused on surgical specialties.

### The Mountain View Model: A Blueprint for Growth

The success of Mountain View Hospital and its affiliate, Idaho Falls Community Hospital, provides a compelling blueprint for future POH development in Idaho. This model is characterized by physician leadership, strategic partnerships (such as with Intermountain Health), community focus, and incremental growth. This model could be replicated in other fast-growing markets in Idaho, such as the Boise metro area or the Coeur d'Alene region. A group of entrepreneurial physicians, backed by a strategic partner, could develop a new POH that offers a high-quality, cost-effective alternative to the dominant non-profit systems.
