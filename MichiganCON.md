# Michigan Certificate of Need & Health Market (The Rojas Report)

**Executive Summary:** Michigan maintains a Certificate of Need (CON) program, originally enacted in 1972 (effective April 1, 1973)【6†L231-L238】 and codified in the Michigan Public Health Code (Act 368 of 1978, Part 222). Key reforms occurred under the **1988 Michigan CON Reform Act** and later amendments in 1993 and 2002【6†L249-L258】【6†L273-L282】. The program remains active (no repeal) and is administered by the **Michigan Department of Health & Human Services (MDHHS)**, specifically its Bureau of Community & Health Systems, together with an 11-member Governor-appointed **Certificate of Need Commission**【14†L111-L119】【6†L249-L258】. Under Michigan law (MCL 333.22209), any person proposing certain projects must obtain a CON before proceeding【111†L165-L173】. Applications are reviewed by MDHHS staff, with final decisions by the MDHHS Director. Statutory and rule requirements include strict timelines (45/120/150 days)【111†L186-L190】, application fees (e.g. $3,000–$15,000 depending on project size)【16†L1042-L1050】, and limited appeal rights (only the **applicant** may request a hearing)【110†L2151-L2160】. Michigan’s CON covers major facilities and services – e.g. hospitals, nursing homes/HLTC units, psychiatric beds, ASCs, imaging (CT/MRI/PET), cardiac cath, NICU, transplants, etc【46†L760-L768】【46†L776-L784】 – and applies to any project exceeding the capital expenditure threshold (currently **$4,272,500** effective Jan. 1 2026【47†L1954-L1960】). Michigan’s hospital market is highly consolidated: Corewell Health (Beaumont/Spectrum) led the state with ~$15.15 billion in revenue (2023)【56†L179-L187】, followed by Henry Ford Health’s system (~$9.6 billion, 2024)【58†L227-L232】 and the University of Michigan Health System (~$7.8 billion, 2023)【64†L25-L33】. These “Big 3” accounted for over 56% of net patient revenues among the state’s largest systems【73†L149-L157】. In insurer markets, Blue Cross Blue Shield of Michigan holds a dominant share (~50% statewide as of 2025)【82†L106-L113】, with Priority Health (~13%)【82†L106-L113】 and UnitedHealthcare (~6%) close behind. Major hospital-owned plans include Corewell’s Priority Health (13%)【82†L106-L113】 and Henry Ford’s HAP (not separately reported). Documented CON denials in Michigan are scarce; one historical example is **Huron Valley Hospital, Inc.**, whose 1977 CON application for a new hospital (Clinton Township) was denied【100†L219-L227】 (local planners found it inconsistent with state criteria【100†L209-L218】). That case later led to litigation (Huron Valley Hosp. v. City of Pontiac, 1986) alleging anti‑competitive CON practices【100†L245-L254】. Aside from such litigation, no major recent scandals involving Michigan’s CON were found.  

## 1. Regulatory Status & History  
Michigan **does** have an active CON law. Legislative action in 1972 established the program (effective April 1, 1973)【6†L231-L238】. The CON statutes are now found in *Act 368 of 1978 (Public Health Code)*, Part 222, and related sections (MCL 333.22201–333.22233)【111†L165-L173】【8†L165-L173】. Major reforms were enacted over time: the 1988 Michigan CON Reform Act reorganized the program【6†L249-L258】, followed by amendments in 1993 (adding ad hoc planning committees) and 2002 (expanding the CON Commission to 11 members, among other changes)【6†L249-L258】【6†L273-L282】. The program was *not* repealed; Michigan remains among the ~36 states with CON laws【6†L231-L238】. 

## 2. The Process (Gritty Details)  
- **Review Agency/Board:**  Michigan’s CON applications are administered by **MDHHS (Dept. of Health & Human Services)**, Bureau of Community & Health Systems, in coordination with the 11-member **Certificate of Need Commission**【14†L111-L119】. The CON Commission (appointed by the Governor) approves program standards, but day-to-day review is done by MDHHS staff【14†L111-L119】. Applications are evaluated by MDHHS’s Certificate of Need Section; final decisions are issued by the MDHHS Director.  
- **Fees:**  Fees are prescribed by statute (MCL 333.22069) and adjusted periodically. As of 2023, for example, fees are tiered by project size: $3,000 for projects up to $500,000; $8,000 for $500,001–$3,999,999; $11,000 for $4,000,000–$9,999,999; and $15,000 for $10,000,000 and above【16†L1042-L1050】. Additional fees apply (e.g. $3,000 “Complex Project” fee, $1,000 expedited review, $500 LOI waiver or minor amendment, and $100 per service annual survey)【16†L1042-L1050】.  
- **Timeline:**  Michigan law sets strict review deadlines in administrative rules: once an application is deemed complete, MDHHS must issue a *proposed decision* within **45 days** for *nonsubstantive* projects, **120 days** for *substantive individual* projects, or **150 days** for *comparative review* projects【111†L186-L190】. In practice, the department has generally met these targets.  For FY2023, average review times were ~36 days (nonsubstantive), 110 days (substantive), and 148 days (comparative)【19†L637-L641】. (See table below.)  
- **Intervention/Objection:**  Michigan law **does not** grant automatic party status to incumbent providers. Only the applicant may appeal a denial. Under MCL 333.22231–32 and CON rules, if an application is denied, *only the applicant* may request a hearing to contest it【110†L2151-L2160】. There is no statutory provision allowing competitors or incumbents to intervene or formally object (Michigan’s Administrative Code Rule 551 explicitly limits hearings to applicants)【110†L2151-L2160】.

## 3. Scope of Regulation  
Michigan’s CON law applies to a broad range of facilities and services.  Under **MCL 333.22209–11** and related rules, a CON is required to: acquire or transfer ownership of a licensed facility; change bed capacity; add or replace certain clinical services; or make large capital expenditures【111†L165-L173】. “Health facility” is defined to include general hospitals, hospital long-term care (HLTC) beds, psychiatric hospitals/units, nursing homes, and freestanding surgical outpatient facilities (plus certain HMOs)【46†L760-L768】.  Specific *covered services* include neonatal intensive care, open-heart surgery, organ transplants, extracorporeal shock-wave lithotripsy, proton/megavoltage radiation therapy, PET, and all inpatient/outpatient surgical, cardiac catheterization, MRI, and CT services【46†L776-L784】. Changes like relocating an existing facility or converting hospital beds also trigger CON review【46†L770-L778】. 

Capital expenditure thresholds are indexed annually by inflation.  For example, the threshold was $3,735,000 in 2023 and $4,002,500 on Jan 1, 2024【16†L1042-L1050】; under the latest adjustment it is **$4,272,500** effective Jan 1, 2026【47†L1954-L1960】.  Any project expanding a regulated service or facility that involves spending above that threshold must obtain a CON【47†L1954-L1960】. (Minor projects below thresholds or certain new psychiatric/chemical dependency facilities may be exempted by statute or temporary moratoria, e.g. recent legislative extension for psych hospitals.)  

## 4. Market Concentration (Who Benefits)  

- **Top Health Systems (by revenue):** Michigan’s healthcare industry is dominated by three systems.  In 2023, **Corewell Health** (the merged Beaumont/Spectrum system) reported about **$15.15 billion** in total operating revenue【56†L179-L187】 (care delivery revenue ~$8.27B).  **Henry Ford Health** (including its new joint venture with Ascension Michigan) had **$9.6 billion** in operating revenue in 2024【58†L227-L232】 ($6.5B net patient revenue).  **Michigan Medicine** (University of Michigan Health System, including UM Hospital, Sparrow Health in Lansing, and UM Health-West) had roughly **$7.8 billion** in operating revenue (FY2023)【64†L25-L33】.  Together these three systems accounted for about 56.3% of net patient revenue among Michigan’s 10 largest systems【73†L149-L157】. (The next-largest is Trinity Health Michigan, about $2B behind Michigan Medicine【73†L149-L157】.)  

- **Local Market Concentration:** Many Michigan metros are highly concentrated.  For example, in Southeast Michigan the combined Henry Ford–Ascension system controls roughly half of area hospital revenues (an AMA report cited ~44.8% of patient revenue in 2023)【58†L227-L232】. In Grand Rapids, Corewell’s Spectrum/Butterworth is dominant (e.g. Butterworth’s hospital net revenue ~$3.16B vs. Trinity Health’s St. Mary’s at $0.67B)【53†L238-L246】.  In Lansing, UM’s Sparrow Health (now a UM affiliate) and Ascension/McLaren control most hospital beds.  Western and Northern Michigan are similarly clustered around Corewell and Trinity (Mercy–Munson) respectively.  (Precise local market shares are not published, but the trend is clear: one or two systems dominate each region.)  

- **Top Insurers:** The Michigan insurance market is one of the nation’s least competitive.  **Blue Cross Blue Shield of Michigan** holds by far the largest share – roughly **50–67%** of the overall market【82†L106-L113】【75†L148-L157】 – depending on the segment.  Its nearest rival is **Priority Health** (the former Corewell/Humana HMO), at about **12–13%**【75†L148-L157】【82†L106-L113】.  National carriers like UnitedHealthcare and Centene (Meridian) each cover on the order of 5–6%【82†L106-L113】.  (In employer-group plans, BCBSMI had ~67% share in 2022【75†L148-L157】 while Priority was ~12%.)  

- **Provider-Sponsored Plans:** Notable hospital-owned health plans include **Priority Health** (owned by Corewell) which covers ~13% of MI residents【82†L106-L113】, and **Health Alliance Plan (HAP)** (formerly Henry Ford/Ascension Michigan’s plan, now part of HF-Ascension).  Smaller examples are McLaren’s Medicaid/MA plans.  These provider plans are significant locally but generally much smaller than the major insurers above.  

## 5. Case Law & Scandals  
**Denied CON Applications:** Public records of Michigan CON denials are scarce.  One documented case occurred decades ago: in 1977 the state **denied Huron Valley Hospital’s CON** application to build a new hospital in Clinton Township【100†L219-L227】.  The local regional council had concluded the project “was not consistent with” planning criteria【100†L209-L218】 (favoring the nearby Pontiac General Hospital).  This denial was later overturned (the CON was granted in 1984) and became the basis of federal litigation (Huron Valley Hosp. v. City of Pontiac) alleging anti‑competitive CON practices.  In that 1986 case, Huron Valley charged that planners had conspired “to favor the continued existence of an established hospital (Pontiac General) at the expense of those… who wanted to enter the market”【100†L245-L254】.  The court found evidence of potential abuses, leading to a significant ruling (affirmed by the 6th Circuit) about the CON commission’s authority【100†L245-L254】.  

Aside from this historic example, we found no recent high‑profile Michigan CON denials or scandals.  (Michigan’s CON process is relatively opaque; denied projects are not widely publicized. No major corruption or antitrust cases tied to Michigan CON laws are documented in the public record.)
