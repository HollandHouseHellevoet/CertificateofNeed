# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Florida

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Florida's Hospital Market?

Florida's hospital market is characterized by significant consolidation and the dominance of a few large health systems, both for-profit and nonprofit. The state is home to over 300 hospitals, with the market heavily concentrated among five major players.

The dominant hospital systems operating in Florida are led by HCA Healthcare, which operates as HCA Florida Healthcare. HCA is the largest single operator in the state, managing approximately 50 hospitals. This for-profit system holds a substantial market share across multiple metropolitan areas, particularly in South Florida, Tampa Bay, and the Orlando region. Following HCA, AdventHealth, a nonprofit system, operates over 30 hospitals in the state, with a commanding presence in Central Florida. Other major players include Baptist Health South Florida, Orlando Health, and BayCare Health System, all of which are nonprofit entities that dominate their respective regional markets.

Recent years have seen continued market consolidation. Orlando Health recently completed the purchase of three hospitals and related physician practices on Florida's east central coast, expanding its footprint. Additionally, physician practice consolidation is accelerating, with Florida ranking third nationally in the number of physician group practices (over 30,500). Large systems are actively acquiring these practices to secure referral networks.

Florida represents a unique regulatory environment because it fully repealed its Certificate of Need (CON) laws for general hospitals in 2019 (effective July 2019) under Governor Ron DeSantis. Prior to this repeal, CON laws created a significant barrier to entry for new facilities. However, the interaction between Florida's deregulated state environment and the federal Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act creates a paradox. While the state allows new hospitals to be built without proving "need," federal law still prohibits new physician-owned hospitals from participating in Medicare. Given Florida's massive elderly population and the necessity of Medicare reimbursement for hospital survival, Section 6001 acts as a de facto ban on new physician-owned hospitals despite the state's free-market posture.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

The physician-owned hospital (POH) landscape in Florida is notably sparse compared to states like Texas or Oklahoma. Based on comprehensive reviews of the 2008 Office of Inspector General list of physician-owned specialty hospitals and recent academic analyses of Medicare-covered POHs, Florida has virtually no traditional, grandfathered physician-owned specialty hospitals operating under the whole-hospital exception.

Instead, physician ownership in Florida primarily takes the form of minority syndication in ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs) or joint ventures with large corporate systems, rather than independent, physician-led acute care hospitals. For example, Baptist Health and Compass Surgical Partners recently launched a network of ASCs in Northeast Florida, offering outpatient procedures across surgical specialties. While these facilities involve physician investment, they are not classified as physician-owned hospitals under Section 6001.

Because Florida lacks a robust cohort of grandfathered POHs, there is no significant data on closures, acquisitions, or quality comparisons specific to independent POHs in the state. The absence of these facilities means that patients in Florida's highly concentrated markets do not have access to the competitive alternative that physician-owned hospitals provide in other regions.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Florida

The impact of Section 6001 on Florida is profound, primarily because it preemptively stifled the development of a physician-owned hospital sector in a state that is otherwise primed for it. Because Florida did not have a large base of POHs prior to the March 23, 2010 enactment of the Affordable Care Act, the state did not experience the high attrition rates (closures or sales) seen in states with established POH markets.

However, the "loss" for Florida is measured in foregone opportunities. The federal ban prevents physicians from leveraging Florida's 2019 CON repeal to build new, competitive facilities. If Section 6001 were repealed, Florida's competitive landscape would likely transform rapidly. The state's lack of CON restrictions makes it one of the most attractive markets in the country for new hospital development.

If the ban were lifted, metropolitan areas with high population growth and heavy hospital concentration, such as Miami-Dade, Broward, Orange, and Hillsborough counties, would be prime targets for new physician-owned facilities. Specialties that are highly profitable and currently dominated by large systems, such as orthopedics, cardiology, and neurosurgery, would likely see the first wave of physician-led competition. This would introduce much-needed price competition and alternative care models to markets currently controlled by regional monopolies.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Florida's political environment is highly favorable to free-market healthcare reforms, though federal law remains the primary obstacle.

At the federal level, Florida's congressional delegation has shown support for repealing the POH ban. Representative Neal Dunn (R-FL) has been a prominent voice in healthcare policy, and several Florida representatives have historically supported free-market healthcare initiatives. The Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act (H.R. 977 / S. 470), which aims to repeal Section 6001, aligns with the general deregulatory stance of Florida's conservative leadership.

At the state level, the most significant regulatory action was the 2019 repeal of the Certificate of Need program for general hospitals, specialty hospitals, and complex medical rehabilitation centers. This legislative victory, championed by the Republican-controlled legislature and signed by Governor DeSantis, removed the primary state-level barrier to hospital construction. Florida no longer requires a CON for these facilities, making its regulatory regime one of the least restrictive in the nation regarding hospital market entry.

The Florida Medical Association (FMA) is a powerful advocacy group in the state. While their primary focus often centers on scope of practice and tort reform, the FMA generally supports policies that enhance physician autonomy and practice viability, aligning with the principles behind repealing Section 6001.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, Florida would immediately become a top-tier market for physician-owned hospital investment. The combination of zero CON requirements, a rapidly growing population, and a high concentration of specialized physicians creates an ideal environment for new entrants.

The most underserved or highest-opportunity markets are the rapidly expanding suburban corridors around Orlando, Tampa, and Jacksonville, as well as the densely populated, highly consolidated markets in South Florida. In these areas, large health systems currently dictate pricing and terms.

The widest gap between demand and physician-owned supply exists in high-margin surgical specialties, particularly orthopedics, spine surgery, and cardiovascular care. Florida's aging demographic drives immense demand for these services. Currently, physicians in these specialties are often forced into employment models or joint ventures where they cede control to corporate partners.

The payer mix in Florida is heavily skewed toward Medicare due to the state's large retiree population. Medicare and Medicare Advantage make up a massive portion of hospital revenues. This demographic reality underscores why Section 6001 is an effective ban in Florida. A hospital cannot survive in this state without accepting Medicare. If the restriction on Medicare participation for new POHs were lifted, the financial viability of physician-led hospitals in Florida would be exceptionally strong, attracting significant interest from private equity, venture capital, and independent physician groups eager to reclaim clinical and operational control.
