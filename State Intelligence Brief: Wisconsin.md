# State Intelligence Brief: Wisconsin

## 1. Executive Summary
Wisconsin represents a paradox in the healthcare landscape: it is a state without traditional Certificate of Need (CON) laws, yet it functions as a highly consolidated, "Captured" market dominated by massive nonprofit health systems. The state boasts the highest commercial payer mix in the nation (78.1%), making it an exceptionally lucrative environment for healthcare providers, which is reflected in its hospital prices being the fourth highest in the country (307% of Medicare rates) [1] [2]. Despite the lack of CON barriers, physician-owned hospital (POH) presence is virtually non-existent, with only one grandfathered facility, OakLeaf Surgical Hospital, operating in the state. The Wisconsin Hospital Association (WHA) exerts immense political influence, effectively maintaining the status quo and protecting the dominance of systems like Advocate Health, Froedtert ThedaCare, and Ascension. For POH investors and advocates, Wisconsin offers a high-reward but politically challenging environment where the primary barrier to entry is federal law (Section 6001 of the ACA) rather than state-level CON regulations.

## 2. Market Classification
**Classification: Captured**

Wisconsin is classified as a **Captured** market. While it technically lacks a traditional CON program (having repealed it by 2000), the market is overwhelmingly dominated by nonprofit mega-systems [3]. The top 10 health systems by net patient revenue and affiliated hospitals are all nonprofits, creating a de facto oligopoly that stifles competition [4]. The political environment is heavily influenced by the hospital lobby, with the WHA maintaining close ties to state leadership, including Governor Tony Evers, who recently joined the WHA Board [5]. This combination of extreme nonprofit dominance and deep political entrenchment makes Wisconsin a captured market, despite the absence of state-level regulatory barriers to entry.

## 3. POH Landscape
The physician-owned hospital footprint in Wisconsin is minimal, constrained entirely by the federal ban on new POHs and expansion.

*   **Existing POHs:** There is only one notable grandfathered POH in the state: **OakLeaf Surgical Hospital** in Altoona (near Eau Claire). Established in 2001 by independent physicians, it is a private hospital designed exclusively for surgery, featuring 52 surgeons across 15 specialties [6].
*   **Quality Metrics:** OakLeaf Surgical Hospital demonstrates exceptional quality, boasting a 5-star patient survey rating from CMS. It significantly outperforms national and state averages in patient experience, with 91% of patients stating they would "definitely recommend" the hospital (compared to the national average of 71%) [7]. It has also received awards from Women's Choice and high performance ratings from U.S. News & World Report for orthopedics [8].
*   **Market Impact:** The recent closures of two HSHS hospitals (Sacred Heart and St. Joseph's) in western Wisconsin have created a significant access crisis. OakLeaf Medical Network has expressed interest in filling these gaps, highlighting the potential for physician-led models to address rural healthcare shortages when traditional systems fail [9].

## 4. Regulatory Environment
Wisconsin's regulatory environment is unique in that it lacks the "double barrier" found in many states.

*   **CON Status:** Wisconsin **does not** have a traditional Certificate of Need program. It ranks 14th (tied with Ohio) on the Cicero Institute's CON Restrictiveness Index, scoring only 5 points [10]. The state does maintain an approval process similar to CON that applies mainly to nursing homes, and sets numerical caps on five service categories, but these do not broadly restrict hospital development [11].
*   **Federal Barrier:** Because there is no state CON law, the primary barrier to new POH development in Wisconsin is Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act. If the federal ban were repealed, Wisconsin would immediately become a highly attractive market for new POH construction due to its lack of state-level restrictions and highly favorable payer mix.

## 5. Political Context
Wisconsin is a politically divided "purple" state, but healthcare policy is heavily influenced by the unified hospital lobby.

*   **State Leadership:** Governor Tony Evers (D) has focused on healthcare affordability but has primarily targeted insurance companies rather than hospital consolidation. His recent addition to the WHA Board underscores the close relationship between the executive branch and the hospital industry [5].
*   **Legislative Dynamics:** Healthcare affordability and rural access are top priorities for the state legislature. However, there is no active state-level legislation promoting POHs.
*   **Federal Delegation:** Senators Tammy Baldwin (D) and Ron Johnson (R) have not co-sponsored current POH legislation (S. 1390). In the House, no Wisconsin representatives are currently co-sponsoring H.R. 4002, H.R. 2191, or H.R. 3022. Representative Mark Pocan (D-WI-2) has actively co-sponsored legislation aimed at strengthening the POH ban [12].
*   **Lobbying Power:** The Wisconsin Hospital Association (WHA) is a formidable political force, having spent nearly $10 million since 2000 on state lobbying and campaign contributions [13]. This entrenched influence presents a significant challenge to any pro-POH advocacy efforts.

## 6. Competitive Dynamics
The Wisconsin hospital market is characterized by extreme consolidation and the dominance of nonprofit mega-systems.

*   **Dominant Systems:** The market is led by massive nonprofit entities, including Aurora Health Care (part of Advocate Health, 19 hospitals, $4.34B NPR), Froedtert ThedaCare Health (18 hospitals, $4.39B NPR), and Ascension Wisconsin (18 hospitals, $2.21B NPR) [4].
*   **Consolidation Trend:** Since 2019, the state has seen significant M&A activity, including the creation of Emplify Health (Bellin + Gundersen) and Advocate Aurora's merger with Atrium Health [14]. This consolidation has raised concerns among employer groups about lack of transparency and rising costs.
*   **Payer Mix Advantage:** Wisconsin hospitals enjoy the highest commercial payer mix in the country at 78.1% [1]. This exceptionally favorable payer mix drives high revenues for existing systems and makes the state highly attractive for potential POH entrants, who typically thrive in markets with strong commercial insurance presence.
*   **340B and Tax Exemptions:** About half of Wisconsin's hospitals participate in the 340B program, generating significant profits (e.g., Gundersen Lutheran earns an estimated $77 million annually in 340B profits) [15]. The WHA aggressively defends the nonprofit tax exemption, claiming these hospitals deliver over 11 times the value in community benefits compared to their tax breaks [16].

## 7. Investment & Growth Opportunities
Despite the political challenges, Wisconsin presents compelling underlying fundamentals for healthcare investment.

*   **Demographic Tailwinds:** Wisconsin's population is aging rapidly, with the over-65 demographic growing by 36% between 2010 and 2020 [17]. Healthcare demand is projected to rise 10% by 2040, driven by the aging baby boomer generation, creating significant need for orthopedic, cardiac, and surgical services [18].
*   **Fast-Growing Markets:** Dane County (Madison), Trempealeau County, and Eau Claire County are among the fastest-growing areas, presenting opportunities for new facility development [19].
*   **Private Equity and ASCs:** With no CON laws restricting Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs), the state is seeing growth in outpatient investment. Venture capital firms like HealthX Ventures and Venture Investors Health Fund are active in the state, supported by public-private initiatives like the Wisconsin Investment Fund [20].
*   **The POH Opportunity:** If the federal ban is lifted, Wisconsin's lack of CON laws, combined with its nation-leading commercial payer mix and aging population, would make it a prime target for POH development. The success and high quality ratings of OakLeaf Surgical Hospital serve as a proof of concept for the physician-owned model in the state.
