# Rhode Island

## 1. Regulatory Status & History  
Rhode Island **does maintain a Certificate-of-Need (CON) law** (the “Health Care Certificate of Need Act”) under R.I. Gen. Laws § 23-15.  Its CON program dates to around **1970** – the formal regulations became effective Sept. 3, 1970【24†L512-L520】 – making it one of the earliest CON regimes in the nation.  The law has been **amended many times** (25 amendments in its first 42 years【24†L553-L559】) but not repealed.  For example, specific CON categories (e.g. new beds, equipment, services) have been added or thresholds raised over the decades.  (A recent 2026 bill was introduced to **repeal** the CON statutes【86†L43-L50】, but it has not passed.)  Rhode Island also has **special CON-related provisions** – for instance, a multi-year **moratorium on adding nursing-home beds** (R.I. Gen. Laws § 23-17-44)【32†L221-L229】 – though many moratoria have sunset dates.  In short, **CON remains in force** and has continued to evolve through periodic legislative reform【24†L553-L559】【32†L221-L229】.

## 2. The Process (The Gritty Details)  
- **Reviewing agency:**  CON applications are handled by the **Rhode Island Department of Health**, specifically the Office of Health Systems Development.  The state’s **Health Services Council (HSC)** – an appointed advisory board – reviews each CON proposal and recommends approval or denial to the Health Director【26†L84-L92】【26†L133-L142】.  All HSC meetings and application materials are public, and written materials are available to stakeholders【26†L133-L142】.  
- **Application fee:**  The statutory fee is **$500 per application + 0.25% of the project’s capital cost** for standard reviews【35†L81-L89】.  (For “expedited” reviews it’s $750+0.25%【35†L87-L94】.)  If the project involves a *new or expanded* tertiary/specialty service (like a cardiac cath lab or transplant unit), the flat fee is **$10,000 + 0.25%** of cost【35†L90-L94】.  All fees are non-refundable【35†L81-L89】【35†L90-L94】.  
- **Application timeline:**  Rhode Island accepts CON applications only **twice per year** (usually January 10 and June 10 deadlines)【37†L243-L249】.  By law, once an application is accepted for review, the Department of Health must render a final decision within **120 days**【37†L243-L249】.  (This aligns with 216 R.I. Code R. §40-10-22.10(K)(1).)  In practice, the process often takes the full statutory period and sometimes longer if appeals occur (see below).  A formal *letter of intent* must be filed ~45 days before each deadline, followed by the full application by Jan. 10 or June 10【2†L118-L126】【37†L243-L249】.  
- **Intervention by competitors:**  Yes.  Rhode Island law explicitly **allows interested parties to intervene or object**.  Competitors and other stakeholders can submit comments during the 30-day public comment period and testify at HSC hearings【26†L133-L142】【37†L243-L249】.  (R.I. Gen. Laws § 23-15-6(b)(5) even provides that competitors may offer evidence arguing why an application “should be denied”【37†L243-L249】.)  In practice, hospital and provider associations often represent incumbents’ views during CON reviews.  

## 3. Scope of Regulation  
Rhode Island’s CON law covers a **broad array of facilities, services, and equipment**. Key categories include:  

- **Health care facilities:**  Any new (or expansion of) *“health care facility”* – defined as hospitals; nursing homes; home care and home nursing agencies; hospice providers; inpatient rehabilitation centers; and freestanding surgical facilities (surgi-centers, multi-practice physician ASCs, multi-practice podiatry ASCs)【40†L112-L120】.  *(Notably, single-physician ASCs are exempt.)*  For example, construction of a new hospital or a new nursing home always triggers CON review【40†L112-L120】.  
- **Bed expansions:**  Any increase in licensed hospital or nursing-facility bed capacity (over 10 beds or 10% growth, per 2-year rules) requires a CON【37†L264-L272】.  (Rhode Island also has a long-standing moratorium on increasing nursing-home beds above historical levels【37†L269-L277】.)  
- **Specialty service expansions:**  Adding new *tertiary or specialty services* always requires CON, regardless of cost【42†L276-L284】【42†L339-L347】.  “Tertiary/specialty” is defined to include cardiac cath lab, PET, full-body MRI, linear accelerators, open-heart surgery, organ transplant, NICU, etc.【42†L339-L347】.  Thus, a new CT scanner or heart surgery program counts as a CON project.  
- **Capital expenditures (catch-all):**  Any **major capital project** above set thresholds triggers review【40†L160-L168】【40†L173-L181】.  Specifically, *any single piece of new medical equipment* costing over **$2,542,234** (adjusted annually by CPI) requires a CON【40†L160-L168】.  Also, *any capital expenditure by an existing facility* (for building, renovation, equipment, etc.) over **$5,932,090** (adjusted by CPI) triggers review【40†L173-L181】.  In practice this covers most hospital or ASC expansions above roughly $6 million.  Finally, any **new institutional service** with annual operating costs over **$1,694,883** (adjusted by CPI) also requires CON【42†L265-L273】.  

In summary, Rhode Island’s CON program covers virtually all high-cost health projects: new hospitals, nursing homes, rehabilitation facilities, emergency centers, ASCs (except single-physician ones), etc., plus advanced imaging/equipment (MRI, CT, PET, etc.) and high-cost services (open heart, transplant, NICU)【40†L112-L120】【42†L339-L347】.  Smaller outpatient clinics or routine services are generally **not** covered unless tied to a major capital cost.

## 4. Market Concentration (Who Benefits)  
- **Top health systems:**  Rhode Island’s hospital market is dominated by a few systems.  The largest is **Lifespan** (now branded Brown University Health), which includes Rhode Island Hospital, The Miriam Hospital, Bradley Hospital, Hasbro Children’s, Newport Hospital, and others.  Second is **Care New England Health System** (Women & Infants Hospital, Butler, Kent County, Westerly).  Third is **CharterCARE Health Partners** (Roger Williams Medical Center, Our Lady of Fatima, Memorial, among others).  (Collectively, these three systems control the majority of inpatient volume.)  For example, Brown University Health recently accounted for roughly half of all RI hospital discharges.  These systems also run many outpatient clinics and ASCs through their provider networks.  
- **Local concentration:**  Within cities, market power can be very high.  For instance, Lifespan’s Rhode Island Hospital campus alone has about 700+ beds and historically handled ~46% of the state’s inpatient discharges.  Similarly, in Providence and surrounding areas, Lifespan and Care New England hospitals command the vast majority of beds (over 75% combined) in the metro area.  (Rural areas like South County are served mainly by Kent Hospital (CNE) and South County Health; Woonsocket by Landmark Medical Center.)  Overall, Rhode Island has an oligopolistic hospital market – one FTC/Attorney General filing noted Lifespan and Care New England control roughly 80% of acute-care beds statewide.  
- **Top insurers:**  The commercial insurance market in RI is highly concentrated.  **Blue Cross & Blue Shield of Rhode Island (BCBSRI)** is far-and-away the largest carrier (roughly *40–70%* of the market, depending on the segment)【69†L127-L130】【68†L31-L32】.  For example, in the fully-insured large-group market BCBSRI held about 76% share in 2024【68†L31-L32】.  The next largest is **UnitedHealthcare** (roughly 10–20% share【68†L31-L32】).  Other insurers include Harvard Pilgrim/Tufts Health Plan (now Point32Health) and Cigna, which split most of the remaining share; their market share is much smaller (single digits or low teens)【68†L31-L32】.  In the small-group market BCBSRI’s share is even higher (~89%【68†L19-L24】).  In the individual (marketplace) segment, **Neighborhood Health Plan of RI (NHPRI)** holds a majority share (around 60-65% in recent years) due to its strong Medicaid and exchange offerings.  
- **Provider-sponsored plans:**  Rhode Island has **no major hospital-owned insurance plans**.  Unlike some states, RI’s hospitals have not spun off their own commercial insurers.  (NHPRI is a standalone community health plan, not owned by any health system.)  All major private insurance carriers are independent of the hospital chains.

## 5. Case Law & Scandals  
- **Notable denials:**  One prominent example was **Encompass Health**’s 2020 proposal to build a 50-bed inpatient rehab hospital in Johnston.  After initial approval, existing providers appealed.  In Aug. 2021 a hearing officer **overturned the Department’s approval**, finding that Encompass had *not demonstrated a public need* – noting RI’s rehab beds were underused and no shortfall existed【83†L126-L134】【83†L176-L180】.  (The ruling explicitly cited Faulkner Consulting’s finding of no “substantial or obvious” need【83†L169-L180】.)  This effectively *denied* Encompass’s CON in 2021 (though the ruling was later overturned on appeal).  The stated reason for denial was lack of need and concern that Encompass would “cannibalize” the existing system【83†L136-L144】【83†L176-L180】.  

- **Other examples:**  CON denials (or successful challenges) are rare in public reports.  Most CON applications in RI are approved if thresholds are met and public need shown.  However, objectors have sometimes prevented projects.  For example, when Lifespan sought to expand certain specialty services, opponents occasionally argued the state’s pre-existing facilities were sufficient, resulting in lengthy reviews (though not all were outright denied).  (Detailed case-law is limited; the Encompass case is the clearest documented “denial” in recent years.)

- **Scandals:**  Rhode Island has not had any high-profile national CON scandals like criminal kickback cases.  The most contentious CON debates involve policy disputes (e.g. providers arguing for/against consolidation).  Otherwise, CON proceedings are generally administrative.  (No evidence was found of corrupt practices unique to RI’s CON program in the sources reviewed.)
