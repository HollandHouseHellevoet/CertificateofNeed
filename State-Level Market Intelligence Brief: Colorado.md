## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Colorado's Hospital Market?

Colorado's hospital market is highly consolidated, with a few dominant systems controlling the vast majority of net patient revenue (NPR) and licensed beds. In 2024, total net patient revenue for Colorado hospitals reached $24.0 billion, a 98.8% increase since 2014 [1]. The market is characterized by the dominance of large, tax-exempt health systems.

### Dominant Hospital Systems

| Health System | Parent/Type | Hospitals in CO | Market Position & Revenue |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **UCHealth** | Nonprofit / Academic | 13 | Largest system by hospitals, beds, and revenue. $6.3B NPR. Dominates the Front Range and Northern Colorado. |
| **HCA HealthONE** | HCA Healthcare (For-profit) | 10 | Second largest system. $2.8B NPR. Major presence in the Denver metro area. |
| **CommonSpirit Health** | Catholic Health Initiatives (Nonprofit) | Multiple | Major player statewide, including St. Anthony Hospital. Significant market share in Denver and mountain communities. |
| **Children's Hospital Colorado** | Nonprofit | Multiple | $1.6B NPR. Dominant pediatric provider in the state and region. |
| **Denver Health** | Public / Safety-Net | 1 | Primary safety-net hospital. Absorbs 24.4% of the state's total uncompensated care costs. |

*Note: The top four systems in Colorado account for approximately 66% of statewide hospital net patient revenue [2].*

### Market Consolidation and Regulatory Environment
Colorado has experienced significant consolidation in recent years. Notable recent transactions include UCHealth's merger with Pueblo's Parkview Health System (finalized in December 2023) and its planned acquisition of Estes Park Health, a critical access hospital, expected to close in 2025 [3]. Banner Health also expanded its footprint by acquiring seven primary care clinics from Village Medical in May 2025 [4].

Crucially, **Colorado does not have Certificate of Need (CON) laws** [5]. This makes Colorado a "Competitive" state in the Rojas Report architecture. Without CON laws, the primary barrier to entry for new physician-owned hospitals is the federal ban on expansion and new Medicare certification under Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act (ACA). If the federal ban were lifted, Colorado would have no state-level regulatory barriers preventing rapid POH development.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Despite the federal ban, Colorado maintains a small but highly successful footprint of physician-owned and physician-led facilities.

### Operating Physician-Owned Hospitals

| Hospital Name | City | Specialty | Status |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Animas Surgical Hospital** | Durango | Surgical / Multi-specialty | Open, Grandfathered |
| **OrthoColorado Hospital** | Lakewood | Orthopedic & Spine | Open, Joint Venture |

**Animas Surgical Hospital (Durango, CO):**
Founded in 2003, Animas Surgical Hospital is a fully physician-owned facility located in La Plata County, a rural/resort area in southwestern Colorado. It serves as a critical alternative to the dominant regional system (Centura/CommonSpirit's Mercy Hospital). Animas is highly regarded for its quality metrics and patient satisfaction, frequently winning national awards. Its presence ensures competitive pricing and alternative surgical access in a geographically isolated market.

**OrthoColorado Hospital (Lakewood, CO):**
Established in 2010 on the St. Anthony Medical Campus, OrthoColorado is a 48-bed specialty hospital operating as a joint venture between local spine and orthopedic surgery practices (including Panorama Orthopedics) and CommonSpirit Health. It is ranked as the #1 hospital for joint replacement in Colorado. While technically a joint venture with a major system, its physician-led operational model demonstrates the high-quality outcomes achievable when physicians have ownership stakes in specialty care.

### The Broader ASC Market
Because of the ACA ban on new POHs, physician investment in Colorado has heavily shifted toward Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs). Colorado currently has 147 licensed ASCs, with approximately 2,350 physicians offering surgical services [6]. This robust ASC infrastructure indicates strong latent demand and available capital for physician-owned inpatient facilities if the federal ban were repealed.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Colorado

The ACA's Section 6001 effectively froze the Colorado POH market in 2010. While Colorado has not seen mass closures of its grandfathered POHs, the state has suffered from a lack of new market entrants during a period of explosive population growth (the state's population grew by 9.2% between 2014 and 2024) [1].

**Expansion Constraints:**
Grandfathered facilities like Animas Surgical Hospital are strictly limited in their ability to add operating rooms or licensed beds to meet the demands of Colorado's growing population. This artificial constraint forces patients into higher-cost, dominant health systems for inpatient surgical care.

**If Section 6001 Were Repealed:**
Given the absence of CON laws, a repeal of Section 6001 would likely trigger immediate development of new physician-owned specialty hospitals in Colorado. The most likely areas for expansion would be the rapidly growing Front Range corridors (Colorado Springs, Fort Collins, and the Denver suburbs) where dominant systems currently enjoy significant pricing power. Orthopedics, cardiology, and general surgery would be the primary specialties targeted for new POH development.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Colorado's political landscape presents a mixed environment for POH reform, with support primarily coming from the Republican delegation.

### Congressional Delegation Support
*   **U.S. Senate:** Neither Senator Michael Bennet (D) nor Senator John Hickenlooper (D) has co-sponsored S. 1390 (Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act) or other major POH repeal legislation.
*   **U.S. House of Representatives:** 
    *   **Rep. Jeff Hurd [R-CO-3]** is a co-sponsor of H.R. 4002 (Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act of 2025) [7].
    *   **Rep. Gabe Evans [R-CO-8]** is a co-sponsor of H.R. 2191 (Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act) [8].

### State-Level Regulatory Environment
While Colorado lacks CON laws, the state government under Governor Jared Polis (D) has been highly active in healthcare regulation, focusing on transparency and affordability:
*   **Hospital Financial Transparency:** HB19-1001 and HB23-1226 require extensive financial reporting from hospitals, including executive compensation and uncompensated care costs [1].
*   **Price Transparency:** SB23-252 mandates that hospitals include Medicare rates in their price postings, going beyond federal requirements [9].
*   **Merger Oversight:** SB26-041 strengthens the Attorney General's oversight of healthcare mergers and acquisitions [10].
*   **The Colorado Option:** The state's public option plan is designed to create competition and lower premiums, indicating a state-level desire to challenge the pricing power of dominant hospital systems.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

Colorado represents a Tier 1 opportunity for physician-owned hospital development in a post-Section 6001 environment.

### Market Vulnerabilities and Opportunities
Colorado hospitals are currently facing severe financial distress. According to the Colorado Hospital Association's Q3 2025 report, nearly 70% of Colorado hospitals ended 2024 with unsustainable margins, and over 80% of rural hospitals cannot achieve sustainable margins [11]. This systemic weakness among traditional hospitals creates a massive opportunity for lean, efficient, physician-owned specialty hospitals to capture market share.

### Payer Mix Dynamics
The payer mix in Colorado has shifted heavily toward government payers. As of 2024, Medicare (41.0%) and Medicaid (19.4%) account for over 60% of hospital coverage, up from 44% in 2010. Commercial insurance accounts for 29.7% [11]. While the high government payer mix challenges traditional hospitals with high overhead, POHs—which typically operate with lower administrative costs and higher efficiency—are better positioned to remain profitable even with Medicare rates, especially in high-margin specialties like orthopedics and cardiology.

### Highest-Opportunity Markets
1.  **Colorado Springs:** A rapidly growing market with an aging population and a strong existing base of physician-owned ASCs (e.g., Northgate Surgery Center, North Springs Surgical Associates).
2.  **Northern Colorado (Fort Collins/Greeley):** Areas dominated by UCHealth where alternative inpatient surgical options are limited.
3.  **Rural/Resort Communities:** Following the successful model of Animas Surgical Hospital, affluent resort communities (e.g., Summit County, Eagle County) offer strong commercial payer mixes and demand for high-quality, localized specialty care.

### Conclusion
Colorado is uniquely positioned for a POH renaissance if federal restrictions are lifted. The combination of zero CON laws, a highly consolidated and financially strained traditional hospital sector, and a robust existing network of physician-owned ASCs creates an ideal environment for rapid, disruptive investment in physician-owned inpatient facilities.
