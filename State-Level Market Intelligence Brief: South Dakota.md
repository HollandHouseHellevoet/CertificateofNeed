# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: South Dakota

**Target State:** South Dakota (SD)
**Focus:** Physician-Owned Hospitals & Market Intelligence
**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report

---

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls South Dakota's Hospital Market?

South Dakota's hospital market is highly concentrated and primarily controlled by three major nonprofit health systems: Sanford Health, Avera Health, and Monument Health. Sanford and Avera are the two largest systems, with a significant presence across the state, while Monument Health is the dominant provider in the western region, particularly in the Rapid City area. According to a 2026 analysis by Yale University's Health Care Affordability Lab, every hospital in South Dakota operates in a market that is either highly concentrated or a monopoly. Specifically, the analysis found that 30 hospitals in the state are in a monopolized market, and 23 are in a highly concentrated market.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sanford Health | Sanford Health | 20+ | Nonprofit | Sioux Falls, Aberdeen, Vermillion, Webster |
| Avera Health | Avera Health | 30+ | Nonprofit | Sioux Falls, Aberdeen, Yankton, Mitchell, Pierre |
| Monument Health | Monument Health | 5 | Nonprofit | Rapid City, Spearfish, Sturgis, Custer |

In the Sioux Falls metropolitan area, the hospital market is a near duopoly between Avera Health and Sanford Health. A 2024 Fitch Ratings report indicates that Avera Health holds a slight majority of the market share at approximately 52%, while Sanford Health accounts for the remaining 47%. Statewide, Avera Health leads with 42% of discharges, compared to Sanford Health's 30%. In the Rapid City metropolitan area, Monument Health is the undisputed market leader, operating as a near-monopoly. A 2026 Fitch Ratings report states that Monument Health maintains a dominant inpatient market share of approximately 96% in its Primary Service Area.

South Dakota's hospital market has seen continued consolidation in recent years, with Sanford Health being the most active acquirer. Notable transactions within the last five years include Sanford Health's acquisition of Prairie Lakes Healthcare System in Watertown in 2025, and the major merger between Sanford Health and Marshfield Clinic Health System, also in 2025. Additionally, Avera Health sold its telemedicine service, Avera eCare, to a private equity firm in 2021.

South Dakota is one of a minority of states that does not have a Certificate of Need (CON) law for hospitals or other healthcare facilities, having repealed its CON laws in 1988. This means that healthcare providers do not need to obtain a state-issued certificate to build new facilities or expand existing ones. However, Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) placed a moratorium on the development of new physician-owned hospitals and placed restrictions on the expansion of existing ones. In states without CON laws like South Dakota, the primary barrier to entry for new physician-owned hospitals is the ACA's general prohibition, not a state-level CON process.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Through extensive research, three currently operating physician-owned hospitals (POHs) have been identified in South Dakota, along with several historical POHs that have been sold or acquired.

**Dunes Surgical Hospital**
Located in Dakota Dunes, South Dakota, Dunes Surgical Hospital is a physician-owned facility that provides a range of surgical services. It is a multi-specialty surgical hospital with 14 operating rooms and 3 clinic treatment rooms. The hospital offers a variety of surgical specialties, including Dental/Oral Surgery, ENT, General, GI, Gynecology, Neurosurgery, Ophthalmology, Orthopedic, Pain, Plastic, Podiatry, Spine, and Urology. Its location near the border with Iowa and Nebraska allows it to serve patients from the tri-state area. The hospital has a 5-star rating from Medicare based on patient surveys. Its primary competitors are the larger hospital systems in the adjacent Sioux City, Iowa, metropolitan area, such as UnityPoint Health and MercyOne.

**Sioux Falls Specialty Hospital**
Sioux Falls Specialty Hospital is a physician-owned hospital located in Sioux Falls, South Dakota. It provides a range of specialty surgical and medical services, including Orthopedics, Pain Management, Gastroenterology/Urology, Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT), Urgent Care, Midwest Imaging, Family Medicine, and Physical Therapy. The hospital has a 5-star rating from Medicare based on patient surveys. Sioux Falls Specialty Hospital operates in a competitive market dominated by two large, integrated health systems: Sanford Health and Avera Health. Its competitive strategy appears to be focused on providing a high-quality, specialized alternative to the larger systems, offering a more physician-directed and patient-centered approach to care.

**Black Hills Surgical Hospital (Acquired)**
Black Hills Surgical Hospital, located in Rapid City, was a physician-owned hospital that specialized in orthopedics, spine, and other surgical services. It was a joint venture between a group of physicians and Medical Facilities Corporation (MFC). In November 2024, it was announced that Sanford Health would acquire Black Hills Surgical Hospital, along with the Black Hills Orthopedic and Spine Center, for a total purchase price of $194 million. This acquisition marked a significant expansion for Sanford Health into the western part of South Dakota and brought a previously physician-owned facility under the umbrella of a large, integrated health system.

**Avera Heart Hospital (Historical Partial Ownership)**
Avera Heart Hospital, located in Sioux Falls, is the region's first hospital dedicated solely to heart and vascular care. Originally, it was a joint venture between Avera Health, MedCath Corp., and a group of physicians. In 2010, MedCath announced its decision to sell its one-third interest in the hospital to Avera McKennan for approximately $20 million, resulting in Avera Health becoming the majority owner.

**Most Significant POH**
Sioux Falls Specialty Hospital is the most significant remaining physician-owned hospital in South Dakota. Its location in the state's largest city and its direct competition with the two dominant health systems, Sanford Health and Avera Health, make it a critical player in the overall healthcare landscape. It provides a competitive alternative to the large, integrated health systems, which can help to moderate prices and drive quality improvements across the market.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on South Dakota

Section 6001 of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA), signed into law on March 23, 2010, has had a significant impact on the landscape of physician-owned hospitals in South Dakota. Since the ACA was signed, South Dakota has seen the sale of at least three hospitals that were physician-owned or had significant physician ownership:

1.  **Dakota Plains Surgical Center (Aberdeen):** An orthopedic-focused hospital owned by Medical Facilities Corporation and physician investors, sold to Avera Health in 2015.
2.  **Lewis and Clark Specialty Hospital (Yankton):** A multi-specialty hospital founded by local physicians in 2002, sold to Avera Health in 2017 and renamed Avera Fox Run Medical Plaza.
3.  **Black Hills Surgical Hospital (Rapid City):** A physician-owned surgical hospital acquired by Sanford Health in late 2024.

Based on the identification of these three POHs operating in 2010 that were subsequently sold, the attrition rate for physician-owned hospitals in South Dakota since the passage of the ACA is estimated to be at least 50%. The closure or sale of these POHs has led to a decrease in competition in several key specialties, most notably orthopedics, pain management, gastroenterology/urology, and ENT.

While it is difficult to definitively determine without direct input from the hospitals, it is highly likely that the remaining POHs in South Dakota (Dunes Surgical Hospital and Sioux Falls Specialty Hospital) are constrained by the expansion ban imposed by Section 6001, unable to add beds, operating rooms, or procedure rooms to meet growing demand.

If Section 6001 were to be repealed, it is likely that South Dakota would see a resurgence of interest in the development of new physician-owned facilities. The metro areas most likely to see new POHs would be Sioux Falls and Rapid City, the state's two largest urban centers, which have a concentration of specialists and a patient population large enough to support new facilities. The specialties most likely to see new physician-owned facilities would be those that are most profitable and have been constrained by the ACA, such as orthopedics, cardiology, neurosurgery, and gastroenterology.

Senator John Thune is listed as a cosponsor of H.R. 3022, the "Restoring Rights for Patients and Physicians Act of 2025," which aims to repeal the ban on provider-owned hospitals. This indicates a supportive stance towards POHs. Neither Senator Mike Rounds nor Representative Dusty Johnson have sponsored or co-sponsored H.R. 4002, H.R. 2191, S. 1390, or H.R. 3022.

**State-Level Context**
An analysis of South Dakota's legal and regulatory landscape reveals no specific state-level legislation that directly addresses physician ownership of hospitals. The state's codified laws prioritize licensing, standards of care, and operational requirements, creating a neutral legal environment for physician-owned hospitals.

South Dakota repealed its Certificate of Need (CON) legislation in 1988. The Cicero Institute's CON Restrictiveness Index gives South Dakota a score of 0 out of a possible 50, ranking it as one of the least restrictive states in the nation. The lack of a CON program removes a significant regulatory hurdle, making it easier for new healthcare facilities, including those owned by physicians, to enter the market.

There is no publicly available information detailing the South Dakota State Medical Association's (SDSMA) official position on physician-owned hospitals or the repeal of Section 6001, as access to their policy finder is restricted to members.

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

Should Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act be repealed, several markets in South Dakota would present significant opportunities for the development of new physician-owned hospitals, largely concentrated in areas with existing health professional shortages.

**High-Opportunity Markets**
*   **Pennington County (Rapid City):** As a major population center and hub for the western part of the state, the existing demand, coupled with specific underserved populations, makes Rapid City a prime market for a new POH. Sanford Health's recent $300 million investment in a new medical center in Rapid City underscores the recognized need for expanded healthcare services in the region.
*   **Hughes County (Pierre):** As the state capital, Pierre is in a county designated as a geographic Health Professional Shortage Area (HPSA). A new POH in Pierre could address this gap and serve the surrounding rural counties.
*   **Brown County (Aberdeen) and Codington County (Watertown):** While not designated shortage areas themselves, these cities are surrounded by counties with geographic HPSAs. POHs in these locations could serve as referral centers for underserved rural areas.

**Specialty Gaps**
The most significant specialty gap in South Dakota is in primary care. According to a 2013 report by the Robert Graham Center, the state will need an additional 162 primary care physicians by 2030 to maintain current utilization rates, a 27% increase from 2010. Other likely specialty gaps include general surgery, orthopedics, cardiology, oncology, and behavioral health.

**Payer Mix and Demographics**
The payer mix in South Dakota is approximately 51% employer-sponsored, 16% Medicare, and 15% Medicaid, according to 2024 data from Becker's Payer Issues. The high percentage of commercially insured patients is a positive indicator for a new POH, as commercial insurance typically reimburses at higher rates than government payers.

South Dakota's population is aging. In 2024, 18% of South Dakotans were 65 and older, up from 14% in 2010. This demographic shift will drive increased demand for healthcare services, particularly for chronic disease management, specialty care, and surgical procedures. The combination of an aging population, a growing demand for healthcare services, and a persistent healthcare workforce shortage creates a favorable environment for the development of new physician-owned hospitals in South Dakota if federal restrictions were lifted.
