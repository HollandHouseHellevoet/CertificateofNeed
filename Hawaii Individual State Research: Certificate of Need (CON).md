# Hawaii Individual State Research: Certificate of Need (CON)

**Objective:** Granular, data-driven investigation into the healthcare market and regulatory environment of Hawaii.

## 1. Regulatory Status & History

*   **Does this state have a Certificate of Need (CON) law?** Yes, Hawaii has a Certificate of Need (CON) law [1].
*   **What year was the law established?** The Hawaii CON law was established in 1974 [2].
*   **Has it been reformed or repealed? If so, when?** The law has not been repealed. It has undergone various amendments over the years, but Hawaii maintains one of the strictest CON programs in the nation, covering 87 of 109 identified medical services and facilities [3].

## 2. The Process (The Gritty Details)

*   **What is the exact name of the state agency or board that reviews applications?** The State Health Planning and Development Agency (SHPDA) [4].
*   **What is the application fee?** The application fee is 0.1% of the total capital cost of the project, with a minimum fee of $250 and a maximum fee of $25,000 [5].
*   **How long does the review process legally take, and how long does it take in practice?** Legally, the standard review process takes 90 days from the date the application is deemed complete. In practice, the process can take up to 90 days or longer if there are requests for reconsideration or public hearings [6].
*   **Are incumbent competitors legally allowed to intervene or object to a new application?** Yes, incumbent competitors and other "affected persons" are legally allowed to intervene, submit written and oral testimony, and request public hearings for reconsideration of SHPDA decisions [7].

## 3. Scope of Regulation

*   **List the specific facilities covered:** Hawaii's CON law covers a broad range of facilities, including Hospitals (acute care, psychiatric, specialty), Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs), Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNFs), Intermediate Care Facilities (ICFs), Home Health Agencies, Hospice Agencies, Diagnostic Imaging Centers (MRI, CT, PET), Radiation Therapy facilities, Cardiac Catheterization labs, and Dialysis Centers [8].
*   **What is the capital expenditure threshold?** The capital expenditure threshold that triggers a CON review in Hawaii is $4,000,000 for capital expenditures, $1,000,000 for new medical equipment, and $400,000 for new clinical services [9].

## 4. Market Concentration (Who Benefits)

*   **Who are the top 3 largest health systems in the state by revenue or market share?**
    1.  **The Queen's Health Systems:** Largest system, with Queen's Medical Center generating $1.42 billion in net patient revenue (2024) [10]. Total system revenue is approximately $2.4 billion [11].
    2.  **Hawaii Pacific Health (HPH):** Second largest system, with Straub Medical Center ($537 million NPR) and Kapiolani Medical Center ($437 million NPR) [10].
    3.  **Hawaii Health Systems Corporation (HHSC):** The state's public hospital system, operating facilities primarily on the neighbor islands.
*   **Identify any highly concentrated local markets within the state:** The Urban Honolulu market is highly concentrated. The Queen's Health Systems holds a 43% market share, and Hawaii Pacific Health holds a 19% market share in the Urban Honolulu MSA. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) for the Urban Honolulu hospital market is 2,625, indicating a highly concentrated market [12].
*   **Who are the top 3 largest health insurance carriers in the state by market share?**
    1.  **Hawaii Medical Service Association (HMSA - Blue Cross Blue Shield):** Dominates the market with a 65% share in the large group market and over 400,000 enrollees [13].
    2.  **Kaiser Foundation Health Plan:** Holds a 31% share in the large group market with over 200,000 enrollees [13].
    3.  **UnitedHealthcare:** Holds a much smaller share, primarily in specific segments like Medicaid or Medicare Advantage.
*   **Are there any major provider-sponsored health plans?** Yes, Kaiser Permanente operates as a fully integrated provider-sponsored health plan in Hawaii. Additionally, HMSA and Hawaii Pacific Health (HPH) announced a plan in January 2026 to affiliate under a new parent nonprofit organization called "One Health Hawaii," which would effectively create a massive, dominant payer-provider entity in the state [14].

## 5. Case Law & Scandals

*   **Find at least one specific, documented instance where a provider was denied permission to build or expand in this state:**
    *   **Provider:** Malulani Health and Medical Center, LLC
    *   **Year:** 2006
    *   **Reason for Denial:** Malulani Health and Medical Center applied for a CON to build a new 150-bed hospital on Maui. SHPDA denied the application (Decision No. 05-25). The denial was heavily influenced by testimony from the incumbent provider, Maui Memorial Medical Center (MMMC). SHPDA concluded that Malulani failed to prove its proposal would not negatively impact the overall costs of health services to the community (HAR 11-186-15(a)(3)) and that it would duplicate existing services, potentially causing MMMC to lose an estimated $54.9 million in annual revenue, thereby threatening MMMC's ability to serve as the community's "safety net" hospital [15].
