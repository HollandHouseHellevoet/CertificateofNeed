### 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls California's Hospital Market?

California's hospital market is highly consolidated and dominated by massive, integrated non-profit health systems. According to a 2021 report by the California Health Care Foundation and the Petris Center at UC Berkeley, hospital markets in most California counties are approaching monopoly levels of concentration, particularly in rural areas [1]. Most markets across the state exceed the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) threshold for being "highly concentrated" (HHI > 2,500) [1].

Kaiser Permanente is the undisputed giant of the California market, operating 40 hospitals and holding a massive share of the state's health insurance market [3]. In Northern California, Sutter Health exercises immense market power. In 2021, Sutter Health finalized a landmark $575 million antitrust settlement with the California Attorney General over allegations that it used its dominant market position to illegally drive up prices [4]. In the greater Sacramento area, Adventist Health holds a commanding 61% market share, effectively controlling the region [5].

Recent consolidation events further demonstrate the concentration of power. In July 2024, UCSF Health announced an agreement to acquire two Dignity Health hospitals in San Francisco (Saint Francis Memorial Hospital and St. Mary’s Medical Center) [6]. In June 2023, Adventist Health acquired the financially struggling Bakersfield Heart Hospital for a nominal sum [7]. To monitor this aggressive consolidation, California established the Office of Health Care Affordability (OHCA), which now requires material change transaction notices for major hospital mergers and acquisitions [8].

California is one of the few states that does not have Certificate of Need (CON) laws, scoring a 0 on the Cicero Institute's CON Restrictiveness Index [9]. However, this lack of state-level barriers does not translate to a free market for physician-owned hospitals. Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act acts as a federal blockade, preventing new POHs from billing Medicare and effectively neutralizing California's permissive state regulatory environment [10].

### 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

The physician-owned hospital landscape in California is sparse and complex. A 2008 Senate Finance Committee report identified five traditional POHs in the state; today, only one of those original five retains any physician ownership [11]. The California Department of Health Care Access and Information (HCAI) currently lists 10 hospitals under the "Investor - Partnership" control type, which serves as the closest proxy for physician ownership [12].

However, this list requires nuance. Eight of these ten hospitals are owned by AHMC Healthcare, a for-profit corporation founded and chaired by Dr. Jonathan Wu [13]. While technically physician-owned at the corporate level, AHMC operates as a large, integrated health system in the Greater San Gabriel Valley, acquiring distressed facilities like Seton Medical Center out of bankruptcy in 2020 [14]. They do not function as the traditional, independent, specialty-focused POHs targeted by Section 6001.

The most significant traditional POH remaining in California is **Fresno Surgical Hospital**. It operates under a joint venture model, with 49% ownership retained by local Fresno physicians and 51% acquired by Saint Agnes Medical Center (Trinity Health) in 2011 [15]. Fresno Surgical Hospital demonstrates the high-quality care typical of POHs; it earned an "A" grade from The Leapfrog Group in Fall 2025 (one of only three hospitals in Fresno to do so) and is rated "High Performing" in back surgery by US News & World Report [16] [17]. It previously held a 5-star rating from CMS [18].

The closure and acquisition of POHs in California have directly reduced patient access to specialized care. Bakersfield Heart Hospital, once a premier physician-owned cardiac facility, struggled financially and was acquired by Adventist Health in 2023, ending its independent physician ownership [7]. Stanislaus Surgical Hospital in Modesto, which became majority-owned by Sutter Health in 2016, had its Medicare provider agreement terminated by CMS in September 2024 due to health and safety violations, leading to an indefinite suspension of operations [19]. Miracle Mile Medical Center in Los Angeles is permanently closed [20].

### 3. Section 6001 Impact on California

Section 6001 of the ACA has effectively frozen the development of independent physician-owned hospitals in California. While exact historical counts are difficult to verify, a 2020 study identified 10 POHs operating in California in 2010 [21]. Since then, at least three significant facilities—Kingsburg Medical Center, Corcoran District Hospital, and Adventist Health Feather River (destroyed by the Camp Fire)—have closed or been sold, representing an estimated attrition rate of 30% for grandfathered POHs [22] [23] [24].

The remaining genuine POHs, such as Fresno Surgical Hospital, are strictly constrained by the Section 6001 expansion ban. They cannot add operating rooms, procedure rooms, or licensed beds beyond their March 23, 2010 capacity without a nearly impossible-to-obtain exception from the HHS Secretary [10]. No California POH has successfully navigated this exception process.

If Section 6001 were repealed, California's lack of CON laws would make it a prime target for rapid POH development. The competitive landscape would shift dramatically, particularly in major metropolitan areas like Los Angeles, Orange County, San Diego, and the San Francisco Bay Area. New physician-owned facilities would likely emerge focusing on high-margin, high-demand specialties such as orthopedics, cardiology, spine surgery, and gastroenterology. This would introduce much-needed competition against the dominant non-profit mega-systems, potentially disrupting their pricing power and forcing improvements in quality and efficiency.

### 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Despite the heavy consolidation of its hospital market, California's federal delegation shows limited support for repealing the ban on physician-owned hospitals.

*   **U.S. Senators:** Neither Senator Alex Padilla (D-CA) nor Senator Adam Schiff (D-CA) has sponsored or co-sponsored the current POH reform bill, H.R. 4002 (Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act of 2025) [25]. While in the House, Adam Schiff did co-sponsor the predecessor bill (H.R. 977) in the 118th Congress, but he has not carried that support into the Senate [26].
*   **U.S. House Members:** Currently, zero members of the California House delegation are co-sponsoring H.R. 4002 [25]. In the previous Congress, Representatives David Valadao (R-CA-22) and Darrell Issa (R-CA-48) co-sponsored H.R. 977 [26].

At the state level, California maintains a highly restrictive regulatory environment regarding physician self-referral. The Physician Ownership and Referral Act (PORA) of 1993 is generally stricter than the federal Stark Law, applying to all payers (including private insurance and cash-pay) and encompassing a broader definition of "physician" [27]. Recent state legislation, such as SB 351 (effective 2026), has further strengthened enforcement against the corporate practice of medicine, indicating a legislative trend toward greater scrutiny of physician financial relationships rather than deregulation [28].

### 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, California would present a massive, albeit complex, opportunity for POH investment. The state's lack of CON laws removes the primary state-level barrier to entry, making it highly attractive compared to states like New York or Kentucky.

The most underserved markets with the highest opportunity for new POH development are in the San Joaquin Valley. This region suffers from higher all-cause mortality, a greater prevalence of chronic conditions, and severe provider shortages, with many areas designated as Medically Underserved Areas (MUAs) and Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs) [29].

Significant specialty gaps exist across the state. There is a projected shortage of 5,000 mental health practitioners by 2026, and 2.7 million Californians live in maternity care deserts [30]. While POHs traditionally focus on orthopedics and cardiology, innovative models could address these critical shortages.

The payer mix in California presents a unique challenge and opportunity. While private and third-party payers account for the largest share of net patient revenue (41.2%), Medicare (38.6% of inpatient discharges) and Medi-Cal (up to 42% of discharges in teaching facilities) cover the largest volume of patients [31]. Any new POH development would need a strategic approach to balance the high volume of public payers with the necessary commercial mix to maintain profitability.

While specific investor networks have not publicly announced plans for California POHs, free-market advocacy groups like the Pacific Research Institute strongly support repealing the ACA moratorium, indicating an underlying appetite for market-driven healthcare solutions in the state [32].
