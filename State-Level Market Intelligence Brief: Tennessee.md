# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Tennessee

**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report: Physician-Owned Hospitals
**Subject:** Tennessee Market Structure, POH Landscape, and Section 6001 Impact
**Date:** March 14, 2026

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Tennessee's Hospital Market?

Tennessee’s hospital market is highly consolidated and dominated by a mix of massive for-profit national chains headquartered in the state and powerful regional nonprofit systems. The state is home to 178 active hospitals serving a population of over 7 million [1]. The market is uniquely shaped by the presence of Nashville, which serves as the corporate headquarters for several of the nation's largest for-profit hospital operators.

The dominant hospital systems operating in Tennessee include:

*Note: Hospital counts and revenue figures are based on 2024-2025 data [1] [2].*

In the Nashville metropolitan area, the market is an oligopoly controlled primarily by HCA Healthcare (operating as TriStar Health), Vanderbilt University Medical Center (VUMC), and Ascension Saint Thomas. VUMC alone generates $5.4 billion in net patient revenue, making it the highest-earning individual hospital system in the state [1]. 

In Northeast Tennessee, Ballad Health operates as a functional monopoly. Formed by the 2018 merger of Wellmont Health System and Mountain States Health Alliance, Ballad Health controls nearly 100% of the inpatient market in the region under a state-approved Certificate of Public Advantage (COPA), which shields the merger from federal antitrust enforcement [3]. 

Recent consolidation events have further concentrated market power. In early 2026, Community Health Systems (CHS) completed the sale of Tennova Healthcare-Clarksville to VUMC for $600 million, expanding Vanderbilt's regional dominance [4]. Previously, in 2021, VUMC acquired Tennova hospitals in Shelbyville and Tullahoma from CHS [5].

Tennessee operates under a highly restrictive Certificate of Need (CON) regime. The Cicero Institute ranks Tennessee as the 36th most restrictive state for CON laws, scoring it at 85 out of a possible 115 points [6]. The state regulates medical inpatient facilities, outpatient surgical centers, imaging equipment, and long-term care facilities. This regulatory framework interacts with Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act to create an insurmountable double barrier to entry for physician-owned hospitals. Even if a physician group could secure the capital and navigate the federal ban on Medicare billing for new POHs, they would still face a state CON board heavily influenced by incumbent hospital systems motivated to block new competition.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

The physician-owned hospital landscape in Tennessee has been severely degraded since the passage of the ACA. Prior to 2010, Tennessee had a modest but active POH sector, primarily consisting of small rural facilities and specialized surgical centers. Today, the sector is characterized by closures, acquisitions, and a few grandfathered specialty facilities.

**Operating Physician-Owned Hospitals:**

*   **Saint Thomas Hospital for Specialty Surgery (Nashville):** This facility operates as a joint venture between Ascension Saint Thomas and physician owners. It focuses exclusively on elective orthopedic and spine surgeries. By partnering with a dominant nonprofit system, the physician owners secured market access and brand recognition, while Ascension captured a highly profitable service line. It remains one of the few active POH models in the state, though its independence is compromised by the joint venture structure [7].
*   **The Center for Spinal Surgery (Nashville):** Listed in 2010 Physician Hospitals of America (PHA) data as receiving its Medicare number just before the December 31, 2010 cutoff, this facility was grandfathered under Section 6001 [8]. It serves the highly lucrative spine surgery market in the Nashville area, competing directly with VUMC and HCA.

**Closed and Acquired POHs:**

The attrition rate among Tennessee's rural POHs has been catastrophic. 
*   **Cumberland River Hospital (Celina):** A rural POH that struggled with financial viability, it closed permanently in March 2019, leaving Clay County without an emergency room. A brief attempt to reopen it in 2020 failed, and it is now being converted into a basic medical clinic [9].
*   **Woods Memorial Hospital (Etowah):** Sold to a physician consortium in 2007 for $4 million, the facility was subsequently acquired by LifePoint Hospitals (a massive for-profit chain) in 2012, stripping it of its physician-owned status [10].
*   **Medical Center of Manchester:** Originally owned by three physicians and a former state legislator, this facility merged with United Regional Medical Center to form Unity Medical Center, consolidating the local market and diluting physician control [11].
*   **Perry Community Hospital (Linden):** Closed in 2020 due to financial distress, it was recently reopened in late 2025 by Braden Health, a corporate operator, removing it from the POH category [12].

The most significant POH dynamic in Tennessee is the total eradication of independent, physician-owned rural hospitals. The facilities that survived did so by aligning with massive corporate systems (like Saint Thomas Hospital for Specialty Surgery) or by focusing exclusively on high-margin specialties in affluent urban centers.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Tennessee

Since the ACA was signed on March 23, 2010, Tennessee has lost nearly its entire independent rural POH infrastructure. Based on 2010 PHA data, at least eight existing POHs and two pending facilities were operating or planned in the state [8]. Today, the attrition rate for independent rural POHs in Tennessee approaches 100%. 

The specialties that lost the most physician-owned competition were general acute care and emergency medicine in rural areas. The closure of facilities like Cumberland River Hospital directly reduced patient access to basic emergency services. In urban markets, the expansion ban has artificially constrained the growth of grandfathered facilities like The Center for Spinal Surgery, preventing them from adding operating rooms to meet the demand of Nashville's booming population.

If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in Tennessee would shift dramatically. The Nashville metropolitan area, despite being the corporate capital of the for-profit hospital industry, would be the primary target for new POH development. The concentration of highly specialized, well-capitalized physician groups in Nashville, combined with the high demand for orthopedic, cardiac, and spine procedures, creates an ideal environment for specialty POHs. Additionally, the Memphis market, currently dominated by Baptist Memorial Health Care, would likely see new physician-led surgical hospitals aimed at capturing commercial insurance revenue.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Tennessee is classified as a "Contested" state within The Rojas Report architecture. It features a highly restrictive regulatory environment but is currently experiencing active legislative debates regarding healthcare reform.

**Federal Representation:**
*   **Senator Marsha Blackburn (R-TN):** Senator Blackburn has not co-sponsored S. 1390 (the Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act) [13]. However, she has introduced other healthcare legislation, such as the Delivering Support for Hospitals in Tennessee Act, indicating a focus on hospital funding rather than POH expansion.
*   **Senator Bill Hagerty (R-TN):** Senator Hagerty has not co-sponsored S. 1390 [13].
*   **U.S. House Delegation:** No representatives from Tennessee have co-sponsored H.R. 4002, H.R. 2191, or H.R. 3022 [14] [15] [16]. The lack of federal support from the Tennessee delegation is notable, given the state's conservative leanings, and likely reflects the immense lobbying power of HCA Healthcare and CHS, both headquartered in the state.

**State-Level Context:**
Tennessee operates a highly restrictive CON program, regulating medical inpatient facilities, outpatient surgical centers, and imaging equipment [6]. However, the state is undergoing a period of regulatory reform. In 2024, the legislature passed PC557, which phases out CON requirements for ambulatory surgical treatment centers and linear accelerators by December 2027 [17]. Furthermore, a 2026 bill (HB 819) proposes removing acute care hospitals from the CON process entirely by 2030 [18].

Crucially, the Tennessee Medical Association signed a coalition letter in October 2025 supporting H.R. 2191 and S. 1390, signaling strong state-level physician support for the repeal of Section 6001 [19].

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, Tennessee would present a bifurcated investment landscape characterized by high-margin urban specialty opportunities and subsidized rural stabilization projects.

**Highest-Opportunity Markets:**
1.  **Nashville/Franklin:** As the wealthiest and fastest-growing region in the state, the Nashville MSA is the prime target for orthopedic, spine, and cardiovascular POHs. The market is currently controlled by HCA and VUMC, but local physician groups possess the capital and patient volume to support independent facilities.
2.  **Knoxville:** Dominated by Covenant Health and the UT Medical Center, Knoxville has a growing aging population that requires high volumes of musculoskeletal care, presenting a clear gap for physician-owned orthopedic hospitals.
3.  **Northeast Tennessee (Tri-Cities):** The Ballad Health monopoly has created significant friction with local physicians and patients regarding pricing and quality. A physician-owned facility in this region would immediately capture market share from patients seeking alternatives to the COPA-protected system.

**Payer Mix Economics:**
The viability of new POH development in Tennessee is heavily influenced by its payer mix. According to Definitive Healthcare, the average Tennessee hospital payer mix is approximately 10.5% Medicaid, 14.2% Medicare, and 75.3% Private/Self-Pay/Other [20]. However, this varies wildly by region. Urban specialty hospitals can achieve commercial payer mixes exceeding 60%, making them highly lucrative. Conversely, rural hospitals rely heavily on Medicare and TennCare (the state's Medicaid program, which covers 22% of the population) [21]. 

The high commercial insurance density in Middle Tennessee makes it the most viable region for immediate, unsubsidized POH development if the federal ban is lifted.
