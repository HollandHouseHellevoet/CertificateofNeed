# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Montana

**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report: Physician-Owned Hospitals
**Subject:** Montana Market Structure, POH Landscape, and Section 6001 Impact

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Montana's Hospital Market?

Montana's hospital market is highly concentrated and dominated by a small number of large, nonprofit health systems. According to March 2026 data from the Yale Tobin Center, 98.4% of Montana's 62 hospitals operate in highly concentrated or monopoly markets, ranking the state fourth nationally for hospital market concentration [1]. The landscape is defined by regional monopolies where single systems control the vast majority of care in the state's largest population centers.

The most significant recent market consolidation event occurred in September 2023, when Billings Clinic and Logan Health (formerly Kalispell Regional Healthcare) officially merged to form the state's largest integrated delivery network [2]. This merger united the dominant provider in eastern and south-central Montana with the dominant provider in the Flathead Valley, creating a system that spans the state.

In the Billings metropolitan area, Billings Clinic and Intermountain Health's St. Vincent Regional Hospital control nearly the entire acute care market. In Great Falls, Benefis Health System operates as a near-monopoly for general acute care. Bozeman Health holds a dominant position in the rapidly growing Gallatin Valley, though it recently announced an expanded collaboration with Billings Clinic in October 2025 [4].

Montana is a notable exception in the regulatory landscape because it repealed nearly all of its Certificate of Need (CON) laws in October 2021 through the passage of HB 231 [5]. Currently, only long-term care facilities (nursing homes) require a CON in Montana. Because Montana lacks CON barriers for acute care and ambulatory surgery centers, the primary barrier to new physician-owned hospital entry is exclusively federal: Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act.

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Montana's physician-owned hospital landscape was largely dismantled prior to the enactment of the Affordable Care Act, leaving the state with no known independent, grandfathered POHs operating today. The history of physician ownership in the state is characterized by early innovation followed by absorption into dominant nonprofit systems or national for-profit operators.

The most significant historical POH in Montana is the facility formerly known as Central Montana Surgical Hospital in Great Falls. Originally established as a physician-owned specialty surgical hospital, it was acquired in 2006 by a partnership between the Great Falls Clinic and Essentia Health [6]. After several transitions, including a partnership with Symbion, Inc., the facility was acquired by Surgery Partners in 2019 [7]. Today, it operates as Great Falls Hospital, a "physician-partnered" facility under the Surgery Partners corporate umbrella. Despite the loss of independent physician ownership, the facility maintains exceptional quality metrics, earning a 5-Star Rating from the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) in 2023 and recognition as one of Healthgrades America's 100 Best Hospitals for Joint Replacement [8]. This facility matters because it demonstrates that the physician-driven model of focused, high-quality surgical care remains viable and superior in quality, even when operating within a corporate structure in a market dominated by Benefis Health System.

Another notable historical POH was HealthCenter Northwest in Kalispell. Originally backed by physician investors (Flathead Physicians Group LLC), the facility became entangled in a complex relationship with Kalispell Regional Healthcare. In 2018, Kalispell Regional Healthcare entities paid $24 million to settle False Claims Act allegations related to Stark Law and Anti-Kickback Statute violations involving physician compensation and referral inducements to the HealthCenter [9]. Following this, the facility was fully absorbed into what is now Logan Health, resulting in the complete loss of independent physician ownership in the Flathead Valley market.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Montana

Because Montana's primary physician-owned hospitals were sold or integrated into larger systems prior to 2010, the state did not experience a wave of post-ACA closures among grandfathered facilities. The attrition rate of grandfathered POHs in Montana is effectively 100%, but this attrition predates Section 6001.

The true impact of Section 6001 on Montana is not measured in closures, but in the complete suppression of new market entrants. Since the ACA was signed on March 23, 2010, the federal ban has prevented any new physician-owned hospitals from opening to challenge the state's highly concentrated nonprofit monopolies. Patients in Billings, Missoula, and Bozeman have been denied access to the focused, high-quality specialty care that physician-owned orthopedic and cardiovascular hospitals provide in other states.

If Section 6001 were repealed, Montana's competitive landscape would transform rapidly. Because the state has already repealed its CON laws for acute care facilities, there is no state-level regulatory barrier to entry. Montana would immediately become a "Reform" state and a prime target for POH development. The most likely areas for new physician-owned facilities would be the rapidly growing Bozeman market, which is currently dominated by a single system, and the Billings market, where specialty surgical hospitals could effectively compete against the Billings Clinic-Logan Health conglomerate.

Neither Senator Steve Daines nor Senator Tim Sheehy has co-sponsored S. 1390 (the Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act). Similarly, neither Representative Ryan Zinke nor Representative Troy Downing has co-sponsored H.R. 4002 or H.R. 2191.

At the state level, Montana is a highly favorable regulatory environment. The 2021 repeal of CON laws (HB 231) removed state-level barriers for hospitals, ambulatory surgery centers, and home health agencies [5]. The Cicero Institute would classify Montana as having one of the least restrictive CON regimes in the nation, as the state only regulates long-term care facilities. Furthermore, state lawmakers have recently expressed frustration with the lobbying power of nonprofit hospitals and have attempted to increase oversight regarding the community benefits these tax-exempt entities provide [10].

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

If Section 6001 were repealed, Montana would represent a unique and highly attractive investment opportunity. The combination of a repealed CON law, a rapidly growing population in key areas, and highly concentrated incumbent hospital markets creates an ideal environment for physician-owned market entry.

The highest-opportunity markets are Bozeman (Gallatin County) and Missoula. Bozeman is one of the fastest-growing micropolitan areas in the United States, yet its acute care market is monopolized by Bozeman Health. A physician-owned orthopedic or general surgical hospital in this market would capture significant commercial volume. Missoula, dominated by Providence, presents a similar opportunity for specialty surgical competition.

The widest gap between demand and physician-owned supply exists in orthopedics, spine, and cardiovascular care. Montana's aging population drives high demand for these services, which are currently captured entirely by the large health systems.

The primary challenge for new POH development in Montana is the payer mix. Montana has a higher-than-average Medicare enrollment rate, with approximately 23% of the population covered by Medicare [11]. Furthermore, Montana expanded Medicaid under the ACA, covering roughly 20.5% of the population [12]. For a system like Benefis in Great Falls, the payer mix is heavily skewed toward government payers (57% Medicare, 16% Medicaid, and roughly 27% commercial) [13]. Any new physician-owned hospital would need to carefully model its financial viability based on capturing a sufficient share of the commercial market, particularly in wealthier enclaves like Bozeman or Kalispell, to offset the high volume of Medicare patients.
