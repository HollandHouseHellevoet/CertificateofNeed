# Ohio State Intelligence Dossier: Physician-Owned Hospitals & Market Dynamics

**State Classification:** Competitive
**Composite Risk Score:** Moderate
**Market Structure:** No CON laws for hospitals, high market concentration, active DOJ antitrust intervention.

## Executive Summary

Ohio represents a uniquely compelling market for physician-owned hospital (POH) expansion and investment. Unlike many states with entrenched hospital monopolies, Ohio lacks Certificate of Need (CON) laws for acute care facilities, removing the primary state-level barrier to entry. While the market is heavily concentrated among massive non-profit systems—most notably the Cleveland Clinic and OhioHealth—recent federal antitrust actions against OhioHealth signal a regulatory willingness to challenge anti-competitive practices. The state currently supports two successful, grandfathered POHs, demonstrating the viability of the model. Furthermore, the recent $485 million acquisition of Summa Health by venture capital firm General Catalyst indicates a shifting landscape where innovative ownership structures are gaining traction and regulatory approval.

## Market Landscape & Dominant Systems

Ohio is the 7th largest state by population (~11.8 million) and features a massive healthcare economy with over 140 non-federal, short-term acute care hospitals generating $221.8 billion in gross patient revenue [1]. The market is heavily dominated by large, tax-exempt integrated delivery networks (IDNs).

**Top 5 Ohio Health Systems by Net Patient Revenue (NPR):**
1. **Cleveland Clinic Health System:** $11.76B NPR, 26 hospitals, 5,522 beds.
2. **Bon Secours Mercy Health:** $8.98B NPR, 41 hospitals, 5,966 beds.
3. **University Hospitals Health System:** $6.64B NPR, 19 hospitals, 2,995 beds.
4. **OhioHealth:** $4.83B NPR, 19 hospitals, 2,460 beds.
5. **Mercy Health:** $4.76B NPR, 24 hospitals, 3,327 beds.

These systems leverage their scale to dictate terms to payers and acquire independent practices, creating a challenging environment for independent physicians.

## Physician-Owned Hospitals in Ohio

Despite the federal ban on new POHs (Section 6001 of the ACA), Ohio is home to two highly successful, grandfathered physician-owned hospitals. Their continued operation and high quality scores provide a strong proof-of-concept for the POH model in the state.

### 1. Ohio Valley Surgical Hospital (OVSH) - Springfield, OH
- **Status:** Operating (Grandfathered)
- **Overview:** Established in 2009, OVSH is a multi-specialty surgical hospital owned by 35 physicians in a joint venture with Premier Health and OhioHealth (since 2022).
- **Quality:** Consistently earns CMS 5-Star ratings for patient experience and is a Press Ganey award winner.
- **Significance:** Demonstrates that POHs can successfully partner with major IDNs while maintaining high quality and patient satisfaction.

### 2. Western Reserve Hospital - Cuyahoga Falls, OH
- **Status:** Operating (Grandfathered)
- **Overview:** Became physician-owned in 2009. Currently majority-owned by Western Reserve Hospital Partners (~200 physicians) with University Hospitals holding a minority stake.
- **Quality:** Earned a CMS 5-Star Quality Hospital rating (2021) and the Healthgrades 2025 Outstanding Patient Experience Award. It operates a 24/7 Emergency Department and is a Level III Trauma Center.
- **Significance:** Proves that POHs can operate as full-service, acute care facilities, not just specialized surgical centers.

### Historical Context: New Albany Surgical Hospital
Opened in 2003 as a joint venture between 30 physicians and Surgical Alliance Corp., this 42-bed facility faced intense pressure from local health systems, which threatened to revoke the staff privileges of physician-owners. It was eventually sold to Mount Carmel Health System in 2007. This pre-ACA case highlights the aggressive tactics incumbent monopolies use to suppress physician-led competition.

## Political & Regulatory Context

Ohio presents a highly favorable state-level regulatory environment, counterbalanced by the federal Section 6001 ban.

### State-Level: Open Market and Antitrust Action
- **No CON for Hospitals:** Ohio's Certificate of Need program applies only to long-term care facilities. There are no CON requirements for hospitals, ASCs, or imaging centers, removing a massive barrier to entry [2].
- **DOJ v. OhioHealth:** In February 2026, the DOJ Antitrust Division and Ohio Attorney General Dave Yost filed a civil antitrust lawsuit against OhioHealth. The suit alleges OhioHealth uses its market power to force insurers into restrictive contracts, preventing the development of budget-conscious health plans [3]. This action signals strong state and federal pushback against hospital monopolies in Ohio, creating a narrative opening for POHs as a pro-competitive solution.

### Federal-Level: Legislative Momentum
- **H.R. 2191 (Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act):** This bill, aimed at easing POH restrictions, has two original co-sponsors from Ohio: Rep. Troy Balderson (R-OH-12) and Rep. Michael A. Rulli (R-OH-6) [4].
- **S. 1390:** The Senate companion bill currently lacks Ohio co-sponsors, presenting an advocacy opportunity, particularly with newly elected Senator Bernie Moreno, who has already shown interest in healthcare price transparency.

## Investment Landscape & Opportunities

The Ohio healthcare market is currently experiencing significant capital influx and structural shifts, making it ripe for investment if federal POH restrictions are lifted.

- **Venture Capital Entry:** In October 2025, Health Assurance Transformation LLC (HATCo), a subsidiary of General Catalyst, completed the $485 million acquisition of Summa Health, converting the non-profit system to a for-profit entity [5]. This first-of-its-kind VC acquisition, approved by the Ohio Attorney General, demonstrates a willingness to embrace non-traditional ownership models to inject capital and technology into the state's healthcare system.
- **System Expansion:** Central Ohio systems (OhioHealth, Mount Carmel, Nationwide Children's) are currently engaged in billions of dollars of expansion projects, indicating strong market demand and available capital.

## Strategic Conclusion

Ohio is classified as a **Competitive** state. The absence of CON laws means that if Section 6001 is repealed or modified, Ohio will be one of the fastest markets to activate for new POH development. The state's large population, combined with the proven success of existing POHs like Western Reserve and OVSH, makes it a prime target. Furthermore, the active antitrust litigation against OhioHealth provides a powerful political argument: physician-owned hospitals are the necessary free-market antidote to the monopolistic practices currently driving up healthcare costs for Ohioans.
