# State-Level Market Intelligence Brief: Nebraska

**Prepared for:** The Rojas Report: Physician-Owned Hospitals
**Subject:** Nebraska Healthcare Market Structure, Financial Flows, and Institutional Accountability

I acknowledge the instructions provided. The following is the definitive market intelligence brief for Nebraska, analyzing the physician-owned hospital landscape, market control, and the potential impact of repealing Section 6001 of the Affordable Care Act.

## 1. Market Landscape: Who Controls Nebraska's Hospital Market?

The Nebraska hospital market is highly concentrated and dominated by three major non-profit health systems, with a significant recent consolidation event reshaping the landscape. According to the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI), Nebraska's hospital market is highly concentrated, with 47.7% of hospitals operating in highly concentrated or monopoly markets [1].

The dominant hospital systems operating in Nebraska are:

| Health System | Parent Company | NE Hospitals | Status | Key Markets | Approximate Market Share |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nebraska Medicine** | University of Nebraska (Pending) | 2 | Non-profit | Omaha, Bellevue | Dominant in Omaha (714 beds, $5.7B+ GPR) |
| **CHI Health** | CommonSpirit Health | 8 | Catholic Non-profit | Omaha, Lincoln, Kearney, Grand Island | Statewide presence (~1,347 beds) |
| **Bryan Health** | Independent | 2 (Main) | Non-profit | Lincoln | Dominant in Lincoln (819 beds, $3.2B+ GPR) |
| **Methodist Health** | Independent | 3 | Non-profit | Omaha, Fremont | Strong in Omaha (573 beds, $2.3B+ GPR) |

*Note: Gross Patient Revenue (GPR) and bed counts are based on recent American Hospital Directory data [2].*

**Recent Consolidation Events:**
The most significant market consolidation event in Nebraska occurred in January 2026, when the University of Nebraska Board of Regents unanimously approved an $800 million proposal to acquire Nebraska Medicine, buying out Clarkson Regional Health Services' stake [3]. This 30-day takeover effectively ends a 30-year partnership and further consolidates power within a state-affiliated entity, raising concerns about market monopolization in the Omaha region.

**Certificate of Need (CON) Laws:**
Nebraska maintains a Certificate of Need program, though it is less restrictive than some states. The Cicero Institute ranks Nebraska with 45 points on its CON restrictiveness index [4]. Nebraska's CON laws primarily restrict medical inpatient rehabilitation beds, psychiatric hospitals, and long-term care facilities [4]. While general acute care hospitals and ambulatory surgical centers have seen some exemptions, the existing CON framework interacts with Section 6001 of the ACA to create a double barrier to entry for physician-owned facilities, particularly those seeking to offer comprehensive inpatient or behavioral health services. Legislative efforts, such as LB 437 in 2025, have attempted to reform or repeal these CON laws to expand access, but the regulatory hurdle remains [5].

## 2. Physician-Owned Hospital Analysis

Despite the Section 6001 ban, Nebraska maintains a small but highly effective footprint of grandfathered physician-owned hospitals (POHs). These facilities consistently outperform the dominant non-profit systems in quality and patient satisfaction.

| Hospital Name | City | Specialty | Status | Role in Local Market |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lincoln Surgical Hospital** | Lincoln | Orthopedics, General Surgery | Operating | High-quality alternative to Bryan Health and CHI Health in Lincoln. |
| **OrthoNebraska** | Omaha | Orthopedics | Operating | Premier orthopedic specialty hospital competing with Nebraska Medicine and Methodist. |
| **Midwest Surgical Hospital** | Omaha | Multi-specialty | Operating | Boutique surgical facility offering an alternative to large Omaha systems. |
| **Nebraska Spine Hospital** | Omaha | Spine Surgery | Operating | Joint venture (CHI Health 51%, Physician-owned 41.8%) [6]. |
| **Nebraska Heart Hospital** | Lincoln | Cardiology | Acquired | Acquired by Catholic Health Initiatives (CHI) in 2011 [7]. |

**Quality and Performance Data:**
- **Lincoln Surgical Hospital:** Recognized as one of America's 50 Best Hospitals for Outpatient Joint Replacement and Outpatient Orthopedic Surgery by Healthgrades for multiple consecutive years (2024-2026) [8]. It maintains exceptional patient experience scores, with 86% of patients rating the hospital a 9 or 10, compared to the national average of 71% [9].
- **OrthoNebraska:** Consistently recognized for top-tier patient care, previously named by Becker's Healthcare as one of 22 hospitals in the U.S. with patient recommendations above 92 percent [10].
- **Midwest Surgical Hospital:** Named one of the "82 physician-owned hospitals to know in America" by Becker's Hospital Review, maintaining a 93% top patient rating [11].

**Acquisitions and Closures:**
The most notable loss of physician-owned competition in Nebraska was the Nebraska Heart Hospital in Lincoln. Originally a 52-bed physician-owned facility, it was acquired by Catholic Health Initiatives (now CommonSpirit Health) in 2011 [7]. Following the acquisition, the hospital was required to adhere to Roman Catholic ethical directives, altering the scope of services available to patients in the Lincoln market [12].

**Most Significant POH:**
**OrthoNebraska** is the most significant POH in the state. As a dedicated orthopedic hospital in the highly concentrated Omaha market, it provides a critical, high-quality alternative to the newly consolidated, state-backed Nebraska Medicine system. Its continued independence and superior clinical outcomes demonstrate the viability and necessity of the physician-owned model.

## 3. Section 6001 Impact on Nebraska

Since the ACA was signed on March 23, 2010, Nebraska has effectively been frozen in time regarding physician-owned hospital development. 

- **Attrition and Loss of Competition:** The state lost a premier cardiovascular competitor when Nebraska Heart Hospital was acquired by CHI in 2011, representing a significant loss of independent, physician-directed cardiac care in the Lincoln market.
- **Expansion Constraints:** The remaining operating POHs, such as Lincoln Surgical Hospital (20 beds) and OrthoNebraska (24 beds), are severely constrained by the Section 6001 expansion ban [2]. Despite high demand and superior quality ratings, these facilities cannot add beds or operating rooms to serve more patients without navigating an arduous and rarely granted CMS exception process.
- **Post-Repeal Landscape:** If Section 6001 were repealed, the competitive landscape in Nebraska would shift dramatically. The Omaha and Lincoln metro areas, currently dominated by Nebraska Medicine, CHI Health, and Bryan Health, would likely see immediate capital deployment for new physician-owned orthopedic, cardiovascular, and multi-specialty surgical hospitals. This would introduce much-needed price competition and alternative care sites in markets where HHI scores indicate high concentration.

## 4. Political & Regulatory Context

Nebraska's political delegation has shown mixed engagement with POH reform, despite the state's conservative leanings.

- **U.S. Senators:** Senators Deb Fischer (R-NE) and Pete Ricketts (R-NE) represent the state. Neither is currently listed as a co-sponsor of S. 1390 (Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act), the primary Senate vehicle for POH reform [13].
- **U.S. House Members:** Representative Mike Flood (R-NE) and Representative Don Bacon (R-NE) have not been prominent co-sponsors of H.R. 4002 (Patient Access to Higher Quality Health Care Act of 2025) or H.R. 2191, indicating a potential gap in advocacy from the Nebraska delegation on this specific issue [14].
- **State-Level Action:** The Nebraska Legislature has seen recent activity regarding healthcare regulation, notably LB 437 in 2025, which aimed to reform the Certificate of Need Act [5]. However, the Nebraska Hospital Association remains a powerful lobbying force, actively opposing federal POH reform bills like H.R. 4002, framing them as a "threat to community hospitals" [15].

## 5. Investment & Opportunity Landscape

The economic fundamentals of Nebraska present a compelling case for physician-owned hospital development, should federal restrictions be lifted.

- **Payer Mix:** Nebraska boasts a highly favorable payer mix for commercial viability. Approximately 53% of residents are covered by employer-sponsored commercial insurance, while Medicaid covers 17% and Medicare covers 15% [16]. This strong commercial base provides the necessary financial foundation for new POH development.
- **Highest-Opportunity Markets:** The Omaha metropolitan area represents the highest-opportunity market. The recent $800 million state-backed takeover of Nebraska Medicine creates a near-monopoly dynamic that is ripe for disruption by agile, high-quality physician-owned facilities. Lincoln also presents opportunities, particularly to challenge the Bryan Health and CHI Health duopoly.
- **Underserved Specialties:** Cardiovascular care (following the CHI acquisition of Nebraska Heart) and comprehensive orthopedic/spine care have the widest gap between patient demand and independent, physician-owned supply.
- **Investment Viability:** The combination of a strong commercial payer mix (53%), a highly concentrated incumbent hospital market, and a proven track record of success among existing grandfathered POHs (OrthoNebraska, Lincoln Surgical) makes Nebraska a prime target for physician groups and private capital if Section 6001 is repealed.
