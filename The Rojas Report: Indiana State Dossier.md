# The Rojas Report: Indiana State Dossier

## Executive Summary
Indiana presents a compelling "Reform" market characterized by a highly concentrated hospital sector dominated by five major nonprofit systems, yet balanced by a favorable regulatory environment for physician-owned hospitals (POHs). The state repealed its Certificate of Need (CON) laws for most healthcare facilities in 1999, retaining them only for nursing homes, which significantly lowers the barrier to entry for new market participants. Despite this regulatory freedom, hospital consolidation remains high, with 14 of 15 Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSAs) classified as highly concentrated. Recent legislative efforts, including price benchmarking laws and bans on physician noncompete agreements, signal a strong political appetite for increasing competition and reducing healthcare costs, which currently rank among the highest in the nation relative to Medicare rates.

## 1. Physician-Owned Hospitals (POHs) Status

Indiana has a robust history of physician-owned hospitals, though the landscape has shifted due to acquisitions by major health systems. The state currently supports several operating POHs, while others have been absorbed or closed.

### Operating POHs
*   **OrthoIndy Hospital (Indianapolis):** Founded in 2005 and rebranded from Indiana Orthopaedic Hospital in 2015, this facility is a premier example of a successful POH. It consistently achieves 5-star ratings from CMS for cleanliness and Healthgrades for patient safety and orthopedic services. Ascension St. Vincent acquired a minority interest in 2009, demonstrating the strategic value of such partnerships.
*   **Ascension St. Vincent Heart Center (Indianapolis/Carmel):** An 80-bed specialty cardiac hospital with physician ownership. It is highly regarded, ranking as the #1 heart hospital in Indiana and #2 nationally for cardiac care.
*   **Dupont Hospital (Fort Wayne):** Part of the Lutheran Health Network (Community Health Systems), this facility maintains partial physician ownership. It recently earned an "A" Hospital Safety Grade from The Leapfrog Group.
*   **Unity Physicians Hospital (Mishawaka):** Formerly United Medical and Surgical Hospital, this 29-bed facility is physician-owned and recognized for high performance in specific procedures.
*   **Pinnacle Hospital (Crown Point):** An operating POH known for high patient satisfaction scores.
*   **PMC Regional Hospital (New Albany):** Jointly owned by SCA Health and local physicians.
*   **Deaconess Women's Hospital (Newburgh):** An operating facility that has actively sought expansion exceptions from CMS.
*   **Harsha Behavioral Center (Terre Haute):** A physician-owned behavioral health facility.

### Closed or Acquired POHs
*   **Clarian West and Clarian North (Avon and Carmel):** Originally opened with physician ownership in 2004 and 2005, these facilities sparked controversy. Indiana University (IU) Health bought out the physician ownership in 2011, integrating them fully into the IU Health system.
*   **Kosciusko Community Hospital (Warsaw):** Now Lutheran Kosciusko Hospital, it was acquired by the Lutheran Health Network (CHS) and recently received a $30 million investment.
*   **Surgical Hospital of Munster:** Sold to Franciscan Alliance in 2011 and converted to a surgery center before eventually closing.
*   **Kentuckiana Medical Center (Clarksville):** Closed in April 2019 after a decade of financial struggles and a Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing in 2010.

## 2. Regulatory Environment (CON Laws)

Indiana is classified as a "Reform" state within the Rojas Report architecture, primarily due to its favorable regulatory stance on facility expansion.

*   **CON Status:** Indiana repealed its comprehensive Certificate of Need program in 1999. In 2018, the state reenacted CON requirements exclusively for comprehensive care facilities (nursing homes).
*   **Cicero Institute Ranking:** Indiana scores 15 points on the Cicero Institute's CON Restrictiveness Index, tying for 18th place nationally. The score reflects the limited scope of its regulations, applying only to medical inpatient (10 points) and long-term care (5 points).
*   **Impact on POHs:** The absence of CON laws for outpatient clinics, ambulatory surgical facilities, and most hospital expansions provides a significant advantage for physicians looking to establish or expand independent practices and specialty hospitals, free from the arbitrary veto power of incumbent mega-systems.

## 3. Market Concentration and Mega-Systems

Indiana's hospital market is highly consolidated, dominated by a few large nonprofit systems. This concentration has led to higher commercial prices and reduced competition in many regions.

### The "Big Five" Nonprofit Systems
1.  **Indiana University (IU) Health:** The largest system, currently constructing a $4.3 billion downtown Indianapolis campus that will consolidate Methodist and University hospitals.
2.  **Ascension St. Vincent:** A major player with significant market share, particularly in the Indianapolis MSA.
3.  **Franciscan Health:** Operates numerous hospitals across the state and has a history of acquiring independent facilities.
4.  **Community Health Network:** A dominant force in central Indiana.
5.  **Parkview Health:** The leading system in the Fort Wayne MSA, holding over 60% of the market share.

### Market Concentration Data (Petris Center Report, 2021)
*   **Highly Concentrated MSAs:** 14 out of 15 MSAs in Indiana have a Hospital Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) greater than 2,500, classifying them as highly concentrated. The mean hospital HHI across the state is 6,330.
*   **Top 3 Market Share:** In several MSAs, the top three hospital systems control nearly the entire market. For example, in South Bend, the top three control 100%; in Evansville, 99.1%; and in Terre Haute, 93.7%.
*   **Recent Consolidation:** In November 2025, the Indiana Department of Health approved the merger of Union Health and Terre Haute Regional Hospital (HCA) using a Certificate of Public Advantage (COPA), despite objections from the FTC regarding the creation of a near-monopoly in Vigo County.

## 4. Financial Landscape and Payer Mix

The financial dynamics of Indiana's healthcare market highlight the tension between high commercial prices and the need for cost containment.

### Payer Mix (Definitive Healthcare, 2025)
*   **Commercial/Private/Self-Pay:** 74.90%
*   **Medicare:** 12.60%
*   **Medicaid:** 12.50%
Indiana's high reliance on commercial insurance makes it an attractive market for providers, but it also places a significant burden on employers and patients.

### Hospital Pricing and Legislation
*   **High Commercial Prices:** A 2024 RAND Corporation study found Indiana's hospital prices to be the 8th highest in the nation.
*   **Price Benchmarking:** In 2024, the five largest nonprofit systems averaged commercial prices at 252% of Medicare rates, down from approximately 259% in 2023.
*   **Legislative Action:** House Enrolled Act 1004 (signed in 2025) requires nonprofit hospital systems with at least $2 billion in annual revenue to keep their aggregate average prices at or below the statewide average. This reflects growing legislative pressure to control costs driven by mega-system consolidation.

## 5. Political Landscape

Indiana's political environment is increasingly focused on healthcare reform, transparency, and reducing the power of consolidated hospital systems.

### Key Political Figures
*   **Governor Mike Braun (R):** Took office in January 2025. A former U.S. Senator with a business background, he has shown interest in healthcare cost reduction.
*   **Senator Todd Young (R-IN) and Senator Jim Banks (R-IN):** Both senators have been active in healthcare discussions. Senator Banks, in particular, has questioned how the Affordable Care Act encouraged hospital consolidation during Senate HELP committee hearings.
*   **Congressional Support for POHs:** Several Indiana representatives are cosponsors of H.R. 2191 (Physician Led and Rural Access to Quality Care Act), which aims to revise restrictions on physician-owned hospitals. Cosponsors include Reps. James R. Baird, Marlin A. Stutzman, Rudy Yakym, and André Carson.

### Recent Healthcare Legislation
*   **SEA 475 (2025):** Bans new noncompete agreements between physicians and hospitals, a significant win for physician independence and mobility.
*   **Healthcare Transaction Reporting Law (2024):** Requires entities to report mergers and acquisitions, increasing transparency in market consolidation.
*   **SB 189 (2026):** Prohibits insurance companies from imposing certain restrictions on patient access, aiming to protect out-of-network billing dispute resolutions.

## 6. Investment Opportunities and Strategic Recommendations

Indiana presents a unique dichotomy: a highly consolidated market dominated by mega-systems, juxtaposed with a favorable regulatory environment (no CON for most facilities) and recent legislative wins for physician independence (noncompete bans).

### Opportunities for PhyCapFund and MedMerge
1.  **Ambulatory Surgery Centers (ASCs) and Specialty Hospitals:** The lack of CON laws makes Indiana prime territory for developing new ASCs and specialty POHs, particularly in orthopedics, cardiology, and behavioral health, where existing POHs have demonstrated success and high quality ratings.
2.  **Physician Independence Initiatives:** With the ban on physician noncompetes (SEA 475), there is a significant opportunity to recruit top talent away from the "Big Five" systems. PhyCapFund can invest in independent practice models and joint ventures that offer physicians equity and autonomy.
3.  **Rural Health Interventions:** Indiana faces significant primary care and behavioral health shortages in rural areas (169 designated HPSAs). Investments in telehealth, rural health clinics, and micro-hospitals can address these gaps while benefiting from potential legislative support for rural access.
4.  **Employer-Direct Contracting:** Given the high commercial prices charged by mega-systems, MedMerge can leverage its AI orchestration services to facilitate direct contracting between self-funded employers and independent, high-quality, lower-cost providers (like POHs and ASCs).

### Advocacy and PHA Engagement
*   **Leverage Congressional Support:** Engage with Indiana's congressional delegation, particularly the cosponsors of H.R. 2191, to champion the expansion of POHs at the federal level.
*   **State-Level Lobbying:** Continue to support state legislation that promotes price transparency and limits the monopolistic practices of mega-systems, building on the momentum of HEA 1004 and the transaction reporting laws.

## Conclusion
Indiana is a critical battleground state for healthcare reform. While mega-systems currently hold significant market power, the regulatory framework and recent legislative actions have created a fertile environment for physician-led disruption. By capitalizing on the absence of CON laws and the new mobility of physicians, strategic investments can introduce much-needed competition, lower costs, and improve care quality across the Hoosier state.
